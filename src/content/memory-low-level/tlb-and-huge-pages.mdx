# TLB and huge pages

Every memory access your program makes goes through **virtual to
physical address translation** (see
`memory-low-level/virtual-memory.mdx`). That translation requires
walking the page tables — which themselves live in memory. The CPU
cache for those translations is the **TLB** (Translation Lookaside
Buffer). When the TLB misses, you pay tens of nanoseconds per access
on top of the data fetch — sometimes more than the data itself
costs.

## The TLB is small

A modern x86-64 core has roughly:

| Cache | Entries (typical) |
| --- | --- |
| L1 data TLB | 64-128 |
| L1 instruction TLB | 64-128 |
| L2 TLB (shared) | 1024-2048 |

The standard page size on x86-64 and ARM64 is **4 KiB**. So 1024 TLB
entries cover at most 1024 * 4 KiB = **4 MiB** of working set. The
moment your hot data exceeds that, you're paying TLB misses.

A full page-table walk on x86-64 traverses **four levels**, each a
memory access. The walks themselves are cached (the page walker has
its own caches), but a true cold walk can be ~50-100 ns. That can
double or triple the cost of an otherwise-cached load.

## When TLB misses dominate

Workloads that scan large random-ish memory regions are TLB-bound:

- In-memory databases scanning multi-gigabyte hash tables.
- Graph algorithms hopping through pointer-rich heaps.
- JVM heaps in the 8-64 GB range under random access.
- Memory-mapped files (`mmap`) over multi-TB datasets — every fresh
  page is a TLB miss *and* a minor fault.

For these, the data fits in DRAM bandwidth-wise, but the *address
translations* are the bottleneck.

## Huge pages: bigger TLB entries

A **huge page** is a single page-table entry that covers a much
larger region:

| Architecture | Standard | Large | Huge |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| x86-64 | 4 KiB | 2 MiB | 1 GiB |
| ARM64 (4K base) | 4 KiB | 2 MiB | 1 GiB |
| ARM64 (16K base) | 16 KiB | 2 MiB (via contiguous PTEs) | 1 GiB |

With 2 MiB pages, the same 1024 L2 TLB entries cover **2 GiB**
instead of 4 MiB — a 512x increase in TLB reach. For database
buffer pools and JVM heaps this often delivers single-digit-percent
to ~30% speedup just by removing TLB miss stalls.

The general rule: **THP `madvise` mode** is the safe default for
most workloads. **`hugetlbfs`** is the right choice for known-large
allocations (DB buffer pools, hypervisor RAM, packet buffers) where
you want guaranteed huge pages and no jitter.

## Why "always-on THP" can hurt

Linux's default `transparent_hugepage = always` lets `khugepaged`
scan memory in the background, find runs of 4 KiB pages, and
defragment them into 2 MiB blocks. The defrag step can stall
your process during allocations. Redis and MongoDB explicitly
recommend `madvise` (only allocations that asked for huge pages
get them) to avoid this — the famous "Redis latency spikes after
upgrading the kernel" issue from 2014 traced to THP.

The trade-off is workload-dependent. For most batch / analytics
workloads, always-on is fine — the throughput win dominates. For
tail-latency-sensitive services, `madvise` is safer.

## How to measure TLB pressure

Linux:

```
perf stat -e dTLB-loads,dTLB-load-misses,iTLB-load-misses,page-faults ./prog
```

A `dTLB-load-misses / dTLB-loads` ratio above ~1% is significant.
Above 5% and TLB misses are likely your top cost. Walking that down
with huge pages or layout changes is worth a measured experiment.

`perf stat -e cpu/event=0x08,umask=0x10/u` (Intel) breaks out
the actual page-walk cycles — the cleanest signal that you should
care.

## TLB shootdowns: the multi-core trap

When one core unmaps or modifies a page mapping (e.g., `munmap`,
`mprotect`, GC compaction), every other core that *might* have that
entry in its TLB needs to be told to invalidate. The kernel sends
an **IPI** (inter-processor interrupt) to every affected core — they
each take an interrupt, flush the entry, and ack. This is a **TLB
shootdown**.

Symptoms: a `mprotect` call that takes microseconds on a single-core
box takes hundreds of microseconds on a 64-core machine, with all
cores briefly stalled. JVM GC with compaction was a classic case
until G1/ZGC got smarter about it. The remediation is usually "do
fewer mapping changes" — batch your munmap, avoid `mprotect` on
hot paths, prefer arena allocators that don't repeatedly remap.

## Practical guidance

- For a DB buffer pool or JVM heap > 8 GiB, enable **2 MiB huge
  pages explicitly** via the database/runtime's flag. The setup
  cost is one boot-time setting; the win is real.
- For latency-sensitive services, set
  `/sys/kernel/mm/transparent_hugepage/enabled = madvise`. Don't
  disable THP entirely — it's useful for the rest of the system.
- Watch `dTLB-load-misses` ratio when profiling a memory-bound app.
  Above 1%, huge pages are likely worth trying.
- Beware **TLB shootdown storms** on big-core boxes — measure
  per-core CPU when your mapping-change-heavy code suddenly becomes
  the long pole.

## The takeaway

The TLB is the cache for "where in physical memory does this virtual
address live?" It's small (~1k entries) so it only covers ~4 MiB of
working set at standard page size. Huge pages (2 MiB, 1 GiB)
multiply TLB reach by hundreds or thousands and are the cheapest
single tuning knob for memory-bound workloads with multi-GB working
sets. Transparent huge pages give you most of the benefit
automatically; explicit hugetlbfs gives you determinism. On big
multi-core systems, TLB shootdowns from frequent mapping changes
become a problem in their own right.
