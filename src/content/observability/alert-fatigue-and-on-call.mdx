# Alert fatigue and on-call hygiene

Alert fatigue is when on-call engineers start ignoring alerts —
not maliciously, just because the volume has eroded their attention.
The dangerous one slips through alongside the hundred routine
ones.

It's both a technical problem (too many alerts) and a process
problem (no feedback loop from on-call to alert authors). Most
teams underestimate it until the day a real incident is missed.

## How fatigue forms

The typical trajectory:

1. New service launches. A handful of basic alerts.
2. Outage happens. Postmortem says "we should have alerted on
   X." A new alert is added.
3. New alert is tuned conservatively (low threshold) to be safe.
4. Repeat ×20 over a year. The on-call now gets 8 pages a night.
5. False positives accumulate. Engineers learn which alerts
   "usually don't matter."
6. A real alert lookalike that "usually doesn't matter" arrives.
   It's ignored. Customer-impacting outage extends.

Each step is locally rational. The aggregate is broken.

## Signals you have fatigue

- **Pages per shift trending up**.
- **Most pages are auto-resolved without action**.
- **Engineers admit they routinely silence pages without
  investigating**.
- **Real incidents are noticed by customer reports before alerts**.
- **On-call is a known burden; people dread their week**.
- **Page-to-action ratio is below 50%** (more than half of pages
  weren't actionable).

If any of these is true, fatigue is real and current.

## Counter-measures

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Per-page review">
After every page, the on-call engineer answers:

- Was the alert actionable?
- Was the runbook helpful?
- Was the threshold right?

If "no" to any, the alert is fixed before the next shift.

This is the single highest-leverage practice. It puts a
feedback loop between the act of being paged and the alert
that paged you.
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Page budget per service">
"This service is allowed to page on-call no more than 3 times
per week." If it pages more, the team is required to invest
in reliability or in fixing the alerts.

Caps the cost of unreliability. Forces explicit choice between
"we accept the fatigue cost" and "we fix the issue."
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="Severity discipline">
Most "alerts" should not page. They should file tickets or write
to a low-priority channel. Only the small set of user-impacting
or data-loss alerts wake people up.

Audit existing alerts: how many are P0 / P1 (page) vs P2 / P3
(ticket)? If P0 / P1 outnumber the rest, severity discipline is
poor.
</Step>

<Step n="4" title="Deduplication and grouping">
A single outage causes 50 underlying alerts to fire. Group them
into one "incident" before paging. Alertmanager, PagerDuty, and
Opsgenie all support this.

```yaml
group_by: ['service']
group_wait: 30s
group_interval: 5m
repeat_interval: 4h
```
</Step>

<Step n="5" title="Suppression and inhibition">
When alert A is firing, suppress related downstream alerts. The
DB outage shouldn't page everyone whose service depends on the
DB; just the DB team.

Done carefully — suppression rules are code that can be wrong.
A bug here means missing genuine secondary issues.
</Step>
</Steps>

## What's worth paging vs filing

A useful taxonomy:

<Compare>
<thead>
<tr><th>Page (wake up)</th><th>Ticket (next day)</th><th>Info (dashboard only)</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>User-visible outage</td><td>Saturation rising</td><td>Background noise events</td></tr>
<tr><td>SLO burning fast</td><td>Slow resource leak</td><td>Routine retry success</td></tr>
<tr><td>Data loss risk</td><td>Degraded performance</td><td>Cache misses</td></tr>
<tr><td>Security incident</td><td>Recoverable error rate spike</td><td>Auto-resolved issues</td></tr>
</tbody>
</Compare>

The default should be **ticket, not page**. Pages are the
exception, reserved for things that need a human now.

## The 3am test

A useful filter for any alert: would you want to be woken up at
3am for this?

- "DB CPU > 80% for 10 minutes" — not 3am-worthy unless it's
  the leading indicator of imminent outage. Page only if
  imminent.
- "Disk full in 6 hours at current rate" — page; you need to
  act before the disk fills.
- "Random pod restarted" — almost never. Pods restart for
  many benign reasons.

If the answer is "no, this can wait till morning," it's a
ticket. If "yes, action is needed now or there's a real cost,"
it's a page.

## On-call rotation health

A well-functioning on-call rotation:

- **Manageable frequency** — not on call every 2 weeks; not
  longer than 1 week at a time.
- **Predictable schedule** — engineers know in advance.
- **Reasonable hand-off** — incoming on-call gets a summary of
  open issues.
- **Backup coverage** — secondary if primary is unavailable.
- **Compensation or comp time** — being on call has cost; the
  org acknowledges it.
- **Authority to fix** — on-call can make architectural changes
  to reduce future pages, not just resolve and reset.

A rotation that mostly involves clicking "acknowledge" on
non-actionable pages is broken.

## Runbooks: the missing piece

A page without a runbook is a research project. With one, it's
a procedure.

Good runbooks include:

- **What this alert means** in plain language.
- **First steps to investigate** with links to dashboards.
- **Likely causes** based on past incidents.
- **Mitigation actions** with the exact commands or links.
- **Escalation path** if you can't resolve.
- **Recent related incidents** for context.

Maintain runbooks as code. Update them after every incident.
Reference them in the alert notification so on-call can click
straight to the runbook.

## Postmortem culture

The post-incident review (postmortem) is the long-feedback loop
for alerting:

- **Did the right alerts fire?**
- **Were they actionable?**
- **Did we miss any alerts that should have fired earlier?**
- **What can we change in alerting as a result?**

A well-functioning team produces alerting changes in 50%+ of
postmortems. Not always new alerts — sometimes deleting a noisy
one is the right outcome.

## Process traps to avoid

- **Adding alerts as the only response to incidents**. Sometimes
  the right answer is a code fix, not a new alert. "Alert
  inflation" makes the rotation worse without preventing the
  next incident.
- **No on-call review process**. Without one, alert quality
  drifts; nobody owns the rotation's health.
- **Allowing "watch-only" alerts to escalate to pages**. If it
  was filed for awareness, leave it as a ticket. Don't promote
  silently.
- **Letting muted alerts accumulate**. If you're muting more
  than fixing, you have a debt mountain that will surprise you.

## Specific tools

- **PagerDuty / Opsgenie / VictorOps (now Splunk On-Call)** —
  paging, escalation, schedules.
- **Alertmanager** — Prometheus's grouping/silencing layer.
- **incident.io / FireHydrant** — incident management with
  alerting integration.
- **Squadcast / xMatters** — alternatives in the paging space.

The tool matters less than the practice. A well-configured
PagerDuty with disciplined alerting beats a fancy newer tool
without process.

## A reasonable on-call goal

A sustainable rotation:

- 0-2 pages per week, on average.
- All pages actionable; all have runbooks.
- Post-shift review takes under 30 minutes.
- Engineers don't dread on-call week.

Reaching this from a noisy state takes 1-2 quarters of active
investment. The alternative is a slow burn that ends in someone
quitting or an incident being missed.
