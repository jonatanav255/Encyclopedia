# Incident response and postmortems

When something breaks in production, the quality of the response
depends as much on **process** as on tools. A team with great
observability and a bad process recovers slowly; a team with
modest tools and a clear process often does better.

This page covers the parts of incident response that matter most,
and how postmortems convert each incident into systemic improvement.

## The phases

An incident has a clear shape:

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Detection">
Something signals a problem. Synthetic monitor fails, alert fires,
customer reports, internal user notices.

Quality metric: **time to detect (MTTD)**. From "the bad thing
started" to "someone knew about it." Ideally minutes.
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Triage">
Determine severity, scope, and ownership. Who should be involved?
Customer-facing or internal? Data loss risk? Page or low-priority?

Quality metric: **time to ack**. From alert to "I'm looking at
it." Should be < 15 minutes for P1s.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="Mitigation">
Stop the bleeding. Restore service to acceptable state. Often
*not* the same as fixing the root cause:

- Roll back the bad deploy.
- Failover to redundant infrastructure.
- Disable the broken feature flag.
- Throttle the source of the problem.

Quality metric: **time to mitigate (TTM)**. The user-felt outage
window. The single most important customer-facing metric.
</Step>

<Step n="4" title="Recovery">
Verify everything is working. Confirm the metrics. Clear backlogs
caused by the outage. Communicate the resolution to stakeholders.
</Step>

<Step n="5" title="Postmortem">
What happened, why, what to do about it. Days or weeks after the
event, but not skipped.
</Step>
</Steps>

The phases blur in practice, but each is worth thinking about
separately. Optimizing detection alone helps nothing if mitigation
is slow.

## The incident commander

For major incidents, one person owns the response. They:

- Coordinate participants ("you investigate the DB; you watch
  the dashboards; you draft the customer update").
- Make calls when there's disagreement ("we're rolling back").
- Communicate status to leadership and customers.
- Decide when the incident is resolved.

The IC is **not necessarily** the most technical person — they're
the most organized one for that moment. Technical depth matters
for the investigators; coordination matters for the IC.

For small incidents, the IC role is implicit; for big ones, it's
explicit, often rotated. Tools like incident.io and FireHydrant
make this formal.

## Communication during the incident

Three audiences, each with different needs:

- **Internal engineers**: detail, real-time chat, command-and-control.
  Slack / Teams.
- **Internal stakeholders** (leadership, support, sales): summary
  status, ETA, customer impact. Updated every 15-30 minutes.
- **External users**: simple status, ETA, no jargon. Status page,
  Twitter, in-app notifications.

The IC owns external comms timing and content. Engineers focus on
the fix; comms are not their job during the active incident.

Templates help:

```
INCIDENT — investigating
Started: 14:32 UTC
Impact: api-gateway p99 latency elevated; some requests
        timing out. Estimated 5% of users affected.
Next update: 14:50 UTC
```

Stating "next update at X" is reassuring even when you don't
have new info. "We'll update when we know more" is worse than
"we'll update in 15 minutes."

## Mitigation > root cause

A common failure mode: the team is so absorbed in finding the
root cause that mitigation is delayed. The right ordering is
almost always:

1. Mitigate first. Stop the bleeding.
2. Root cause after stability is restored.

This requires having clear "mitigation buttons" — feature flags,
rollback procedures, traffic shifting, circuit breakers. If your
mitigation options are "fix the code," you'll be slow.

A useful staff-engineer investment: building the panic-button
infrastructure so on-call has options.

## When the playbook fails

Not every incident matches a runbook. The novel ones are the
challenging ones.

A few patterns that help:

- **Form hypotheses explicitly.** "I think the DB is slow." Now
  test that hypothesis with one specific signal. If false, rule
  it out and form the next.
- **Avoid simultaneous changes.** When the team is throwing
  multiple mitigations at the wall, you can't tell which helped.
  One change at a time, observe, then continue.
- **Don't assume the obvious.** The most recent deploy is the
  most common cause, but not always. Verify before rolling back
  unnecessarily.
- **Bring in fresh eyes.** Someone outside the active investigation
  often sees the missing detail. Don't wait too long to ask.

## Postmortems: structure

A useful template:

- **Summary**: what happened, in one paragraph.
- **Impact**: who was affected, how, for how long.
- **Timeline**: with timestamps.
- **What went well**: usually some things did.
- **What went wrong**: technical and process.
- **Root cause analysis**: contributing factors, not "the one
  cause."
- **Action items**: with owners and due dates.

The template prevents skipping painful sections.

## Blameless

The Google SRE book's foundational policy. The postmortem is
about **systems and process**, not about which engineer made
which mistake.

Why this matters:

- **People who fear blame don't share context**. Information needed
  to prevent the next incident gets withheld.
- **Real causes are systemic.** "Engineer pushed a bad change" is
  almost never the deepest cause. The deeper cause is "the
  system allowed a bad change to be pushed without catching it."
- **Blame doesn't motivate improvement.** It motivates fear.

This doesn't mean nobody is accountable; the team is. It means
the postmortem isn't the venue for individual accountability.
Separate conversation if needed.

## Action items: the long-feedback loop

The single most important thing a postmortem produces. Common
failure modes:

- **No action items.** The incident is "explained" but nothing
  changes. Same incident a quarter later.
- **Too many action items.** A wishlist of 30 items that nobody
  prioritizes; none happen.
- **No owners.** "Someone should..." → nobody does.
- **No due dates.** Items linger for a year.

A workable rule:

- **3-7 action items**. Pick the highest-leverage.
- **Each has an owner**. The owner agrees they own it.
- **Each has a due date**. Realistic but specific.
- **Each is tracked** in the same system as other engineering
  work. Not a separate "incident TODOs" doc nobody reads.

Track completion. If 80%+ of action items get completed within
30 days, the postmortem process is healthy. If under 50%, you're
generating intentions, not actions.

## Categories of action items

Most useful: those that reduce *time to detect* or *time to
mitigate*, or that prevent the category:

- **Add an alert** for the leading indicator (detection).
- **Add a runbook** for the symptom (mitigation speed).
- **Build a feature flag / kill switch** for the problematic
  code path (mitigation option).
- **Add a pre-deploy check** for the failure mode (prevention).
- **Improve the dashboard** that on-call uses (faster
  understanding).
- **Refactor the code** that made the bug possible (prevention).

Less useful: "be more careful," "improve test coverage in
general," vague aspirations.

## Patterns across incidents

A staff engineer's value is in seeing patterns the team misses:

- "Every incident this quarter involved cache stampedes — we
  need a single-flight library."
- "Every postmortem mentions slow database queries — we need
  query-time observability."
- "We keep getting paged for D2C signups overnight — they should
  not be paging events; queue them and handle in business hours."

A quarterly review of postmortems is often more valuable than
any individual postmortem. Look at the aggregate, not the
trees.

## When to declare an incident vs degraded

Two states:

- **Incident**: user-facing problem worth coordinated response.
- **Degraded**: a known problem, partially mitigated, not enough
  impact to escalate. Tracked, watched, but not an emergency.

The cost of declaring an incident: noise, ceremony, possible
unnecessary panic. The cost of *not* declaring when you should:
slower mitigation, missing stakeholders, surprised customers.

A reasonable bias: when in doubt, **declare**. The retro cost
of an unnecessary declaration is low; the cost of a missed
declaration is high.

## The team's incident maturity

A maturity curve worth measuring against:

- **Level 1**: incidents happen, are handled ad hoc, sometimes
  forgotten. Maybe a postmortem.
- **Level 2**: incidents have a process, owners, runbooks.
  Postmortems happen consistently.
- **Level 3**: postmortems produce action items, mostly completed.
- **Level 4**: patterns across incidents drive systemic
  improvements. Action items prevent classes of issues, not just
  individual ones.
- **Level 5**: incidents per unit work declining over time
  because of accumulated investments. Severity decreasing.

Most companies hover at level 2-3. Climbing to 4 is the staff
engineer's contribution: not handling more incidents better, but
making fewer happen.
