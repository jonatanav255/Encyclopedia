# Observability as a platform

A productive observability investment is one that **makes every
team faster, not just the platform team's own dashboards**. The
platform-team mindset shift: deliver capabilities, not just
infrastructure.

This page is about how to think about observability when it's
your responsibility as a platform discipline, and how to build it
so engineers actually use it.

## The platform mindset

A typical evolution:

- **Stage 1**: "We have Prometheus and Grafana." Engineers use it
  if they happen to know it. Some teams have great dashboards;
  others have none.
- **Stage 2**: "We standardized on Prometheus + Loki + Tempo."
  Engineers know which tool to use, but the integration is
  manual per service.
- **Stage 3**: "We provide observability as a platform." New
  services get instrumentation, dashboards, alerts, and SLOs by
  default. Engineers focus on app logic, not on plumbing.

Reaching stage 3 is the platform team's job. It's not "running
Prometheus better"; it's "shipping observability as a product."

## What "observability as a product" means

The product has users: every engineer who writes service code.
The platform team owes them:

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="A clear path to instrumentation">
A new service template, an SDK, copy-pasteable middleware. The
question "how do I emit a span?" has a one-line answer for
every supported language.
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Sensible defaults">
Out of the box, a new service has:
- HTTP request metrics and traces.
- DB query metrics and traces.
- Structured logs with trace_id and request_id.
- A basic dashboard.
- The four golden signals alerting at platform defaults.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="A self-service path to customization">
Teams can add business-specific spans, attributes, and metrics
without filing tickets. The platform doesn't gatekeep, just
provides guardrails.
</Step>

<Step n="4" title="Reliable infrastructure">
The platform itself meets its own SLOs. If logs are lost or
queries time out, the platform is broken.
</Step>

<Step n="5" title="Documentation and education">
How to read traces, how to write alerts, how to think about
SLOs. Internal training, runbooks, examples.
</Step>

<Step n="6" title="Cost visibility">
Each team sees their costs. The platform makes the bill
intelligible, not opaque.
</Step>
</Steps>

## The service template

The single most leveraged investment. A "new service" template
ships with:

```
new-service/
├── instrumentation/        # OTel SDK setup
├── metrics/                # default histograms / counters
├── dashboards/             # Grafana JSON with golden signals
├── alerts/                 # SLO-aware alert rules
├── runbooks/               # template runbook
└── README.md
```

When an engineer scaffolds a new service, all of this is
populated. Their first deploy already has metrics, traces, logs,
dashboards, and alerts. Adding business observability is the
delta they own.

This isn't just convenience. It's the difference between
"observability eventually" and "observability from day one."

## Conventions and standards

The platform team's most influential output is *conventions*.

- **Span naming**: standard verbs and resource patterns.
- **Attribute naming**: OTel semantic conventions, extended for
  team's domain.
- **Metric naming**: `service_metric_unit_total` pattern.
- **Log fields**: required, optional, deprecated.
- **Dashboard layouts**: the four golden signals always above
  the fold.
- **Alert severities**: P0/P1 pages, P2/P3 tickets.
- **Runbook structure**: what every runbook must contain.

A team where conventions are clear moves fast and consistently.
A team where they aren't has half the dashboards using different
field names and twice the cross-service investigation friction.

## Tooling investments worth making

In rough priority order:

- **A "service catalog" with observability links**. Every service
  has a page; on the page: link to dashboard, alerts, runbooks,
  recent deploys, owners, dependencies. Backstage, Cortex, or
  custom — pick one.
- **Deploy markers everywhere**. Vertical lines on every dashboard
  for every deploy.
- **Cross-signal correlation**. Click metric exemplar → trace →
  logs → profile. Each transition should be one click.
- **PR-time observability checks**. New metrics linted; new spans
  named correctly; new alerts have runbooks.
- **Cost dashboards per team**. Visible, owned.

Each of these is several engineer-months of work. Each pays back
across the entire engineering org.

## Education and support

A platform team that ships features but doesn't teach them gets
low adoption. The features sit unused. The investment was wasted.

Concrete education work:

- **Onboarding sessions** for new engineers. 1 hour, every quarter.
- **Office hours** with the platform team. Drop in, ask questions.
- **Internal documentation**, kept current. Not pinned to old
  Confluence pages no one updates.
- **Examples**. Real annotated dashboards, alerts, runbooks
  showing the patterns.
- **Pairing during incidents** (when capacity allows). Few things
  teach observability faster than debugging with a platform
  engineer at your side.

The platform team that does *only* infrastructure work has a
weaker effect than one that splits time between infrastructure
and education.

## Measuring platform success

Common mistakes in measuring platform team success:

- **Measure usage in vanity**. "10,000 dashboards created!" might
  just be cruft.
- **Measure uptime in isolation**. The platform up 100% but
  engineers struggling to use it isn't a win.

Better metrics:

- **Adoption**: what % of services have the default instrumentation
  active.
- **MTTR**: mean time to resolve incidents. Is observability
  helping?
- **Page quality**: rate of actionable pages vs noisy.
- **Engineer satisfaction**: do engineers find the platform
  useful? Surveys.
- **Cost per request**: are observability costs growing faster or
  slower than traffic?

These are the metrics that capture whether the platform is doing
its job.

## Org structures that work

Three patterns commonly seen:

- **Dedicated platform team** owns Prometheus, Grafana, Loki,
  Tempo, plus the standards. Teams call them when they need
  help.
- **Embedded SREs** within product teams. They specialize but
  sit close to engineers.
- **Federated** — a small core platform team plus designated
  observability champions on each product team.

At small scale, federated works. At large scale, dedicated
platform team plus champions is most common.

What doesn't work: nobody owns it. Observability falls between
"infrastructure" and "product" teams; both assume the other
handles it; the result is gaps.

## Knowing when to do less

A common platform-team failure mode: building too much. Every
service gets 30 default metrics, 100 default labels, 50 default
log fields. The output is unusable; the cost is high.

Signs you've overbuilt:

- Dashboards too crowded to read.
- Alerts firing more than acting upon.
- New engineers afraid to add their own instrumentation because
  there's already so much.
- Cost growing without clear additional value.

The remedy: ruthless review. Cut what isn't used. Default to
less.

## Knowing what to leave to teams

Not everything should be platform-mandated. The right boundary:

- **Platform owns**: substrate, conventions, defaults, tools,
  inter-team SLOs.
- **Teams own**: their service's business-specific instrumentation,
  their alerts beyond the defaults, their runbooks, their
  internal SLOs.

Teams have local knowledge the platform can't have. Forcing every
detail through the platform team creates a bottleneck and
discourages ownership.

## Long-term trajectory

A platform that's mature 3-5 years looks like:

- Instrumentation is automatic for the common cases.
- New engineers learn the conventions in their first week.
- Incidents are diagnosed in tens of minutes, not hours.
- Cost grows proportionally to traffic, not faster.
- The platform team spends most of its time on improvements, not
  fighting fires.

That state isn't reached by writing more dashboards. It's reached
by treating observability as a product, building for the
engineers who use it, and pruning what doesn't earn its cost.

## The staff engineer's role

A staff engineer working on platform observability typically:

- Sets vision and standards.
- Reviews architecture for instrumentation gaps.
- Mentors more junior platform engineers.
- Owns cross-team migrations (e.g., moving everyone to OTel).
- Negotiates priorities with leadership.
- Spots patterns across incidents and drives systemic investments.

Not: writing the next alert rule themselves. Not: building one
more dashboard. Those are good ways to be busy without compounding.

The leverage is in **shaping how the rest of the org practices
observability**. That's a different job from doing observability
yourself.
