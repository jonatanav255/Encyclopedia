# Sampling strategies

Observability data scales with traffic. At small scale, you keep
everything. Past a certain volume, keeping everything costs more
than you're willing to spend, and querying it gets slow. So you
sample.

Sampling done well preserves the signals you care about (errors,
slow requests, representative examples of every kind of traffic).
Done poorly, it deletes exactly the traces you needed during the
incident.

## Why sample at all

The cost components that scale with kept volume:

- **Ingest** (network, ingestion CPU).
- **Storage** (hot, warm, cold tiers).
- **Query latency** — more data = slower aggregations.
- **Backend index pressure** — each event is indexed.

For a service at 100k req/s producing 10 spans/request, **1
million spans per second**. Storing all of them for 30 days =
tens of TB. Cost of storage + ingest exceeds the cost of the
compute fleet.

Sampling lets you keep enough to debug without paying for
exhaustive history.

## Head-based vs tail-based sampling

The first axis. Where in the lifecycle is the keep/drop decision
made?

For most teams: **head-based default sampling + tail-based
override for important traces**. The combination wins.

## Head-based strategies

Simple and effective when consistency across the trace matters.

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Probabilistic">
"Keep N% of traces, drop the rest." Often 10% or 1%.

```
if (hash(trace_id) % 100) < 10:
    sample = true
```

All spans in the same trace agree (because they share the trace
ID). Predictable cost. Simplest implementation.
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Rate-limited">
"Keep up to N traces per second." Caps cost regardless of traffic
spikes.

```
token bucket: 100 traces/sec
if token available:
    sample = true
    consume token
```

Useful for sudden traffic spikes that would otherwise multiply
sampled traces.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="Adaptive / per-operation">
"Keep at least K traces per operation per minute, plus N%
beyond." Guarantees coverage of rare operations.

```
each operation has a budget of 10 traces/minute
overhead: probabilistic 1% beyond that
```

Catches the long tail of operations that wouldn't appear in pure
probabilistic sampling. Salesforce's argument for "per-key
sampling" follows this pattern.
</Step>

<Step n="4" title="Custom / business-logic">
"Always sample if the user is in the enterprise tier."
"Always sample if the request came from QA."

Lets you investigate specific scenarios without losing the trace
to random sampling. Implement at the gateway, set the sampling
flag in the trace header.
</Step>
</Steps>

## Tail-based strategies

The decision is made after the trace finishes. Now you can sample
by what actually happened.

```yaml
policies:
  - errors_always: sample if any span has error status
  - slow_always: sample if total duration > 1000ms
  - probabilistic_else: 5% random for everything else
```

The key win: **you keep every error trace and every slow trace
even at low overall sample rates**. The traces you actually look
at during incidents are kept; uninteresting "happy path" traces
are dropped.

What it costs:

- **Memory at the Collector**: full trace buffered until the
  decision wait time (typically 30s). For a 100k trace/s service,
  this is gigabytes of in-memory data.
- **Collector becomes a critical infrastructure component**:
  losing a Collector loses every trace that was buffered.
- **Decision latency**: traces aren't queryable until the
  decision delay elapses.

For most services, the trade is worth it. The Collector setup is
the operational cost you pay for vastly more useful sampled
data.

## Sampling for metrics is different

Metrics are pre-aggregated at the source, so "sampling" looks
different:

- **Don't actually sample counters**. A counter sampled at 10%
  reports 10% of the count. Use the full count and scale at
  query time, or sample only the *exemplars* (which point to
  traces), not the metric itself.
- **Sample exemplars**, which are pointers from a histogram
  bucket to a representative trace. The metric is full
  precision; only the exemplar is sampled.

A common confusion: "we sample 10% of traces, do we also sample
10% of metrics?" No — metrics aggregate per request; sampling
metrics breaks the count. Sample at the trace layer only.

## Sampling for logs

Logs at scale are often sampled too:

- **Drop DEBUG / INFO at high volume**, keep WARN / ERROR.
- **Sample repeated log events**: "log 1 in 100 of `cache hit`."
  Track the count as a metric separately.
- **Always log errors and outliers unsampled**.
- **Per-request sampling**: if the trace is sampled, log fully;
  if not, log only errors. Keeps log volume tied to trace volume.

The OTel Collector supports log sampling rules. Loki / Vector
likewise.

## What goes wrong

- **Sampling decisions inconsistent across services**. Service A
  decides to sample the trace; service B decides to drop. You get
  half-traces. Use head-based sampling that propagates the
  decision via the trace flag (`-01` in `traceparent` means
  sampled).
- **Important traces lost in pure probabilistic sampling**. The
  user's bug-report request is at 0.1% probability of being
  kept. Mitigate with tail sampling for errors/slow, or with
  custom rules that boost sampling for specific cookies / users.
- **Cost still high after sampling**. Often because the wrong
  layer is sampled. Sampling spans drops trace cost, but if you
  also keep all the logs and all the metric exemplars, you've
  saved less than you think.
- **Buffer overflow at the Collector during tail sampling**.
  Memory limiter kicks in, drops random traces — your "100% of
  errors" policy now misses some errors. Size memory generously
  or use rate-limited tail sampling.

## A reasonable default policy

For a typical mid-scale service (10k - 100k req/s):

- **Head sample at 10%** as the baseline.
- **100% of errors** (tail sampled or via explicit `errored:
  true` propagated up).
- **100% of requests > p95 latency** (tail sampled).
- **Tier-based override** for important users / customers.
- **Per-operation budget** to avoid losing rare endpoints.

Cost: ~12-15% of full sampling cost. Coverage: nearly 100% of
the traces you'd actually want to inspect.

## How much is enough

If the question "can I find the trace for X?" can be answered
yes 95% of the time, the sampling is fine. If it's a coin flip
("is the trace I need in the store?"), boost sampling for the
things you care about.

A useful drill: ask your team to pick 5 recent customer support
tickets and find the relevant traces. If you can find them all,
sampling is generous enough. If half are missing, tune up.

## Sampling doesn't replace good logging

A common mistake: lean heavily on traces, sample aggressively,
realize during an outage that the trace you need isn't in the
store. **Logs at WARN+ should never be sampled**. They're cheaper
than traces and form the safety net when sampling fails you.
