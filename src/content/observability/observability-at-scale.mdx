# Observability at scale

A two-engineer startup can run a basic Prometheus + Grafana setup
on a single VM. A thousand-engineer company with hundreds of
services and petabytes of telemetry has fundamentally different
problems.

This page covers what changes — operationally, organizationally,
and architecturally — when observability itself becomes a large
system.

## What breaks first

The order in which a naive setup fails as you grow:

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Single Prometheus runs out of memory">
A few thousand series is fine. Past ~1 million active series,
memory pressure starts. Past ~10 million, a single instance is
infeasible.
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Log volume exceeds budget">
A few GB/day is cheap. A few TB/day starts hurting. At 10s of
TB/day, log retention windows shrink to fit the budget, and
people start losing the ability to investigate older incidents.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="Cardinality explodes">
Someone adds a label with high-cardinality values. The TSDB
starts struggling. Query latency goes from milliseconds to
seconds.
</Step>

<Step n="4" title="Dashboards multiply">
Every team made their own. Two thousand dashboards exist. No one
knows which to look at during an incident.
</Step>

<Step n="5" title="Alert volume becomes unsustainable">
Each new alert was reasonable on its own. The aggregate is
hundreds of pages per week.
</Step>

<Step n="6" title="Tracing infrastructure costs more than compute">
Spans at full sampling start outpacing application telemetry.
Sampling becomes essential — but tail sampling needs centralized
buffers, which become their own infrastructure problem.
</Step>
</Steps>

Each is solvable, but the solutions are architectural changes,
not "more of the same."

## Architectural shifts

What "observability at scale" looks like architecturally:

<Compare>
<thead>
<tr><th>Component</th><th>Small scale</th><th>Large scale</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Metrics storage</td><td>Single Prometheus</td><td>Federated Prometheus + Thanos/Mimir/Cortex</td></tr>
<tr><td>Log storage</td><td>Single Elastic or Loki</td><td>Distributed Loki/Elastic + S3 archives</td></tr>
<tr><td>Trace storage</td><td>Single Tempo or Jaeger</td><td>Distributed Tempo + S3</td></tr>
<tr><td>Collectors</td><td>Per-pod sidecar</td><td>Sidecar + gateway aggregation layer</td></tr>
<tr><td>Sampling</td><td>10% head everywhere</td><td>Tail-based at gateway</td></tr>
<tr><td>Ownership</td><td>Single team or none</td><td>Platform team + service teams</td></tr>
</tbody>
</Compare>

The pattern: **decouple collection from storage; tier storage by
recency**. Hot, warm, cold. Object storage for cold (cheap, slow);
SSD-backed indexes for hot (expensive, fast).

## Federation and aggregation

For metrics: a single Prometheus per cluster, federated up to a
global Thanos/Mimir/Cortex that:

- Indexes across clusters.
- Stores history in object storage (S3, GCS).
- Serves queries that span clusters.

The same per-cluster Prometheus continues to handle local alerting
and recent data; the federation layer answers historical and
cross-cluster queries.

For logs/traces: the OTel Collector acts as a similar aggregation
point. Per-host agents → per-cluster gateway → global storage.

## Cost as a first-class concern

At scale, observability cost is real money. A typical breakdown:

- **Logs**: 60-70% of total observability spend in many orgs.
- **Traces**: 15-25%.
- **Metrics**: 10-15%.
- **APM agents and tooling**: 5-10%.

Common reductions:

- **Drop noisy log fields at the agent**. Whole categories of logs
  (health checks, debug, info on hot paths) often shouldn't ship
  past the agent.
- **Tail sampling for traces**. Keep errors and slow traces;
  drop the rest aggressively.
- **Histogram metrics with fewer buckets**. Each bucket is a
  series.
- **Aggressive retention tiering**. 24 hours hot is enough for most
  ops; 7 days warm; 30+ days archived to S3.
- **De-duplicate metrics across services**. Multiple services
  exposing the same low-value default metrics adds up.

Targets to aim for: observability spend as a fraction of compute
spend should be < 10-15%. Above that, audit.

## Cardinality at scale

The single largest hidden cost driver. Mitigations:

- **Per-team cardinality budgets**. Each team owns a budget of
  series. Auto-alert when 80% used.
- **Linting / pre-commit checks**. Reject PRs that introduce
  potentially high-cardinality labels.
- **Quotas at the TSDB**. Reject writes from teams that exceed
  their share.
- **Stream-side aggregation**. Drop labels at recording-rule
  level for low-value series.

Cardinality discipline is one of the highest-leverage policies a
platform team can enforce. Without it, costs grow exponentially
with team count.

## Standardization vs autonomy

A tension at scale. Each team wants flexibility to instrument
their service; the platform team wants consistency.

The right balance:

- **Standardize the data model and conventions**. Field names,
  resource attributes, log formats, semantic conventions. Use
  OTel as the lingua franca.
- **Standardize the tools**. One trace backend, one log backend,
  one metric backend. Multiple of each is operational overhead
  with little benefit.
- **Allow flexibility in what to instrument**. Teams choose
  what business-logic spans to emit, what alerts to set, what
  dashboards to build.

The platform owns the substrate; the teams own the content.

## Platform team's role

A dedicated observability platform team at ~50+ engineer
organizations becomes valuable. Their job:

- **Operate the backends**. Prometheus, Loki, Tempo (or equivalent
  vendor stack).
- **Provide SDKs and templates**. Default instrumentation for new
  services so each team doesn't reinvent.
- **Set conventions**. Naming, fields, semantic conventions, log
  formats.
- **Enforce quotas and budgets**. Cardinality limits, retention
  tiers.
- **Operate dashboarding infrastructure**. Grafana, deploy
  annotations, runbook standards.
- **Train teams**. On reading traces, writing alerts, using SLOs.

A common anti-pattern: the platform team treats observability as
"infrastructure" with low-level focus on Prometheus internals,
while teams struggle to use the stack effectively. The platform's
deliverable is teams' productivity, not raw uptime of the metric
store.

## Multi-tenancy

When one observability stack serves multiple teams or business
units:

- **Isolation**: one team's cardinality spike shouldn't crash
  another team's queries. Per-tenant quotas and resource limits.
- **Authorization**: teams see their data and only their data.
- **Cost allocation**: teams see their own cost. Shows up on their
  bill in some org structures. Drives discipline.
- **Standard onboarding**: a "new service" template comes with
  default dashboards, alerts, instrumentation hooks.

Mimir, Cortex, Loki, and Tempo all have first-class multi-tenancy.
Single-tenant tools (vanilla Prometheus) are insufficient at scale.

## SLO discipline at scale

At small scale, SLOs are nice-to-have. At large scale, they become
critical for inter-team contracts:

- "Service A's SLO is 99.9% successful." Service B depends on A
  and has its own SLO of 99.9%. Math: B's max achievable SLO is
  bounded by A's SLO. A's reliability is B's input.
- "Platform's SLO" is the floor for any service built on it. If
  the platform's logging SLO is 99.5%, no service can promise
  better logging-correlated debugging.

Inter-team SLO awareness changes architectural decisions:
sometimes the right answer is to fail open (degrade rather than
fail) rather than depending on a lower-SLO dependency.

## Postmortem culture

At scale, individual incidents compound into culture. A healthy
observability culture has:

- **Blameless postmortems** documented and shared.
- **Pattern recognition across incidents** — recurring themes
  drive systemic investments.
- **Observability gaps identified in postmortems** lead to
  follow-up work, not lip service.
- **Stakeholder access to postmortems** — engineering, product,
  leadership.

A team that does N postmortems and produces ~N follow-up
investments is improving. A team that documents incidents without
following through is on a slow regression.

## Vendor vs self-host

A perennial question. The trade-offs:

<Compare>
<thead>
<tr><th></th><th>Vendor (Datadog, Honeycomb, NR)</th><th>Self-host (Prom/Loki/Tempo)</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Time to first value</td><td>Hours</td><td>Weeks</td></tr>
<tr><td>Operational burden</td><td>Their problem</td><td>Yours</td></tr>
<tr><td>Cost at low scale</td><td>Cheap (free tiers)</td><td>Cheap (one VM)</td></tr>
<tr><td>Cost at large scale</td><td>Can become a top-3 line item</td><td>Cheaper in compute; expensive in headcount</td></tr>
<tr><td>Lock-in</td><td>High (OTel mitigates)</td><td>Low</td></tr>
<tr><td>Features</td><td>Polished, integrated</td><td>You assemble</td></tr>
</tbody>
</Compare>

For most orgs, the inflection point is around 100M-1B events per
day. Below: a managed vendor saves engineering time at acceptable
cost. Above: a dedicated platform team operating open-source
stacks usually wins on cost, with significant headcount investment.

OpenTelemetry's existence makes this less of a one-way decision —
the data model is portable.

## The team's job, simplified

A staff engineer responsible for observability at scale spends
their time on:

- **Standardization** — what conventions everyone follows.
- **Quotas and budgets** — what limits prevent runaway cost.
- **Tooling and templates** — what makes individual teams faster.
- **Incidents and patterns** — what the recurring failure modes
  reveal about the platform.
- **Cost optimization** — what changes return more than they cost.

Not on: writing the next alert rule, building one more dashboard,
chasing one specific metric.
