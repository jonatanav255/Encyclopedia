# SLOs and error budgets

A **Service Level Objective** (SLO) is a target for a measurable
service quality (usually reliability or latency). An **error
budget** is what's left between the SLO and 100%. Together they
turn "is the service reliable enough?" from a debate into a
calculation.

This page focuses on the practical use of SLOs and error budgets
in day-to-day engineering. The terminology overlaps with
`devops/slo-sli`; this one is the observability-side view.

## SLI, SLO, SLA

Three terms in the same family:

- **SLI** (Service Level Indicator) — a measurement. The
  fraction of requests that succeed; the p99 latency.
- **SLO** (Service Level Objective) — a target for the SLI.
  "99% of requests successful over 30 days."
- **SLA** (Service Level Agreement) — a contract. "If we miss
  99% over 30 days, we pay you back X."

SLI is the metric; SLO is the internal target; SLA is what
you've sold to customers. SLA is usually weaker than SLO (you
miss SLO sometimes; you really don't want to miss SLA, ever).

## The error budget

If the SLO is "99.9% of requests successful in a 30-day window,"
the error budget is **0.1%**.

```
30 days × 1440 minutes/day × 0.001 = 43.2 minutes of "down" allowed
```

(More precisely: 0.1% of all requests in the window can fail.
Calendar time matters less than request count.)

What the budget enables:

- A "fast" deploy that causes 10 minutes of degraded latency
  burns ~25% of the month's budget. Acceptable if you have
  budget; concerning if you've already had two such deploys.
- A planned migration that needs an hour of degraded service
  is feasible if budget allows.
- The number is concrete enough to argue with. "Should we
  freeze releases?" becomes "How much budget is left?"

## Burn rate

A budget consumed at 1× burn rate would run out exactly at
the end of the window. Higher rates run out sooner.

```
burn rate = (current error rate) / (allowed error rate)
```

- Burn rate 1: on track.
- Burn rate 10: at this pace, 30 days of budget gone in 3 days.
- Burn rate 100: gone in 7 hours.

The classic burn-rate alert pattern (from Google's SRE workbook):

- **Page** if 5% of budget burns in 1 hour (very fast burn).
- **Page** if 10% burns in 6 hours (sustained burn).
- **Ticket** if 2% burns in 24 hours (slow burn).

The thresholds reflect: you want to be alerted before the
budget runs out, but only for burns severe enough that a
human should act.

## Multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerts

The recommended pattern:

```
Fast (5% / 1h)    AND   Slow (5% / 5m)
Fast (10% / 6h)   AND   Slow (10% / 30m)
```

A burn must be sustained over both windows to trigger. Avoids
flapping on momentary blips while catching genuine sustained
problems.

The Prometheus expression looks like:

```
(
  job:slo_errors_per_request:ratio_rate5m{job="api"} > 14.4 * 0.001
  and
  job:slo_errors_per_request:ratio_rate1h{job="api"} > 14.4 * 0.001
)
or
(
  job:slo_errors_per_request:ratio_rate30m{job="api"} > 6 * 0.001
  and
  job:slo_errors_per_request:ratio_rate6h{job="api"} > 6 * 0.001
)
```

The multipliers (14.4, 6) and durations are calibrated so that
14.4% / 1h gives roughly "5% of monthly budget burned" given a
99.9% SLO. Most SLO-aware tools generate this.

## Choosing the SLO

Two failure modes:

- **Too lax**: "99% latency under 5 seconds" — easily met, doesn't
  reflect a quality goal.
- **Too strict**: "99.999% successful" — impossible to meet; team
  burns out chasing five nines.

The right SLO is **slightly above what users find acceptable,
but achievable with reasonable effort**.

A useful test:

- What latency do users start complaining at? p95 should be
  comfortably under that.
- What error rate do users find frustrating? Set the threshold
  a bit better than that.
- Can the current architecture meet this without heroic effort?
  If yes, fine. If no, either invest in the architecture or
  lower the target.

99.9% is a reasonable starting point for most public services.
99.99% is for critical paths (auth, payments). 99.999% is
exceptional and very expensive.

## SLOs and feature work

An honest SLO + error budget integration changes how the team
decides what to work on.

- **Budget intact**: you have permission to ship features
  aggressively. Burn budget on changes.
- **Budget exhausted**: stop shipping features; invest in
  reliability until budget recovers. Make this an explicit policy,
  not a debate per incident.

This is the SRE book's "freeze releases" approach. The team
sometimes resists it (everyone wants to ship). The policy works
because it's pre-agreed and quantitative — you're not debating
in the heat of an incident.

## SLO panels on dashboards

A typical "service health" dashboard panel:

```
SLO: 99.9% success over 30 days
Current 30-day: 99.84%   (40 minutes used of 43-min budget)
Burn rate (1h): 0.5×     (healthy)
Burn rate (24h): 0.8×    (healthy)
[time series: budget remaining over 30 days]
```

The single time-series chart of "budget remaining over time"
is the killer visualization. A team can see at a glance: budget
healthy, gradual burn, sudden spike, etc.

## Common pitfalls

- **Choosing SLOs from infrastructure metrics, not user
  experience**. "CPU below 80%" is not an SLO; it's a saturation
  signal. SLOs come from user-felt quality.
- **One SLO per metric per service**, leading to dozens. Too
  many makes triage hard. Pick 1-3 critical SLOs per service.
- **Calendar-based windows that reset abruptly**. "December 1
  resets the budget" → a December 2 outage takes the team by
  surprise because budget had been near zero. Rolling 30-day
  windows handle this.
- **SLO is the actual SLA**. Then you can't ever burn budget
  without contractual consequences. Set SLO tighter than SLA.
- **Not enforcing the policy**. The team agreed to freeze
  releases on exhausted budget, then doesn't. The mechanism
  dies; back to ad-hoc decisions.

## Multiple SLOs and weighted budgets

A service with multiple SLIs (success, latency-p95,
latency-p99) has multiple budgets. Some teams keep them
separate ("we have budget for latency, no budget for errors").
Others combine into a single composite.

Separate is simpler to reason about. Combined makes alerting
cleaner.

A useful compromise: **one composite SLO at the top** (e.g.,
"successful and fast"), plus per-SLI views for debugging which
component is breaching.

## When SLOs aren't worth the ceremony

- For internal-only tools where no one really cares about
  uptime metrics.
- Pre-product-market-fit; the product is changing too fast for
  SLOs to mean anything.
- Where the service has no measurable quality (rare).

For everything else, SLOs are the most useful management
construct in observability. They turn "is this good enough?"
into a number that everyone — engineers, managers, business —
can agree on.
