# Cost and cardinality management

At staff scope, observability isn't free engineering hygiene — it's
a meaningful line item. A mature observability stack at a
1,000-engineer company costs millions per year. Without active
management, the cost grows faster than the system grows; with
active management, it stays proportional and the team gets more
value per dollar.

This page is about the levers a staff engineer or platform team
uses to keep observability cost healthy.

## Where the money goes

A typical breakdown of observability spend:

<thead>
<tr><th>Component</th><th>Typical % of spend</th><th>Cost driver</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Log storage and ingest</td><td>50-70%</td><td>Volume × retention</td></tr>
<tr><td>Trace storage and ingest</td><td>15-25%</td><td>Volume × retention × indexing</td></tr>
<tr><td>Metrics</td><td>10-15%</td><td>Series cardinality × retention</td></tr>
<tr><td>APM / tooling licenses</td><td>5-10%</td><td>Hosts, users, features</td></tr>
</tbody>
</Compare>

Logs dominate because every request can produce many entries, and
each entry is full text. Metrics are usually cheapest because they
aggregate at the source, *unless* cardinality is out of control
— then they can become a top-1 cost.

## The cost-vs-value question

Before cutting cost, ask: which spending is producing value?

A useful framing:

- **Hot** data (queried frequently): worth the cost.
- **Warm** data (queried during investigations): worth keeping
  cheaply.
- **Cold** data (rarely queried): archive aggressively.
- **Dark** data (never queried): stop ingesting.

Most observability stores have substantial dark data — metrics no
one dashboards, logs no one greps, traces never opened. Cutting
this is free value: lower cost, no loss.

A quarterly audit: which 20% of metrics drive 80% of queries?
Which 80% of metrics are never queried? Drop them.

## The cardinality dragon

The single biggest hidden cost driver. Once again, because it
deserves repetition:

- Each unique label combination is a separate time series.
- Storage and ingest grow linearly with cardinality.
- A high-cardinality label (user_id, request_id) can cause
  100-1000× cost increase from one mistake.

Defenses at staff level:

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Cardinality budgets per team / service">
Each team gets a budget of active series. Auto-alert at 80%.
Auto-reject at 100% (with override path).
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Lint at code-review time">
Tools that scan PRs for new label patterns and warn on suspect
ones. Several open-source options; some teams write custom
linters.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="Quotas at the TSDB">
Mimir / Cortex / Datadog all support per-tenant limits. Set them.
A team that hits the limit can request more with justification.
</Step>

<Step n="4" title="Cardinality dashboards">
A dashboard showing "top 10 metrics by series count" and
"top 10 labels by cardinality." Review monthly. New high-cardinality
labels are visible.
</Step>

<Step n="5" title="Pre-aggregation / recording rules">
For metrics that genuinely need to be high-cardinality at the
source, aggregate at recording-rule level into a lower-cardinality
output series for storage.
</Step>
</Steps>

## Log volume tactics

Logs are usually the largest cost. Tactics, ordered by impact:

- **Drop noise at the agent.** Health checks, debug logs in prod,
  known-uninteresting events. Stops them before storage.
- **Sample high-volume events.** Log 1% of cache misses; count
  separately as a metric.
- **Drop log fields you don't need.** A 200-field event with 20
  useful fields is 90% waste.
- **Reduce retention.** 7 days hot, 30 days warm, 90 days cold.
  Most investigations happen in the first 24 hours.
- **Tier storage.** Hot in fast-query store, cold in S3. 5-10×
  cost difference.
- **Compress.** Most agents do this; verify it's on.
- **Filter at the source.** Some apps log every cache lookup
  because someone debugged it once. Now it's permanent
  background noise.

A good rule of thumb: **for any log line you emit, ask "would I
search for this?"** If the answer is "no," it doesn't need to be
in the index. Either it's a metric or it shouldn't exist.

## Trace cost tactics

After cardinality, the biggest trace cost driver is sampling
strategy:

- **Aggressive head sampling** — drop most traces at the source.
- **Tail sampling for errors and slow requests** — keep what
  matters.
- **Cap spans per trace** — pathological traces with thousands of
  spans cost disproportionately. Some collectors will drop
  these.

For a typical service: 5-10% head sampling, plus 100% tail
sampling on errors and high-latency outliers. Total: maybe 10-15%
of full data, but 95% of the analytical value.

## Metric cost tactics

For metrics specifically:

- **Reduce label cardinality** (the dragon, again).
- **Cut unused metrics.** Audit which queries hit which metrics.
- **Larger scrape intervals** for non-critical metrics. 60s
  instead of 15s = 4× fewer data points.
- **Drop unused buckets** from histograms. A histogram with 25
  buckets that you only ever query for p50/p95/p99 could be
  10 buckets.
- **Shorter retention** for high-cardinality metrics. Old per-pod
  data is rarely queried; old aggregated data is queried
  routinely.

## The vendor switching tradeoff

A typical pattern: company starts on Datadog at small scale,
grows, Datadog bill becomes painful, considers self-host.

The decision is rarely as simple as "self-host is cheaper":

- **Compute and storage costs** of self-host are lower at scale,
  but...
- **Headcount cost** of operating it is significant. 2-4
  engineers full-time at scale.
- **Time-to-value** of vendor features. Switching to self-host
  means rebuilding some integrations.
- **Quality of incident response** — vendor tools are often more
  polished.

A common middle path: stay on vendor for products that are
hard to replicate (APM, RUM); move high-volume cheap commodities
(logs, basic metrics) to self-host. Hybrid stacks are common.

OpenTelemetry lowers the switching cost — instrumentation is
portable. The decision is now about backends, not instrumentation.

## Showback and chargeback

A useful organizational tactic: **show each team their share of
observability cost**.

- "Your service costs $X/month in logs, $Y in metrics, $Z in
  traces. Total: $...".
- Show the cost on a dashboard. Visible to engineers, managers,
  and leadership.
- For teams that exceed allocations: conversation about why.

The behavior change from showback alone is often substantial.
Teams find ways to cut costs when they see what they spend.

Chargeback (actually billing teams) is more politically loaded
and harder to roll out, but even stronger in effect.

## Optimization is an ongoing process

Not a one-time project. The natural state of an observability
stack is to grow without bound; active management keeps it
bounded.

A cadence that works:

- **Weekly**: cost dashboard review by platform team.
- **Monthly**: top growers, anomaly investigation.
- **Quarterly**: per-team allocations review. Tactical optimizations
  with the worst offenders.
- **Annually**: strategic review. Tool selection, retention
  policies, contractual commitments.

Most savings come from disciplined ongoing review, not heroic
one-off projects.

## When to spend more

A staff engineer's job isn't only to cut cost. Sometimes the right
answer is to **spend more on observability** because the team is
operating blind in a way that's expensive in other ways.

Concrete indicators:

- Incidents extend longer than they should because nobody can find
  the issue.
- The team is debating in postmortems whether problem X happened
  (no data to confirm).
- A new architecture is being adopted without observability
  parity to the old one.
- Engineers spend hours per week pasting log lines together
  instead of using tools.

In these cases, the cost of *not* spending exceeds the cost of
spending. Quantify if you can: "we lost X engineer-hours and Y
customer-incidents because of observability gaps; investing
$Z/year would prevent most of it."

## What to escalate

Observability cost is one area where senior leadership often has
real input:

- **Tool selection** — strategic, affects roadmap.
- **Major retention changes** — affects compliance, incident
  response capability.
- **Multi-year vendor contracts** — financial commitment.
- **Headcount for platform team** — organizational.

Tactical optimizations (sampling rules, retention adjustments,
specific dashboards) — handle within the team.

A platform team that knows the difference between strategic
escalation and tactical autonomy operates more efficiently than
one that escalates everything or nothing.

## A reasonable end state

A team operating at a healthy observability cost equilibrium:

- Observability spend is 5-15% of compute spend.
- Cost grows linearly with traffic, not faster.
- Teams know their cost and have local incentives to manage it.
- Cardinality is bounded and budgeted.
- Retention tiers match query patterns.
- Dark data is regularly purged.
- Tools are selected for value, not just inertia.

Reaching this isn't a one-quarter project. Maintaining it isn't
either. But the steady state is much better than the alternative
of cost-driven panic every 6-12 months.
