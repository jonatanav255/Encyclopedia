# GC tuning

Garbage collection is a fixed feature of most server-side runtimes
— JVM, .NET, Go, Node.js, Python. You can't escape it; you can
only shape how it interacts with your latency profile.

The instinct to "tune the GC" is usually misplaced. The actual win
is almost always **producing less garbage**, with tuning as a
second-order concern.

## The GC tradeoff triangle

Every collector trades among three goals:

- **Low pause times** — single GC events don't stop the world for
  long.
- **High throughput** — total time spent collecting is a small
  fraction of total runtime.
- **Low memory footprint** — runs within tight heaps.

You can win two and lose the third; no collector wins all three at
once. Modern concurrent collectors (G1, ZGC, Shenandoah, Go's
collector) push hard on the first by giving up some of the others.

## Generational hypothesis

Most allocators bet on the **weak generational hypothesis**: most
objects die young.

- A request handler allocates 50 objects: parsed JSON, query
  params, intermediate values, response object. Almost all become
  garbage when the request ends.
- A handful of objects live longer: caches, connection pools,
  application state.

Generational collectors split the heap:

- **Young generation** ("nursery", "Eden") — small, fast, collected
  frequently. Most objects die here.
- **Old generation** ("tenured") — bigger, collected less often,
  more expensively.

Young-gen collections (minor GCs) are cheap. Old-gen collections
(major / full GCs) are expensive — the latency spikes you fear
come from old-gen work.

The big-picture trade: low-pause collectors do more concurrent
work (uses CPU during the program's normal execution) at the cost
of higher steady-state CPU overhead. ZGC might cost 5-15% throughput
to win you sub-ms pauses.

## Three actionable interventions, in order

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Reduce allocation">
The largest, most reliable lever. Less garbage = fewer GCs = less
total pause time. Tactics:

- Reuse buffers (`ByteBuffer`, `sync.Pool`, object pools where
  appropriate).
- Avoid unnecessary boxing (`int` → `Integer` in JVM, large
  closures in JS).
- Stream rather than load fully into memory.
- Pre-allocate arrays/maps with known capacity.
- Avoid creating intermediate collections (filter + map chains
  that allocate transient lists).

Allocation profilers exist for every major runtime: JFR for JVM,
`go tool pprof -alloc_objects`, Chrome DevTools heap snapshot for
V8.
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Right-size the heap">
A heap that's too small triggers GCs constantly. A heap that's
too big takes longer per GC and uses more RAM.

The shape of the curve: lots of GC at small heaps, gradually less
until the heap is comfortable, then diminishing returns. Modern
collectors are designed for **heap occupancy ~30-50%** at steady
state — if you're running at 80% live data, GC is fighting you.

The JVM heuristic: heap big enough to keep old-gen utilization
below ~50% even under load.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="Pick the right collector">
Only after the above. Default collectors are good for default
workloads; specialized collectors win for specific shapes.

- Latency-sensitive (user-facing, SLO-bound): ZGC, Shenandoah on
  JVM; Go's default; .NET Server GC with sustained-low-latency
  mode.
- Throughput-bound (batch jobs, ETL): JVM ParallelGC; longer
  pauses acceptable, total work minimized.
- Tight-memory environments: G1 with small heap; sometimes the
  serial GC for tiny heaps.
</Step>
</Steps>

## The metrics that matter

For diagnosis, watch:

- **GC pause time** (max and p99). Should be a single-digit
  percentage of total time at most.
- **GC frequency**. Young-gen GC every few seconds is normal.
  Every 100ms = under-provisioned heap or allocation storm.
- **Heap occupancy after GC**. Should drop substantially. If it
  stays high, you have a leak or genuine working-set growth.
- **Allocation rate** (bytes/sec). Compare across deploys — a
  sudden jump means a new allocation hot path was introduced.

In the JVM, GC logs (`-Xlog:gc*`) give you all of this. Use a
visualizer like GCViewer or upload to gceasy.io.

## Anti-patterns

- **Calling `System.gc()` / `runtime.GC()` manually**. Almost
  always wrong. The collector knows when to run; forcing it just
  causes a big GC at a bad moment.
- **Allocating in a hot loop "because it's fine."** A
  million-element loop that allocates 100 bytes per iteration =
  100MB of garbage per execution. Even a fast GC notices.
- **Caching forever without bounds**. Now every entry is
  long-lived; the cache promotes to old-gen; major GCs become
  more expensive. Use bounded caches.
- **Tuning GC flags without measurement**. Each generation of
  JVM has different "magic incantation" advice on the internet,
  half of it outdated. Profile, then tune one knob at a time,
  always with a baseline.

## Special case: latency-sensitive low-allocation services

For services where p99 latency is critical, the right move is often
**zero-allocation hot paths**:

- Allocate everything you need up front, at startup.
- Reuse buffers / pools for transient objects.
- Avoid common patterns that hide allocations (string concatenation,
  iterator chains).
- Use value types or stack allocation where the language permits
  (Rust, Go's escape analysis, Java records with escape analysis).

A request handler that allocates *nothing* under normal load won't
trigger young-gen GC at all in steady state. Your tail comes down
dramatically. Worth it only for paths where you've already measured
GC as a contributor.

## When GC isn't the problem

People reach for GC tuning because pauses are visible in profiles.
Often the underlying problem is:

- A memory leak (live data grows; old-gen fills; major GCs
  intensify).
- An allocation hot path (lots of churn; young-gen GCs frequent
  even though pauses are short).
- An undersized heap (collector is fine; you're starving it).

Diagnose first. The "fix" of switching collectors without
understanding the workload often shifts the problem to a different
shape rather than solving it.
