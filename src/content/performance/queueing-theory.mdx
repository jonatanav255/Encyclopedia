# Queueing theory basics

You don't need a textbook treatment to get a lot of value out of
queueing theory. Two laws and one rule of thumb explain most of
the surprising behavior of saturated systems.

This page covers the ones that show up most in production
intuition: Little's Law, the utilization-latency curve, and the
counterintuitive fact that **adding workers helps differently
than adding capacity**.

## Little's Law

The fundamental conservation law of queueing systems:

```
L = λ × W
```

- **L** — average number of items in the system
- **λ** — arrival rate (items per unit time)
- **W** — average time an item spends in the system

It holds in steady state for *any* queueing discipline, *any*
distribution. No assumptions about Poisson, FIFO, or anything
else. As long as the system is stable (in = out over the long
term), Little's Law is true.

What it tells you:

- **You can derive any of the three from the other two.** Measured
  arrival rate (5,000 req/s) and average latency (200ms)? Then on
  average you have 1,000 requests in flight. Useful for capacity
  planning.
- **Reducing latency reduces concurrency.** If you halve latency
  at the same arrival rate, you halve the number of in-flight
  requests — fewer connection pool slots, fewer threads, less
  memory.
- **Cutting concurrency at fixed throughput cuts latency.** Doing
  less in parallel makes each individual request finish faster.
  This is non-obvious until you've used it.

## Utilization and latency

The most consequential intuition: **the closer you push utilization
to 100%, the more disproportionately latency grows.**

For a single-server queue with Poisson arrivals and exponential
service (M/M/1), the average wait time in the queue is:

```
W_q  =  ρ / (μ × (1 - ρ))
```

where ρ is utilization (0 to 1), μ is the service rate. The
`(1 - ρ)` denominator is the dragon. Look at how `1 / (1 - ρ)`
behaves:

<thead>
<tr><th>ρ</th><th>1/(1-ρ)</th><th>Notes</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>0.50</td><td>2</td><td>Linear-ish region. Doubling rate doubles wait.</td></tr>
<tr><td>0.70</td><td>3.3</td><td>Still tolerable.</td></tr>
<tr><td>0.80</td><td>5</td><td>Wait is now equal to service time.</td></tr>
<tr><td>0.90</td><td>10</td><td>Latency is dominated by wait, not work.</td></tr>
<tr><td>0.95</td><td>20</td><td>Each 1% utilization gain doubles tail wait.</td></tr>
<tr><td>0.99</td><td>100</td><td>One bad day from outage.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</Compare>

This is why production systems aim for 50-70% utilization in
steady state. You're not "leaving capacity on the table." You're
buying **headroom for spikes** and keeping the system off the
hyperbolic part of the curve.

<Callout type="rule">
At high utilization, the relationship between throughput and
latency is no longer monotonic in a useful way. Small bursts that
would be unremarkable at 70% utilization become outages at 95%.
</Callout>

## Why bursts matter

If your average is 70% utilization, a 30% burst still leaves you
in the well-behaved region. If your average is 95%, even small
variability tips you over the edge — and recovery from
saturation is **non-linear**: the queue built up during the
spike takes a long time to drain, so latency stays bad for
minutes after the spike ends.

The technical name: **bufferbloat in queueing**. The same physics
that causes huge queues in network buffers shows up in app-level
queues.

## More servers vs faster servers

You have a queueing problem. You can either add more workers (more
parallelism) or make each worker faster (lower service time).
Which is better?

For high-utilization queues, **adding workers helps more than
making the same worker faster** — because more workers reduce
variance.

This is the M/M/c result. Going from 1 server at 95% utilization
to 2 servers at 47.5% utilization (same arrival rate) doesn't
just halve wait time; it dramatically improves the tail. Many
short-running servers beat few long-running ones, *at fixed
total capacity*.

In practice:

- **Compute-bound workloads**: faster CPUs help. The work itself
  is the limit.
- **Mixed/wait-heavy workloads**: more parallel workers help. The
  arrivals' variance is the limit.

A common pattern: scale horizontally when capacity needs to grow,
not vertically. Smaller instances, more of them.

## SRT-1: shortest remaining time

Scheduling discipline matters for tail latency. Two flavors:

- **FIFO**: simple, fair, p99 dominated by long jobs holding up
  short ones (head-of-line blocking).
- **SRT (Shortest Remaining Time)**: each job's wait time matches
  its size. Big jobs wait longer; small ones zip through. p99 of
  short jobs drops dramatically.

Most systems use FIFO because the cost of estimating job size is
prohibitive. But there are exceptions: **separate queues per size
class** is the discrete approximation. Have a fast-lane for small
requests, a slow-lane for big ones.

Practical example: a worker pool that splits "small fast" requests
from "large slow" ones, so a big job doesn't head-of-line block
30 small ones behind it.

## Tail amplification by fan-out

When a user request fans out to N backends and waits for all of
them, the latency the user sees is the **maximum** of N independent
backend latencies. With independent backends:

```
P(user sees ≥ p99 latency)  ≈  1 - (1 - 0.01)^N
```

- N=1: 1%
- N=10: ~10%
- N=100: ~63%

For high fan-out services (a single page might trigger 50-100
backend calls), the p99 the user sees is much worse than any
individual backend's p99. The fix is to reduce N (cache, batch) or
reduce per-backend p99 to push amplification down.

## Putting it together: capacity planning

A useful diagnostic checklist for a service:

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Measure peak λ">
What's the arrival rate at peak? Real measurements, not estimates.
</Step>
<Step n="2" title="Measure W at peak">
Average latency. Then per-percentile (p50/p95/p99).
</Step>
<Step n="3" title="Compute L">
λ × W. Compare to your provisioned capacity (worker count, pool
size). Are you running at 30% of capacity? 90%?
</Step>
<Step n="4" title="Plot the latency vs throughput curve">
At what throughput does the knee start? You want to operate at
60-70% of that knee throughput.
</Step>
<Step n="5" title="Plan for 2-3× headroom">
Real spikes don't ask permission. Plan capacity that handles 2-3×
steady-state without crossing the knee.
</Step>
</Steps>

This is enough math to make decisions that beat "I dunno, scale
it up." More formal queueing theory exists (M/G/c, network of
queues, Jackson networks) but adds rigor without changing the
intuition.
