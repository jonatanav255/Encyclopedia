# Little's Law, Amdahl, and Universal Scalability

Three formulas, each a one-liner, each more useful than ten
intuitions. They tell you the bounds on what your system can do,
no matter how much you optimize.

## Little's Law (review)

```
L = λ × W
```

- **L** — concurrent items in the system at steady state
- **λ** — throughput (items per unit time)
- **W** — latency per item

Any two determine the third. It holds under almost any conditions.
This is the only one of the three that's a hard *equality* (the
other two are *upper bounds*).

Typical uses:

- "We do 5,000 req/s at 200ms latency. So we have 1,000 concurrent
  in-flight requests. Our connection pool, our worker pool,
  whatever's serving those — they all need to handle 1,000
  concurrent or we have a bottleneck."
- "If we double throughput at the same latency, in-flight
  doubles. Plan accordingly."

## Amdahl's Law

```
Speedup(N) = 1 / ((1 - p) + p / N)
```

- **N** — number of parallel workers / cores / shards
- **p** — fraction of the workload that's parallelizable (0 to 1)

The maximum theoretical speedup from adding parallelism. The bound
is:

```
max speedup = 1 / (1 - p)
```

If your workload is 90% parallel, the maximum speedup is **10×**,
no matter how many cores you throw at it. If 99% parallel, max is
100×. The remaining serial fraction is the floor that limits
everything.

What this tells you:

- **Adding cores doesn't help most workloads as much as you'd hope.**
  At p=0.9, going from 4 to 8 cores improves speed by ~17%, not
  2×.
- **The serial fraction is what you have to attack.** A 1%
  improvement in the serial part is often more valuable than
  doubling the parallel cores.
- **Diminishing returns are sharp.** The first few cores help
  hugely; the next few help less; eventually nothing helps.

The classic chart:

```
Speedup
   10 |.................   ← p=0.9, asymptote
    8 |        ___________
    6 |     __/
    4 |  __/
    2 |_/_____________________ p=0.5, asymptote at 2
       1    4    16    64   N
```

Note Amdahl's Law **never models negative effects**. It says
"speedup at N cores ≤ X." It doesn't say "speedup might *decrease*
beyond N cores." Real systems often do.

## Universal Scalability Law (USL)

Gunther's USL extends Amdahl by modeling **contention** (alpha) and
**coherence** (beta):

```
Speedup(N) = N / (1 + α(N - 1) + βN(N - 1))
```

- **α** — contention coefficient (serial-ish fraction)
- **β** — coherence coefficient (cost of cross-worker
  coordination)

The first two terms reproduce Amdahl. The β term — coherence —
matters because in real systems, adding workers means more
coordination between them: cache invalidations, lock cache-line
bouncing, mutex hot-spots, distributed consensus rounds. This
cost grows *quadratically* in N.

The result: **the USL has a maximum**. Past a certain N, adding
more workers makes things *worse*.

```
Speedup
   10 |       ___
    8 |      /   \___                  ← peak around N=8, then declines
    6 |     /
    4 |    /
    2 |   /
       1    4    16    64   N
```

This is what you actually see in benchmarks. Throughput rises with
N, peaks somewhere, then falls. The USL is the equation that
matches the curve.

Practical implications:

- **There's an optimal worker count** for any given workload. Past
  it, you lose. Find it empirically.
- **Reducing β has compounding returns.** Cutting coherence cost
  in half doesn't just speed up by 2× — it raises the peak N
  significantly.
- **Lock-free / share-nothing designs reduce β**, often dramatically.
  This is why "scale-out" architectures (independent instances)
  outperform "scale-up" (more cores per instance) at large N.

## Fitting USL to your system

A reasonable scaling experiment:

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Measure throughput at N=1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 workers">
Same workload, varying parallelism.
</Step>
<Step n="2" title="Plot throughput vs N">
Eyeball the curve.
</Step>
<Step n="3" title="Fit USL coefficients">
Tools like the `usl` R package, or just by hand: find the α that
matches the early sublinearity, find the β that matches the
peak/decline.
</Step>
<Step n="4" title="Predict N_max">
The N at which throughput peaks. Operate near it but not past it.
</Step>
</Steps>

A typical software service has α in the range 0.01-0.1 (some
genuinely serial parts) and β in the range 0.001-0.01 (some
coordination overhead). N_max often lands at 8-32 workers per
machine, after which more workers hurt.

## How the three connect

- **Amdahl** bounds *peak* parallel speedup.
- **USL** explains *where* peak occurs and what happens past it.
- **Little** tells you the *concurrency level* you need to support
  to achieve a given (throughput, latency) point.

A flow that uses all three:

- "We need 10,000 req/s at 200ms latency."
- Little: 2,000 concurrent in-flight requests.
- USL: at our measured α=0.05, β=0.001, peak speedup is at N≈30.
- Therefore: we need ceil(2000/30) ≈ 67 instances, each running
  30 workers, to hit the throughput at the latency target.

This is the kind of math that turns "we need to scale" into a
concrete plan.

## The serial fraction is the limit

The biggest practical takeaway from Amdahl and USL: **focus on
reducing the serial fraction**. Locks, single-leader writes,
cache invalidations, sequence-number generators — these are the
parts that bound scaling.

Patterns that reduce serial fraction:

- **Sharding by key**: each key's work is serial within its
  shard but parallel across shards.
- **Eventually consistent reads**: no leader-coordination on
  reads.
- **Conflict-free replicated data types (CRDTs)**: writes from
  multiple replicas merge without coordination.
- **Read replicas + write leader**: at least scales reads.

Patterns that *increase* serial fraction (avoid):

- A global lock around hot state.
- A single sequence number that all writes must claim.
- A leader election protocol triggered on every write.

The pattern is clear: **anything that requires coordination
across all workers is a serial fraction**, and Amdahl says it
caps your total speedup.

## A useful three-word summary

- Little: *conservation* (concurrency = throughput × latency).
- Amdahl: *speedup is bounded by the serial fraction*.
- USL: *coordination cost eventually wins*.

Memorize these and you can reason about most scaling questions
faster than running another benchmark.
