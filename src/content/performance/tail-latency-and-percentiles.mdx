# Tail latency and percentiles

The most consequential idea in service performance after Little's
Law. Once you internalize it, you stop reporting averages.

## Why averages mislead

Real latency distributions are heavily right-skewed. Most requests
are fast; a small fraction are dramatically slow. Their *mean*
sits below the slow tail; their *median* sits even lower.

A real-shape histogram:

```
count
  ^
  | *
  | **
  | ***
  | ****
  | *****
  | ******    *                              *
  +---------------------- latency ----------------->
   1ms                 50ms       250ms          2s
```

Mean ≈ 60ms. Median ≈ 30ms. p99 ≈ 800ms. Max ≈ 2s.

The mean tells you the wrong story. **The slowest 1% of requests
is where every real customer-impact event lives.**

## Percentiles, properly

A percentile p`N` is the value below which `N%` of observations fall.

- **p50** (median): half of requests faster, half slower.
- **p95**: 95% of requests are faster. 5% are slower.
- **p99**: 1% of requests are slower. *One in a hundred.*
- **p99.9** ("three nines"): 1 in 1,000.
- **p99.99**: 1 in 10,000.

Reporting two percentiles is more honest than one. A useful
baseline summary: **p50, p95, p99**. Add p99.9 when you have the
volume to estimate it (you need at least 10,000 samples for a
stable p99.9; less than that and your "p99.9" is noise from
individual events).

## Why tail latency is dispproportionately important

Three reasons:

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Users feel the slowest, not the typical">
A user who experiences a slow request remembers it. The user who
got the median doesn't think about it. Tail experiences shape
product perception.
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Tail amplification on fan-out">
Modern services fan out: one user request becomes 10 internal
calls. The user waits for the slowest. If each backend's p99 is
800ms but they're independent, the **p99 of the slowest of 10**
is approximately p99.9 of one call — substantially worse.

```
P(at least one slow) = 1 - (1 - 0.01)^10 ≈ 0.096

≈ 10% of user requests get the "p99 experience"
```

The user sees the **slowest of 10 backends' tails** as their
latency. The p99 of the user-facing service is much closer to
the p99.9 of any individual backend than to its p99.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="Retries make it worse">
A request times out at p95 → retry. The retry adds latency. A
naive setup turns slow requests into doubly-slow requests.
Without hedging or careful retry policies, tail goes from p99 to
"forever."
</Step>
</Steps>

## What causes tail spikes

Tails are made of *correlated* slow events:

- **GC pauses.** A GC stops the world for 50-200ms. Anything
  running at that moment lands in the tail. Languages with
  generational/concurrent collectors push these to the high
  percentiles but they're still there.
- **Lock contention.** Most requests don't fight for the lock;
  one in a hundred does. That one waits.
- **CPU scheduling.** Your thread was de-scheduled. Modern Linux
  CFS is usually fine, but cgroup CPU limits cause exactly this:
  steady-state fine, occasional throttling spikes.
- **Network glitches.** A retransmit costs the request 200ms.
  Doesn't happen often; when it does, it's the tail.
- **Cold caches / first-of-its-kind requests.** First request to
  a new partition fetches from disk; second through Nth hit the
  buffer.
- **Coordinated omission** — see below.

## Measuring percentiles correctly

The hidden trap: **you can't average percentiles**. Two services
each at p99=200ms have *some* combined p99, but it isn't 200ms,
isn't 400ms, and you can't derive it from the individual p99s.

To measure correctly, you store the distribution (histogram, HDR
histogram, t-digest) and compute percentiles globally. Storing
"p99 per minute" and averaging across minutes is wrong.

Tools that do this right:

- **HdrHistogram** — high-precision histograms, mergeable. The
  gold standard.
- **t-digest** — approximate, very mergeable. Used by Datadog,
  AWS CloudWatch.
- **Prometheus histograms** — fixed bucket boundaries; merging
  works but accuracy depends on bucket choice.
- **Prometheus summaries** — pre-computed quantiles per instance;
  **not** mergeable. Don't use for service-wide percentiles.

## Coordinated omission

The most pernicious tail-latency measurement bug, named by Gil
Tene. A client issues one request per second; if the server
stalls for 10s, the client only issues one request during the
stall (because the previous one blocked). Result: the 10s outage
is recorded as **one slow request**, not 10. The percentile reports
near-zero impact from a 10-second outage.

Two fixes:

- **Open-loop load**: requests fire on schedule regardless of
  whether the previous one returned. wrk2 and many other modern
  load testers default to this.
- **CO correction in the recorder**: HdrHistogram has a
  "record with expected interval" API that interpolates missing
  samples during stalls.

If you've ever measured "p99 = 30ms" while users complained,
coordinated omission is the likely cause.

## Working on the tail

Reducing tail latency is structurally different from reducing
mean latency. Three approaches:

- **Reduce the variance.** Smaller GC pauses, smaller lock
  hold times, smaller per-request work. The tail is dominated
  by variance, not by mean.
- **Hedge or speculate.** Issue the same request twice to two
  backends after a short delay; take whichever returns first.
  Used by Google for tail amplification. Doubles load on the
  tails, drops the tail dramatically.
- **Cap the work.** Time-budgeted requests: cancel below tier
  if the request has consumed N ms. The user sees a less-detailed
  answer, but it arrives in time.

A useful framing: **the tail is what your worst-case dependency
plus your worst-case GC plus your worst-case CPU schedule looks
like, simultaneously.** Lowering it means cutting each contributor
or designing around them.

## Reporting in practice

A useful default dashboard panel:

- Top line: p50, p95, p99 over time.
- Second line: max over time (the worst single event).
- Histogram heatmap: distribution shape per minute.

Anyone who looks at a mean-latency dashboard and concludes "we're
fine" is missing the user-felt experience.
