# Capacity planning

Capacity planning is *deciding how much computer to buy* before
you need it. Done well, it prevents outages from sudden growth
and saves money from over-provisioning. Done poorly, it's either
"we're paying 4× too much" or "we're on fire again."

The basic shape: project demand, characterize cost per unit,
compute headroom, decide when and how to add capacity.

## The three honest questions

Capacity planning answers three questions, in order:

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="How much can each unit handle?">
What's the peak throughput one instance / pod / shard can serve
before it stops meeting SLO? **Not** "how fast can it go" — "how
fast can it go *while staying within latency budget*."
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="What's demand going to look like?">
Steady-state today, growth trajectory, daily/weekly seasonality,
expected spikes from product events.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="What's the safety margin?">
Headroom for surprises. Typically 2-3× expected steady-state at
peak.
</Step>
</Steps>

The math is the easy part. Getting honest numbers for the first
two is the hard part.

## Measuring per-unit capacity

The right number is **throughput at SLO**, not max throughput.
Concrete procedure:

- Run a load test against one instance.
- Ramp arrival rate up; watch the latency distribution.
- Find the rate where p99 hits the SLO ceiling.
- That rate is "1 unit's capacity."

For a service with `p99 < 300ms` SLO, an instance might max out
at 600 req/s on raw throughput but only sustain 400 req/s while
staying under 300ms p99. The capacity number is 400 req/s.

Be honest about the variance: capacity isn't a single number,
it's a distribution. A node provisioned today might serve 350
req/s; another, 450 req/s. Plan for the median, alert on
deviations.

## Demand projection

Three sources of demand:

- **Organic growth** — usually fittable to an exponential or
  logistic curve based on history. Linear extrapolation
  over-estimates if you're in maturity, under-estimates if
  you're in growth.
- **Seasonality** — daily/weekly/yearly. Black Friday, holiday
  retail, Monday morning login rush. Easy to forecast from
  historical data.
- **Event-driven spikes** — product launches, marketing
  campaigns, news mentions. Pre-coordinate with product.

A useful framing: **peak hour** (often 2-4× steady-state) drives
your provisioning, not the average. Designing for average
guarantees you're under-provisioned at peak.

## The headroom factor

How much buffer above peak? Conventional wisdom:

- **2× peak**: standard for stateless services with fast scaling.
- **3-4× peak**: for services with slow scaling (warm-up, leader
  election, sharded state) or strict SLOs.
- **Under 2× peak**: dangerous unless you're confident about
  auto-scaling reacting fast enough.

Reasons headroom matters even with auto-scaling:

- **Scaling has latency**: new instances take 30s to 5 min to
  warm up. The current instances need to handle the surge in the
  meantime.
- **Failure correlation**: AZ outage takes out 1/3 of capacity.
  Remaining 2/3 needs to absorb the load.
- **Latency-utilization curve**: at >80% utilization, latency
  grows hyperbolic. You want to be on the linear part.

## Stateful vs stateless

Stateless services scale by adding instances. Stateful systems
(databases, caches, queues) have more constraints:

- **DB read replicas** scale reads but writes are still single-leader-bound.
- **Sharded systems** scale by adding shards, but resharding is
  expensive — you have to plan ahead.
- **Caches** scale by adding nodes, but cold cache after scaling
  causes thundering herd on the source of truth.

For stateful systems, "capacity" includes write rate, storage
volume, shard count, replication factor. Capacity planning takes
longer (changes can take days to roll out) and matters more (you
can't react in 5 min).

## The cost dimension

A capacity plan that ignores cost is incomplete. The structure:

- **Per-unit cost** — what does one instance cost per hour?
- **Marginal cost of headroom** — 50% headroom on 100 instances
  = 50 idle instances of cost.
- **Reserved vs on-demand** — long-term commits get 30-60%
  discount. Useful for steady-state; on-demand for spikes.
- **Spot / preemptible** — even cheaper, but can be reclaimed.
  Use for batch / async workloads with retries.

A typical cost-conscious shape: reserved for steady-state floor,
on-demand for daily peak, spot for batch workloads, autoscaling
to fill the gap.

## Common failure modes

- **Provisioning for the average.** Average is fine; peak is when
  the outage happens.
- **Forgetting compounding** — DB connection pool sized for 10
  app servers, then you scale to 100 app servers. Now the DB has
  10× the connection pressure, which it can't handle.
- **Cliff effects.** Adding the 11th node trips the next limit
  (DB max connections, kernel socket limit, S3 partition limit).
  Capacity is non-linear once you cross a limit.
- **Single-AZ planning** — capacity adequate when nothing fails,
  insufficient when one AZ goes down.
- **Cache effectiveness assumptions** — capacity per node assumes
  current cache hit rate. New product feature changes the
  access pattern; hit rate drops; effective capacity halves.
- **Auto-scaling lag** — auto-scaling responds to current
  metrics, which is the past. Lagging indicators can over-shoot
  and under-shoot.

## Modeling: Erlang and queueing

For services where queueing is a major factor (call centers,
real-time messaging, voice), Erlang C formulas estimate how many
agents/servers are needed to keep wait times under a threshold,
given arrival rate and service time distribution.

For typical web services, M/M/c gets close enough. The key
intuition: **more parallel servers beat faster servers at fixed
total capacity**, because variance gets smoothed across more
units. See `queueing-theory`.

## A capacity planning checklist

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Measure current per-unit capacity">
At SLO, not at max throughput. Update quarterly.
</Step>
<Step n="2" title="Forecast 1-quarter and 1-year demand">
Include seasonality, expected launches. Mark uncertainty
explicitly.
</Step>
<Step n="3" title="Compute provisioning at 2-3× peak">
For each tier: app, DB, cache, queue. Don't forget the dependent
systems.
</Step>
<Step n="4" title="Identify cliffs">
Where do you hit a hard limit (max connections, AZ capacity,
account quota)? File requests in advance.
</Step>
<Step n="5" title="Track utilization at peak">
The leading indicator of when you'll need more.
</Step>
<Step n="6" title="Run a chaos exercise">
Take out an AZ. Does remaining capacity sustain SLO? If not,
you're not actually planning for the failure case.
</Step>
</Steps>

## A reasonable cadence

- **Monthly**: review utilization trends, top growth services.
- **Quarterly**: full capacity plan refresh; reserved-capacity
  decisions.
- **Annually**: strategic limits — datacenter contracts, multi-region
  decisions, big architectural changes (sharding strategy, CDN
  tier).
- **Event-driven**: ad-hoc reviews before known traffic events.

Capacity planning isn't glamorous. It also isn't optional, and
the cost of skipping it is the kind of incident that gets a
postmortem.
