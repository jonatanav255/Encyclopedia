# Recommender systems in prod

A production recommender is two systems pretending to be one. The
**model side** ranks items by relevance to a user. The **system
side** retrieves candidates, fans out to rankers, reranks for
business rules, and serves in tens of milliseconds. Most teams
that try to "just use embeddings" learn the hard way that the
model is the easy 30% and the pipeline is the other 70%.

For the **system layout** — services, indexes, caching, fanout
shape — see `system-design/recommendation-system-architecture.mdx`.
This page is the **model side**: candidate generation, ranking,
the metrics that lie, and the feedback-loop pathologies that
ruin recommenders given time.

## The four-stage pipeline

The industry standard shape, regardless of domain:

Why a funnel at all: the heavy ranker is a deep model running on
GPU-or-equivalent, and **you cannot afford to run it on 10
million items per request**. So you cheaply narrow first, then
spend latency budget on the items most likely to matter.

Stage budgets in a typical 100ms p99 system:

- **Candidate generation**: 20–30ms, returns 500–5000 items.
- **Light rank**: 10–20ms, scores cheaply, keeps top 100–200.
- **Heavy rank**: 30–50ms, deep model, fine-grained scores.
- **Rerank**: 5–10ms, diversification, freshness, policy filters.

Latency-wise, candidate generation is the surprise — it has to
be sub-30ms over a corpus of millions of items, which is why ANN
indexes (HNSW, IVF, ScaNN) exist.

## Candidate generation: where two-tower lives

The two main families and where they earn their place.

### Collaborative filtering (matrix factorization, ALS)

Build a user × item interaction matrix; factor it into low-rank
user and item vectors. Classic, still useful for cold-ish users
with some history. Falk's *Practical Recommender Systems*
treats this exhaustively. Problems:

- **Cold-start.** New users and new items have no row/column.
- **Sparse interactions.** Most users have engaged with &lt;1% of
  items.
- **Retrain cost.** Re-factoring a billion-row matrix nightly is
  not cheap.

### Two-tower retrieval (the modern default)

Train one tower that maps `(user features, history)` to a vector;
another tower that maps `(item features)` to a vector. Optimize
so that dot-product `(user, positive_item)` > dot-product `(user,
negative_item)`. At serving time:

1. Encode the user → query vector.
2. ANN-search the item vector index → top K candidates.
3. Hand off to ranking.

The wins:

- **Decoupled** — item vectors are precomputed and indexed;
  user encoding is online but cheap.
- **Handles content features**, so new items get reasonable
  vectors before they have any interactions.
- **Scales** to billion-item corpora via HNSW or IVF-PQ indexes.

The traps:

- The **two towers are trained jointly**, so a change to one
  side means re-encoding the other. Item vectors drift; the
  index has to be rebuilt.
- The **dot-product objective** is convex in vector space but
  doesn't encode business rules — "show diverse items," "don't
  show items the user already saw" — those live in rerank.
- **Negative sampling matters.** In-batch negatives produce
  retrieval that overfits to popular items. Hard negatives
  (items the user *almost* engaged with) are the standard fix.

### Hybrid

In practice you generate candidates from multiple sources and
union them:

- Two-tower retrieval (personalized).
- Item-to-item ANN (similar to what they just engaged with).
- Trending / fresh items (cold-start fallback, novelty).
- Editorial (curated lists for high-stakes slots).

A typical Netflix-shaped homepage is the union of 8–20 candidate
generators, each contributing rows. The ranker decides ordering
across them.

## Ranking: it's a classifier you happen to sort by

The heavy ranker is usually a **pointwise binary classifier**
trained to predict "will this user engage with this item?" —
P(click), P(watch >30s), P(purchase). At serving time you sort
by predicted probability.

Common architectures:

- **Wide-and-deep** (Cheng et al., 2016) — wide linear part for
  memorization, deep MLP for generalization. Old but a strong
  baseline.
- **DLRM / DCN** — Facebook / Google deep-learning rankers
  designed for high-cardinality categoricals via embedding
  tables.
- **Gradient-boosted trees** (XGBoost, LightGBM) — still beat
  deep models on small-to-medium-feature problems with strong
  tabular features. Easy to train, easy to debug, often the
  pragmatic choice.

Multi-objective is where it gets real:

- You don't actually want to maximize CTR. You want CTR *and*
  watch time *and* not-fatigue *and* diversity. The standard
  trick is to predict multiple targets jointly (multi-head
  network), then combine scores at rerank with weights tuned
  to business outcomes.
- Pareto frontier between objectives is real — pushing CTR
  cannibalizes long-term retention. This is where ML meets
  product judgment.

## Cold start: the unavoidable problem

The categories and what actually works:

<Cheatsheet caption="Cold-start strategies that earn their keep">
| Variant | What it means | Practical defense |
|---|---|---|
| New user, no history | Just signed up | Onboarding signals (region, language, declared interests); popular-in-segment fallback; bandit exploration |
| Returning user, sparse history | A few interactions | Content-based retrieval on items they touched; popularity backoff |
| New item, no engagement | Just uploaded | Content features in the item tower; trending lift on first few views; editorial slots |
| New domain entirely | Launching in a new country | Pretrain on global data; fine-tune on local; expect 2-3 months of quality lag |
</Cheatsheet>

For new items, the **bootstrapping problem** is structural — you
can't learn what users like about an item until users see it,
but users won't see it until you rank it. The two real fixes:

1. **Use content features** so the item tower produces a
   reasonable vector before any interactions.
2. **Reserve exploration slots** — a fixed % of impressions
   that go to under-explored items, even if their predicted
   score is lower. Thompson sampling or epsilon-greedy. Cost:
   short-term CTR. Benefit: a recommender that doesn't ossify.

## Feedback loops and filter bubbles

The most underrated production failure mode. Your recommender
sees what the user clicked. The user only clicks what you
showed. **You are training on data that you generated.**

The pathologies:

- **Popularity bias.** Popular items get shown more, get
  clicked more, get predicted to be more popular. The
  long-tail starves.
- **Filter bubble.** Users who clicked political-content-X get
  shown more X-aligned content, never see other views, your
  metrics report engagement is up while the system narrows the
  user's diet.
- **Confirmation loop.** A user accidentally clicks an item;
  the recommender doubles down; the user now sees more of the
  category they didn't actually want.
- **Position bias.** Items at position 1 get clicked far more
  than position 10 regardless of relevance. If you train on
  raw clicks, you're training a "predict position" model, not
  a relevance model.

Mitigations (none are free):

- **Inverse propensity scoring (IPS)** for training labels —
  weight clicks by 1 / P(was shown). Reduces popularity bias in
  training.
- **Position bias models** — explicitly model P(click | shown
  at position k); decouple from relevance.
- **Diversity terms** in rerank — penalize too-similar items
  in the result set. MMR, DPP, or simpler greedy diversity.
- **Exploration slots** — see cold-start; same defense.
- **User-facing controls** — "show me less of this" gives the
  user a way to escape the bubble; it also provides clean
  negative training data.

The point isn't to "fix" the feedback loop — you can't, you're
always training on data influenced by past recommendations. The
point is to **build defenses that keep the loop loose enough**
that the system doesn't collapse into showing every user the
same 50 most-popular items.

## Online vs offline metrics

The single biggest trap in recommender work.

<Compare>
<thead>
<tr><th></th><th>Offline metrics</th><th>Online metrics</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>What they measure</td><td>Model quality on held-out historical data</td><td>User behavior in production</td></tr>
<tr><td>Examples</td><td>AUC, NDCG@k, MAP, MRR</td><td>CTR, conversion, watch time, retention</td></tr>
<tr><td>Time to compute</td><td>Minutes (after training)</td><td>Days to weeks (A/B tests need stat-sig)</td></tr>
<tr><td>Tells the truth?</td><td>Usually no</td><td>Usually yes</td></tr>
</tbody>
</Compare>

Offline metrics improve. Online metrics don't move, or move
*opposite* to offline. Why:

- Offline data is what *the old model showed*. A new model that
  would have shown different items has no labels for those items
  — it gets penalized for showing things the old model didn't.
- Offline AUC weighs all items equally; online users only see
  the top few.
- Online has selection effects, novelty, fatigue, and
  ecosystem-level dynamics offline can't simulate.

The discipline:

- **Use offline metrics to gate experiments** — if offline
  drops, don't ship.
- **But ship is decided by online A/B** — offline win is
  necessary, not sufficient.
- **Counterfactual evaluation** (Bottou et al., off-policy
  estimators) can help when A/B is expensive, but it's a
  research-grade tool with strong assumptions.

The healthiest team I've seen had a wall display of "offline
delta vs online delta for the last 30 experiments" — a scatter
plot of how often the two agreed. The slope was lower than
anyone wanted; the correlation was real but messy.

## Serving constraints that shape the model

The model doesn't get to ignore the system:

- **Latency budget.** Heavy ranker has 30–50ms for ~100 items.
  This caps model size (parameter count, feature count). A
  brilliant transformer ranker that takes 200ms is useless.
- **Throughput.** A homepage is ~10 ranked slots × hundreds of
  candidates × millions of users. GPU rankers need batching
  and careful memory management.
- **Freshness.** Item features (popularity in last hour, recent
  engagement) need to be in the online store within minutes,
  not days. See `ai/mlops-pipelines-and-feature-stores.mdx`.
- **Consistency.** Two requests from the same user within the
  same session should not show wildly different rankings unless
  state changed. Session-level caching is normal.

## What good looks like

- Two-tower retrieval + hybrid candidate sources with explicit
  budgets.
- Heavy ranker with multi-objective heads; weights tuned via
  online experiments, not gut.
- Cold-start handled by content features + exploration slots,
  not "popular items for new users" alone.
- Feedback-loop defenses (IPS, position-bias modeling, diversity
  terms) wired into training and rerank.
- Offline metrics treated as a gate, online A/B as the truth.
- Per-segment online metrics — overall lift hides regressions on
  cohorts.
- An on-call rotation that owns recommender quality the same way
  an SRE team owns latency.

## The discipline

- **The funnel is not optional.** You cannot rank a million
  items per request; design for the budget.
- **Two-tower retrieval is the modern default**, but cold-start
  and feedback loops are not fixed by it.
- **Online metrics are the ones that matter.** Offline AUC is
  a gate, not a verdict.
- **You are always training on data your past model generated.**
  Build defenses against the loop; don't pretend it isn't there.
- **The system around the model is most of the engineering.**
  See `system-design/recommendation-system-architecture.mdx`
  for the system half of the picture.
