# Bandits and online learning

The honest take: most of what gets shipped as "personalization,"
"recommender," or "smart ranking" in production is a **bandit**,
not RL. Full reinforcement learning needs state transitions and
delayed rewards over a horizon — the things that make Atari and
robotics hard. A bandit is the degenerate case: one decision, an
immediate reward, do it again. That's almost every product
decision you'd want to learn online: which thumbnail, which
notification copy, which email subject line, which of these 20
candidate items goes in slot 1.

You don't need policy gradients for this. You need to think
clearly about explore-vs-exploit, and stop running A/B tests for
problems that A/B is the wrong tool for.

## The multi-armed bandit, plain

K levers (arms). Each pull of arm `i` returns a reward drawn
from an unknown distribution with mean `μ_i`. You don't know
which arm has the highest `μ`. You pull arms one at a time and
want to maximize total reward over T rounds.

The tension is built into the setup:

- **Exploit.** Pull the arm whose observed mean is highest so
  far — you'll get the most reward *if your estimate is right*.
- **Explore.** Pull a less-tried arm to improve your estimate
  — you'll learn faster *but pay short-term reward*.

If you only exploit, you lock in whatever looked good on the
first 3 pulls and never discover the actually-best arm. If you
only explore, you pull arms uniformly forever and waste reward
you should have collected.

A bandit algorithm is just a rule for picking the next arm that
balances the two.

## The four algorithms you'll actually use

<Cheatsheet caption="Bandit algorithms in increasing order of how often they're the right pick">
| Algorithm | Rule | Knob | When |
|---|---|---|---|
| Epsilon-greedy | With prob ε pull random; else best-so-far | ε (typically 0.05–0.1) | Baseline; works; tune ε by hand |
| Decaying epsilon | ε shrinks over time (e.g. 1/sqrt(t)) | initial ε | When traffic is steady and arms don't change |
| UCB1 | Pull `argmax μ̂_i + sqrt(2 ln t / n_i)` | None (parameter-free) | When you want a defensible, no-tuning default |
| Thompson sampling | Sample θ_i from posterior of each arm, pull argmax | Prior (usually Beta(1,1)) | The modern default; Bayesian, smooth, robust |
</Cheatsheet>

Two things that bite people new to this:

- **Epsilon-greedy is genuinely fine** for most product
  decisions. It is not embarrassing to ship. It loses ~5–10%
  of optimal reward to its random exploration, which is much
  less than you'll lose to a broken metric or a wrong reward
  signal.
- **Thompson sampling** is the one I'd reach for if I were
  doing this from scratch in 2026. It naturally pulls arms in
  proportion to the probability they're best — no tuning, no
  weird step-function behavior, and the math composes cleanly
  with Bayesian priors when you have informative beliefs about
  new arms.

UCB is great in papers. In production its bonus term
(`sqrt(2 ln t / n_i)`) is unintuitive to operate and Thompson
sampling does the same job with fewer surprises.

## Contextual bandits: the version that pays the bills

The vanilla MAB assumes arms have a single fixed reward
distribution. In a real product, the reward depends on **who's
asking**. A thumbnail that works for one user demographic
bombs for another. So you add features.

A **contextual bandit** is a MAB where each round you observe a
feature vector `x_t` (user, device, time, session history) and
you want to learn a function `μ_i(x)` — the expected reward of
arm `i` given context `x`. You pull the arm with highest
predicted reward, with some exploration.

Two common implementations:

- **LinUCB** — linear model per arm, UCB-style bonus on the
  predicted variance. Cheap, interpretable.
- **Neural / GBM contextual bandit** — train a model that takes
  `(x, arm)` features and predicts reward. Pick by argmax over
  arms, with Thompson sampling via dropout or ensembling for
  exploration. This is what most production "personalization"
  systems are under the hood.

If you've ever seen a job posting that says "Personalization ML
Engineer," it's almost always asking for a contextual bandit
plus the infrastructure to log impressions, rewards, and run
the loop online. Sutton &amp; Barto call it the bridge between
supervised learning and full RL; in practice it's its own thing.

## Why bandits beat A/B tests for ranking-shaped problems

A/B test logic:

1. Pick variant A or B uniformly at random.
2. Collect data for N days.
3. Compute lift, decide winner, ship.

That's a bandit with `ε = 1` and a single decision point at the
end. You explored maximally the entire time. If A was 50% worse
than B, you served the bad variant to half your users for the
full test duration. That's the *intended* tradeoff — you bought
a clean statistical estimate.

For "which of these 20 candidate items goes in slot 1," that
tradeoff is awful. You have:

- **Many arms** (every item, every layout, every ranking
  weighting).
- **Cheap exploration cost per arm** (one impression).
- **No need for a clean treatment-effect estimate** — you don't
  care *why* item 7 is winning, you just want to keep showing
  it.

Bandits dominate here. Epsilon-greedy with ε=0.05 spends 5% of
traffic exploring and 95% exploiting; A/B testing spends 100%
exploring until the test concludes. Over a year, bandits
collect roughly an order of magnitude more reward.

<Callout type="info">
The mental model that helped me: A/B testing is bandit with
ε=1 forever. Bandits are A/B with ε that decreases as you learn.
Same family, different settings.
</Callout>

## When NOT to use bandits

The honest counter-cases. You want A/B when:

- **You need a counterfactual estimate of business impact.**
  "Did this feature lift revenue 2%?" requires a clean
  treatment / control split with no contamination. Bandits
  contaminate by design — the policy adapts during the
  experiment, so the variants weren't sampled independently.
- **You're shipping a single, durable decision.** New checkout
  flow vs old. You won't keep both forever; you'll pick one.
  Get a clean estimate, ship the winner.
- **The reward signal is delayed by days.** Bandits assume you
  observe reward soon after the action. If "did this user
  retain" takes 30 days, the bandit can't learn fast enough to
  beat A/B's clean statistical machinery.
- **You're going to make policy / legal / fairness claims** on
  the result. Adaptive sampling complicates the statistics
  enough that you'll spend more time defending the analysis
  than running the experiment.

The pattern teams converge on: **bandits for the within-product
"which item, which copy, which slot" decisions, A/B for "did
this big-bet feature work."** Falk's *Practical Recommender
Systems* and AI Engineering both make this split explicit.

## The "exploration looks like a bug to the PM" problem

A real conversation that will happen to you:

> PM: "User 12345 keeps seeing weird items recommended. Item
> 47 has nothing to do with their history."
>
> You: "We're allocating ~5% of impressions to exploration. We
> need to learn whether item 47 works for users like 12345."
>
> PM: "That looks broken. Can you turn it off?"

The political problem with bandits is that exploration looks
like a bug. A user sees a recommendation that doesn't fit their
history; the PM thinks the system is malfunctioning. The
explanation — "we are deliberately taking short-term suboptimal
actions to learn long-term optimal ones" — is real but doesn't
land in a Slack thread.

What helps:

- **Bound exploration tightly on high-stakes slots.** Top slot
  on the home page is mostly exploit. Lower slots and "more
  like this" rows can carry more exploration.
- **Log exploration vs exploitation as a metric.** "Today we
  spent 4.7% of impressions on exploration; expected lift from
  this learning over 30 days is X." Makes the cost visible
  and accountable.
- **Cap the surprise factor.** Don't let exploration pull from
  the whole catalog uniformly; restrict to a "candidate set"
  the user might plausibly like. Pure random recs *do* feel
  broken.
- **Reserve a small holdout** that gets no exploration — pure
  exploit — and report the gap. Lets you defend the
  exploration spend with data.

## The infrastructure you actually need

Bandits look simple in pseudocode and require non-trivial
infra in production.

<Steps>
<Step title="Impression logging">
Every recommendation you serve must be logged with: arm picked,
context features at decision time, exploration vs exploit flag,
the bandit's predicted reward. Sample logging is not enough —
biased toward exploit. You need every impression.
</Step>
<Step title="Reward attribution">
Map user events (click, purchase, dwell) back to the
impressions that caused them. Position bias, attribution
windows, multi-touch — all the standard analytics traps.
Without this you have no reward signal.
</Step>
<Step title="Policy refresh">
The bandit's "what's the best arm" estimate has to update.
Options: micro-batch (recompute every 5 min from a rolling
window), online (per-event updates with a feature store), or
nightly batch (cheap, slower learning). Pick by latency need.
</Step>
<Step title="Off-policy evaluation">
Before deploying a new bandit policy, evaluate it on logged
data from the old policy. Inverse propensity scoring (IPS) is
the standard technique. Without OPE you're shipping policy
changes blind.
</Step>
<Step title="Safe rollout">
Same rules as ML model deploys — canary on a small slice,
compare to the previous policy, automatic rollback on regression.
See `ai/mlops-pipelines-and-feature-stores.mdx`.
</Step>
</Steps>

If you can't do all five, you don't have a bandit system, you
have a bandit-flavored heuristic. That's fine for prototypes;
don't pretend otherwise.

## Off-policy evaluation, briefly

The trap: you trained a new policy that scores higher on
offline metrics. Will it actually win in production? The
naive comparison is unfair — the new policy wants to show
different items, but you only have rewards for the items the
*old* policy showed.

The fix: **inverse propensity scoring**. Weight each logged
reward by `1 / P(old policy would have shown this)`. The
weighted reward is an unbiased estimate of the new policy's
performance. Two practical caveats:

- **Variance explodes** when the old policy almost never showed
  what the new policy wants to show — small propensities, huge
  weights, noisy estimate.
- **You need to log propensities.** If your old policy didn't
  record `P(arm | context)` at decision time, you can't compute
  IPS retroactively. Log propensities from day one.

Doubly-robust estimators (DR, SNIPS, switch-DR) trade some
variance for some bias. In practice, IPS is the one you
implement first; DR is the one you reach for when IPS variance
is killing your signal.

## The discipline

- Bandits are MAB with explore/exploit. Contextual bandits are
  MAB with features. Full RL needs state transitions and a
  horizon — usually you don't have those.
- Epsilon-greedy is fine. Thompson sampling is better. UCB is
  a paper algorithm.
- Use bandits for "which item / copy / slot," A/B for "did this
  big feature work." Don't fight the tools.
- Exploration looks like a bug. Make it visible, bound it,
  defend it with numbers.
- Log every impression with context, arm, propensity, exploit
  flag. No logs, no learning, no OPE.
- Off-policy evaluation with IPS before any policy change ships.
