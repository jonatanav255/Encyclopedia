# Attention and context windows

The context window is the bag of tokens the model can see in one
forward pass — system prompt, conversation history, retrieved
documents, the current user message, and any output it's generated
so far. Providers advertise it in big numbers ("1M tokens!") and
charge for it linearly, but the model's *effective* recall over that
window is not linear. Long context is real, useful, and full of
sharp edges.

This page is the engineer's view: what the context window is, what
it actually costs, and where it breaks. For the systems-level cost
of holding tokens in GPU memory, see
`ai/model-serving-and-inference-latency.mdx`.

## What the context window actually is

A decoder-only transformer takes a sequence of `N` tokens and
produces a probability distribution over the next token. `N` is
bounded by:

- A trained positional encoding (RoPE, ALiBi) with a maximum
  effective range.
- The KV cache memory available on the serving GPU.
- A hard `max_model_len` configured by the server.

When a provider says "200K context," they mean the model was
fine-tuned with positional encodings extending that far and the
inference server will accept that many input tokens. They do *not*
mean the model uses all of them equally.

The other side of the same coin: every token in the context window
sits in the KV cache for the duration of the request. KV cache
memory scales linearly with sequence length (see
`ai/model-serving-and-inference-latency.mdx` for the formula).
Roughly **0.15–1.5 MB per token** depending on model — a Llama 3
70B with GQA is on the lower end (~0.3 MB/token), a dense MHA model
on the upper end (~1.5 MB/token). A 200K-token request can demand
30–60 GB of KV cache by itself. That is the whole reason long
context is expensive.

## Quadratic attention, linear KV

The thing every junior engineer says: "attention is O(N²) so 1M
context must be 1M² operations." This is true for the math of one
attention layer in one forward pass, and misleading for what you
actually pay.

/>

So the quadratic cost shows up in prefill latency, not in steady
streaming. The linear KV-cache cost shows up in how many concurrent
long-context sessions you can hold. Different bottlenecks; track
them separately.

Practical sizing: for a 70B GQA model at fp16, expect roughly
**~0.3 MB per token** of KV cache per request (so ~300 MB for a 1K
prompt, ~30 GB at 100K). Bump to **~1.5 MB per token** for older
70B-class MHA models. Multiply by your concurrency target, then
subtract from GPU memory after weights. That number is your real
context budget.

## Lost in the middle

Liu et al. (2023), "Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use
Long Contexts," ran a controlled experiment: place a relevant
document at positions 1, 5, 10, 15, 20 of a 20-doc prompt, ask a
question that requires it, measure accuracy.

The result was a U-curve. Models recovered information near the
**start** and near the **end** of the context with high accuracy
(80%+); accuracy at the **middle** dropped to as low as 50% — worse
than feeding the model *no* context at all on some tasks.

This wasn't a bug in one model. GPT-3.5, Claude, and open-weight
models all showed the same shape. It hasn't gone away with bigger
context windows; if anything, the U gets wider.

What it means in practice:

- **Put the most important context at the start or end of the
  prompt.** System prompt at top, user query and retrieved
  documents at the bottom is a sensible default.
- **Don't dump 50 RAG hits in arbitrary order.** Reranking is not
  optional past ~10 hits — the model will under-attend to whatever
  lands in the middle.
- **Few-shot examples in the middle of a long prompt are
  effectively wasted.** Move them adjacent to the user query.

## Needle in a haystack — what it measures and what it doesn't

The "Needle in a Haystack" (NIAH) eval, popularized by Greg
Kamradt, drops a single specific fact ("the best food in San
Francisco is a Dungeness crab sandwich at Swan Oyster Depot") into
a long, irrelevant document (essays, Paul Graham, etc.) at varying
depths and lengths, then asks the model to retrieve it.

Modern frontier models claim near-100% NIAH accuracy across their
full context. Anthropic and OpenAI run versions of this on every
release.

Why it's not the whole story:

- NIAH is **single-fact extraction**. Real long-context tasks
  usually need *multi-hop synthesis* — combine fact A at position
  20K with fact B at position 180K and infer C. Models are
  dramatically worse at this.
- NIAH facts are **out-of-distribution noise** in their haystack.
  The "needle" is semantically distinct from the surrounding
  text. Real-world relevant chunks look similar to irrelevant
  ones — that's the hard case.
- NIAH doesn't measure **distraction** — what happens when there
  are 10 plausible-looking but wrong answers in the context. This
  is where production RAG actually breaks.

The honest read: NIAH at 100% means the model *can* attend to
position N. It does not mean the model *will* prefer position N
over plausible distractors. Build your own task-shaped eval before
shipping a long-context system.

<Callout type="warning">
The numbers we've seen in our own evals: a model that scores 100%
on single-needle NIAH at 500K tokens may score 30–50% on
"summarize the three contracts mentioned in the document" at the
same length. Long context recall on synthesis tasks degrades
sharply past ~64K tokens for most current models, even when the
provider advertises 1M.
</Callout>

## Sliding-window and sparse attention

Full attention every token to every other token is the brute-force
approach. Several models trade some of that for cheaper long
context:

<Cheatsheet
  columns={['Variant', 'What it does', 'Tradeoff']}
  rows={[
    ['Full attention', 'Every token attends to every prior token', 'O(N²); the default; best quality at moderate N'],
    ['Sliding-window (Mistral)', 'Each token attends only to last W tokens (W≈4096)', 'O(N×W); cheap; loses early context unless stacked with cache rotation'],
    ['Sparse / local + global', 'Most tokens local, a few "global" attend everywhere', 'Longformer-style; engineered for documents'],
    ['Ring attention', 'Distribute KV cache across GPUs in a ring; rotate K/V blocks', 'Scales to multi-million context across a cluster; serving complexity'],
    ['Linear attention / SSMs', 'Mamba, RWKV — recurrent state instead of attention', 'O(N) compute and memory; quality still trails strong transformers on most tasks'],
  ]}
  caption="Most production frontier models are still mostly full-attention with engineering tricks (chunked prefill, GQA, KV quantization)."
/>

Mistral's sliding-window in particular is worth understanding: it
sounds like "the model can only see the last 4K tokens," but
because attention stacks across layers, each layer's window is
4K but the effective receptive field after 32 layers is much
larger. In practice, sliding-window models recall far-back tokens
worse than dense-attention models, but at a fraction of the cost.

For our use case, the heuristic: **if you advertise long context
and use sliding-window attention, your recall past the window is
going to disappoint someone.** Read the model card carefully.

## KV cache and long context in production

The serving-side reality, expanded from
`ai/model-serving-and-inference-latency.mdx`:

- **One long-context request can starve everyone else.** A user
  with a 200K-token prompt holds 30 GB of KV cache. On an 80 GB
  H100 with 40 GB of weights, that's most of the remaining memory.
  Your concurrent capacity drops from 30 sessions to 2.
- **Chunked prefill is mandatory.** Without it, a 200K prefill
  freezes decode for every other session for 5–15 seconds.
- **KV cache quantization** (FP8 or INT8 KV) is the lever that
  buys you more concurrent long-context sessions. Quality cost
  is usually negligible; benchmark on your task.
- **Prompt caching** (Anthropic, OpenAI, Gemini all ship versions
  of this) lets you reuse the KV cache for a stable prompt prefix
  across requests. If you have a 50K-token system prompt that
  never changes, this is a ~90% TTFT and cost reduction on
  warm hits.

## The "we shipped 1M context" reality

Story from one team's deployment of a 1M-token long-context model
for legal document Q&A:

- NIAH at position 950K — 100%. Marketing page accurate.
- Multi-hop synthesis ("compare clauses across these three
  contracts") at 800K input tokens — **30% accuracy**, vs 85% at
  32K input tokens with retrieval over the same docs.
- TTFT at 800K input — **8.4 seconds**, vs 600ms with retrieval.
- Cost per query — **40× higher** than the retrieval baseline.

The lesson isn't "long context is fake." It's that "stuff
everything in the context window and trust the model" is the
wrong default. Retrieval + reranking + a 32K context window beats
1M context for almost every information task in 2026, on quality,
latency, and cost.

Long context shines for tasks where you *can't* retrieve:
processing a single coherent document end-to-end (a meeting
transcript, a single legal contract under 200K tokens, a codebase
that fits), or where the relevant context isn't keyword- or
embedding-discoverable.

## When to actually use long context

A practical decision tree:

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Does the task need synthesis across the document, or is it lookup?">
Lookup → RAG. The whole point of retrieval is to put the relevant
2K tokens in front of the model instead of 200K of noise.
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Does the corpus fit in context after dedup and compression?">
If your "corpus" is one 50-page meeting transcript, just paste it.
If it's a 10GB document store, RAG is the only option.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="Have you benchmarked recall at the depth you need?">
Run a task-specific eval at the context length you intend to use.
Don't trust NIAH; build a multi-hop eval over your own data.
</Step>

<Step n="4" title="Is the prefix stable enough to cache?">
If yes, prompt caching makes long-context economically viable.
If no — every request changes the long context — you'll pay full
prefill every time.
</Step>
</Steps>

## What we tell people

- The context window is a hard ceiling, not a usable area. Plan
  for effective recall to drop past ~64K tokens on synthesis
  tasks, even on models that advertise 1M.
- Put critical context at the start or end. The middle is where
  attention goes to die.
- NIAH is necessary but not sufficient. It tests extraction, not
  synthesis or distraction handling. Build your own eval.
- One long-context request can monopolize a GPU. Cap input length
  at the gateway and quote a separate price tier for long
  contexts if you offer them.
- Retrieval almost always beats stuffing the context window — on
  quality, cost, and latency. Long context is the fallback when
  retrieval can't find the right chunks, not the default.
- Prompt caching is the only thing that makes a 100K-token system
  prompt economically defensible. Without it, you pay full
  prefill on every request.
