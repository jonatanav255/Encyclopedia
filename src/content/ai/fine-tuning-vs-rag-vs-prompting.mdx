# Fine-tuning vs RAG vs prompting

The question gets asked at every AI design review: "should we fine-
tune the model?" The honest answer is "almost certainly not, yet."
Of the three levers you have — prompting, retrieval, fine-tuning —
they solve different problems, and 80% of teams that reach for
fine-tuning needed better prompts or better retrieval.

This page is the decision framework. For the building blocks
underneath, see `ai/prompt-patterns.mdx` (if it exists),
`ai/embeddings-fundamentals.mdx`, and
`ai/agent-loops-and-tool-use.mdx`.

The single most important sentence in this whole topic: **fine-
tuning teaches behavior, not knowledge.** A fine-tune is good at
"always respond in this JSON shape" or "use this brand tone of
voice." It is terrible at "remember these 5000 facts." The latter
is what RAG is for.

## The decision tree, top down

Walk this in order. Stop at the first lever that works.

<Steps>
<Step n="1" title="Can a better prompt fix it?">
Try this first, always. The 2026 prompt-engineering toolkit:
- Clearer instructions, fewer ambiguities.
- A handful of well-chosen few-shot examples (3-8).
- A system prompt that explicitly states the role and output format.
- Chain-of-thought via "think step by step" or the provider's
  thinking mode (Claude extended thinking, OpenAI o1-style).
- Structured output via JSON mode / tool calling.

This costs nothing to try, ships in an hour, and resolves 60-70% of
"the model is dumb" complaints. If it works, you're done. Pin the
prompt, version it, eval it.
</Step>

<Step n="2" title="Is the problem 'the model doesn't know this'?">
If the model lacks a fact, no amount of prompting will conjure it.
Two reasons it might be missing:
- **Recency** — the fact is newer than the model's training cutoff.
- **Privacy** — the fact is your private data the model has never
  seen (and shouldn't).

Both are RAG problems. Build a retrieval pipeline:
- Index your data with an embedding model (or hybrid BM25 + vector).
- At query time, retrieve top-K relevant chunks.
- Stuff them into the prompt.
- Cite sources back to the user.

RAG can be added in a week, iterated forever, and updated by
re-indexing — no model retrain. See `ai/embeddings-fundamentals.mdx`
for the building blocks.
</Step>

<Step n="3" title="Is the problem 'style, format, or task shape'?">
The model produces correct content but in the wrong format, voice,
or structure consistently. Examples:
- Always emit medical reports in the hospital's specific schema.
- Match the brand's casual tone, every time.
- Pick a label from a fixed taxonomy of 50.

This is what **LoRA / SFT** is good at. You fine-tune on a few
hundred to few thousand (input, output) pairs in the desired shape.
The model learns the *mapping*, not new facts.

Order of escalation:
- Constrained decoding (JSON Schema mode, regex constraints) for
  format — no fine-tune needed.
- Few-shot examples in the prompt for style — try first.
- LoRA fine-tune if the few-shot approach is too token-heavy, too
  inconsistent, or you want to move from a big expensive model to
  a smaller cheap one with style preserved.
</Step>

<Step n="4" title="Is the problem 'the base model is wrong for the domain'?">
Rare, but real. Medical, legal, code in an obscure language,
non-English languages with weak coverage. The base model
fundamentally lacks the right vocabulary or reasoning pattern.

Options, in order:
- A better base model (Med-PaLM, MedGPT-style domain models, or
  open-weights models pre-trained on your domain).
- Continued pre-training on your domain corpus (rare; expensive;
  usually not the right answer).
- Full SFT on a high-quality domain dataset.

This is the only place full fine-tuning earns its cost. Be sure.
</Step>
</Steps>

## The "model gets smarter next quarter" risk

The thing that nukes most fine-tuning investments: you spend three
months building a fine-tuned Llama-3-8B that scores 78% on your
eval. Next quarter, GPT-5 or Sonnet-5 ships and scores 92% on the
same eval with the same prompt — zero training, zero fine-tune.

The investment didn't just under-perform; it actively held you
back. You shipped a worse experience for 90 days while the rest of
the field moved. The cost wasn't the GPU hours; it was the
opportunity cost.

When this risk is highest:

- The base model you fine-tuned is small and the next-gen big model
  closes the gap on your task.
- Your training data isn't a proprietary moat (open-source examples
  the new model already saw).
- You're fine-tuning to improve raw capability, not to enforce a
  specific style or format the base model can't be made to follow.

When the risk is lower:

- You're fine-tuning to compress (small model + LoRA produces
  outputs as good as the big model at 1/20th the cost).
- Your training data is genuinely private — your customer support
  tickets, your code style, your domain corpus.
- You need on-device or air-gapped inference where you can't call
  GPT-5 anyway.

The decision rule that ages well: **fine-tune for cost reduction
and behavior shaping, not for capability gains.** Capability is
what the base-model arms race delivers. Don't try to out-compete
foundation labs at their game.

## RAG patterns that actually ship

RAG looks simple in a demo, hard in production. The bugs cluster:

- **Chunking strategy.** Splitting docs at fixed 1000-char
  boundaries cuts mid-sentence. Use semantic chunking, or chunk by
  document structure (sections, headings).
- **Pure-vector retrieval misses keywords.** A user search for
  "error code E1024" returns nothing — the embedding model doesn't
  encode the literal string well. **Hybrid search** (BM25 + vector,
  combined by reciprocal rank fusion) consistently beats pure
  vector for technical content. See `ai/embeddings-fundamentals.mdx`.
- **Top-K is a lie.** Setting `k=5` doesn't mean you got 5 relevant
  results; it means you got the 5 nearest in the embedding space.
  Always rerank with a cross-encoder (Cohere Rerank, or a local
  one). Reranking improves top-1 accuracy 20-40% in real evals.
- **Stale index.** Your data updates; the index doesn't. Add a
  staleness SLO. Reindex frequency depends on how fast truth
  changes.
- **Citation hallucination.** The model invents sources. Force
  cite-with-doc-ID in the output, and post-verify that the doc IDs
  exist in the retrieved set. Reject responses where they don't.

## When fine-tuning is genuinely the right answer

The cases that hold up:

- **Style transfer at scale.** You have 100K examples of "what
  good looks like" for your task. Few-shotting becomes
  prohibitively expensive in tokens; fine-tune a smaller model
  instead. Common in customer support, content moderation,
  structured extraction.
- **Format adherence.** The model needs to emit a specific schema
  every time, and JSON mode + prompt isn't getting you to four-nines
  compliance. A LoRA trained on the schema gets you there.
- **Task that resists few-shot.** Some tasks (subtle classification,
  domain-specific reasoning) just don't pattern-match from 8
  examples but do from 800. Fine-tune.
- **Cost compression.** Migrating from a workhorse-tier model
  (~$3/M input) to a fine-tuned cheap-tier model (~$0.15/M input)
  at the same quality is a real, repeatable win. The win is the
  cost ratio, not the capability.

## Common anti-patterns

- **Fine-tuning to teach facts.** "We'll fine-tune so it knows our
  product docs." It will memorize some, hallucinate others, refuse
  to update without retraining, and your eval scores will go down.
  Use RAG.
- **Fine-tuning before prompt iteration.** You haven't squeezed
  the base model yet. The marginal gain from fine-tune is small
  vs. the gain you left on the table in prompting.
- **Training on production outputs from a bigger model.** Distillation
  of GPT-4 outputs into a smaller fine-tune. Legally complicated
  (TOS issues), epistemically lossy (you train on hallucinations
  too). Some teams do it; do it with eyes open.
- **Fine-tuning with 50 examples.** Below ~500 examples, LoRA is
  noise. Below 2000, often noise. You need real data volume.
- **Never re-evaluating.** You fine-tuned six months ago. The base
  model has shipped two upgrades. Have you re-tested whether the
  fine-tune still adds anything?

## The eval discipline

Whichever lever you pull, the question "did this help" has the same
answer: **an eval set you trust**. The mistake everyone makes is
shipping a fine-tune or a new RAG pipeline based on a 20-example
spot-check.

What "trust" looks like:

- 200+ labeled examples covering the failure modes you've seen in
  production.
- A judge — either human, or LLM-as-judge with a calibrated rubric.
- Run on every model swap, every prompt change, every fine-tune,
  every RAG config change.
- Tracked over time as a metric. If quality drops, find out which
  change did it.

A team without an eval suite cannot reason about fine-tuning ROI.
They're guessing. See `ai/what-is-ai-engineering.mdx` for why
this discipline matters in general.

## Composability

These levers compose. The real production stack:

- **Strong base model** (the latest big foundation model).
- **Versioned prompt** with carefully chosen few-shots.
- **RAG** for the facts that aren't in the model.
- **Tools** for actions and live data.
- **Maybe a LoRA** on top, for style/format consistency.
- **Constrained decoding** for structured output.

Each lever fixes a class of problem. Stacking them is the norm.
Replacing one with another is the bug — they're complementary, not
substitutes.

## The discipline

- Prompt first. Always. It's free and ships today.
- If the model doesn't *know* something, RAG. Fine-tuning won't
  teach it facts reliably.
- If the model produces the wrong *shape* or *voice*, fine-tune is
  on the table — but try few-shot + constrained decoding first.
- Fine-tune for cost reduction or behavior shaping, never for raw
  capability gains. The base-model race will eat your investment.
- LoRA before full SFT. Full SFT before continued pre-training.
- Hybrid retrieval (BM25 + vector) + reranking beats pure vector
  for almost every real corpus.
- Every change goes through an eval suite of 200+ examples. No
  evals, no fine-tuning.
- Plan the upgrade path. When the next base model ships, will your
  fine-tune still pull its weight?
