# SRE fundamentals

Site Reliability Engineering — the discipline Google formalized
and the rest of the industry adopted — is "what happens when you
treat operations as a software problem." Most modern production
teams use SRE concepts even if they don't call themselves SREs.

This page covers the core practices: SLOs, error budgets, toil,
on-call, postmortems, and incident response.

## SLO, SLI, error budget

Three terms that anchor everything.

- **SLI (Service Level Indicator)**: a specific metric of service
  behavior. "Fraction of HTTP requests returning < 200ms."
- **SLO (Service Level Objective)**: a target for that metric.
  "99.5% of requests under 200ms over 28 days."
- **Error budget**: the inverse of the SLO. "0.5% of requests
  can fail (or be slow) per 28 days."

The error budget is **the budget for changes that might break
production**. As long as you're within budget, ship freely. If you
burn through it, slow down.

Without SLOs:

- Every error is an alert. Burnout.
- "Better is always better" — engineering pressure to chase 100%.
- No shared language for "good enough."

With SLOs:

- Alerts page only when you're burning budget faster than expected.
- "We have 50% of our budget left, we can ship the risky feature."
- Reliability and velocity become measurable trade-offs.

## Choosing SLIs

Pick metrics that reflect what users experience:

- **Latency**: response time as observed at the user (not the
  server).
- **Availability**: fraction of requests that succeed.
- **Throughput**: requests served per second (for batch).
- **Quality**: for ML / search, did the user get a good answer?
- **Freshness**: how recent is the data the user sees?

The **RED method** (Rate, Errors, Duration) per service is a
common starting point. **USE** (Utilization, Saturation, Errors) is
for infrastructure.

**Avoid**:

- Single-customer metrics (one big customer's outage skews
  everything).
- Vanity metrics that don't reflect user pain.
- Metrics you can't measure reliably.

## Setting SLO targets

The naïve approach: aim for "five nines" (99.999%). Almost never
the right answer.

Realistic targets:

- **99.9% (43m/month)** — typical "production-grade" API.
- **99.95% (22m/month)** — payment-critical or finance.
- **99.99% (4m/month)** — pager-life-altering. Demands HA
  everywhere, multi-region, zero-downtime deploys, etc.
- **99.999% (26s/month)** — Google-search-level. Reserved for
  infra layers most services depend on.

Each nine ~10× the engineering cost. Most B2B SaaS lives at 99.9%.
Be honest with yourself.

## Multi-window burn rate alerts

Naïve alerting: "page if error rate > 1% for 5 minutes." Two
problems:

- **Slow burns**: 0.5% error rate sustained for a week burns the
  budget but never trips the alert.
- **Spikes**: 50% errors for 30 seconds page everyone for nothing.

**Multi-window burn rate** considers how fast you're spending the
budget:

```
Page if:
  burn rate over 1h is > 14.4× normal  (would exhaust budget in 2d at this pace)
  AND
  burn rate over 5min is > 14.4× normal (real, not stale)
```

Two windows: short (real-time) + long (recent trend). Both must
agree before paging.

A second tier of alerts:

```
Ticket if:
  burn rate over 6h is > 6× normal
  AND
  burn rate over 30min is > 6× normal
```

Lower priority — investigate during business hours.

This pattern (from Google's SRE Workbook) cuts alert fatigue
dramatically. Pair with dashboards for the slow-burn cases that
don't quite page.

## Toil

Toil = repetitive, manual, automatable work that doesn't improve
the system long-term. "I have to manually restart the worker every
night." "I run this cleanup script weekly."

SRE rule of thumb: **toil should be < 50% of an SRE's time**.
Beyond that, the team can't make progress on engineering work.

Track toil:

- Time spent on tickets.
- Time spent on manual deploys.
- Time spent investigating false alerts.

Convert toil to automation:

- Self-healing systems (auto-restart on health failure).
- Runbooks → runbooks-as-code.
- Manual processes → CI pipelines.

The reward isn't just hours saved — it's the team's ability to
focus on reliability projects.

## On-call

Someone has to respond when production breaks. The discipline:

### Rotation

- **Primary + secondary**: at least two people on-call simultaneously.
- **Weekly rotation**: long enough to handle multi-day incidents,
  short enough not to burn out.
- **Follow-the-sun** for global teams: hand off at end of business
  hours.

### Compensation

On-call is real work. Pay extra (per shift), give comp days for
paged nights, or both. Volunteers-only on-call doesn't scale.

### Tooling

- **Alert routing** (PagerDuty, Opsgenie): wakes the right person
  with escalation.
- **Runbooks linked from every alert**: not "page someone, they
  remember what to do" — explicit steps in a wiki.
- **Just-enough access**: on-call has admin to fix the immediate
  problem. Audit trails preserve accountability.

### Workload

If on-call is busy (multiple pages per shift):

- Reduce alerts. Many "alerts" are tickets in disguise.
- Fix the underlying instability. Repeated pages = systemic issue.
- Add SLO budgets so unstable services slow their own deploys.

On-call that pages once a week is sustainable. Once a day is a
crisis.

## Incident management

When something breaks:

### Roles

- **Incident Commander (IC)**: coordinates, doesn't fix. Decides
  what to do; delegates execution.
- **Communications Lead**: customer + internal comms (Slack
  updates, status page).
- **Subject Matter Expert (SME)**: the engineer doing the actual
  fix.

For small incidents, one person plays all roles. For Sev-1, split.

### Communication

- **Single incident channel** in Slack (or equivalent). All
  discussion there.
- **Status page** updates every 30 minutes minimum.
- **Internal stakeholder updates** at the same cadence.
- **External (customer) communication** depends on impact —
  proactive for outages, transparent about timeline.

### Severity levels

- **Sev 1**: major customer-facing outage. All hands, war room,
  exec notified.
- **Sev 2**: degraded service or partial outage. On-call + secondary
  engaged.
- **Sev 3**: low-impact bug or single-customer issue. Ticket.

Don't over-classify. Sev 1 every week → no one takes it seriously.

## Postmortems

After every Sev-1 (and any Sev-2 worth learning from):

### Blameless

- Focus on **systems**, not people. "The deploy pipeline let bad
  code through" not "Alice deployed bad code."
- Assume everyone acted reasonably with the information they had.
- Surfacing problems without blame produces better data than
  finger-pointing.

### Structure

1. **Summary**: 2-3 sentences. What happened, impact, duration.
2. **Timeline**: minute-by-minute, who did what.
3. **Root cause**: what failed, why.
4. **Contributing factors**: why didn't earlier defenses catch it?
5. **What went well**: the team's wins. Important for morale.
6. **What went wrong**: gaps to fix.
7. **Action items**: specific, owned, dated. Track to completion.

### Action items

The output of every postmortem. Common types:

- **Detection**: monitoring gap that delayed response.
- **Mitigation**: faster way to stop the bleeding.
- **Prevention**: stop the class of bug from recurring.

Track action-item completion. Postmortems with no follow-through
are theater.

## Game days / chaos engineering

Production breaks. The question is when, not if. Game days
practice:

- Kill a random pod / VM. Does the app recover?
- Cut network to a region. Does failover work?
- Spike CPU on a service. Do downstream consumers handle gracefully?

Chaos engineering tools: **Litmus**, **Chaos Mesh**, **Gremlin**.
Run weekly or monthly in staging; quarterly in production.

The goal: discover failure modes during business hours, not at 2am
during a real incident.

## Capacity planning

Predict when you'll need more resources before you hit a wall.

- **Track growth**: requests/sec over time, by service.
- **Project**: linear / exponential / seasonal forecast.
- **Provision ahead**: scale infra before the cliff.

For autoscaled services, capacity planning is "what does the
autoscaler ceiling need to be?" For fixed infra (DB, cache), it's
"when do we upgrade?"

Quarterly capacity reviews catch most issues. Black-swan events
(viral mention, conference traffic spike) need separate disaster-
preparedness.

## Reliability vs velocity

The eternal tension. The SRE answer: **error budgets resolve it
quantitatively**.

- Below SLO (burning budget): freeze risky changes. Focus on
  reliability work.
- At SLO (steady state): normal feature velocity.
- Above SLO (banking budget): take risks. Try new things.

This removes the "should we ship vs should we be careful?" debate
— the data answers.

## Cost discipline

Reliability has cost. At staff level:

- 99.9% costs $X / month.
- 99.95% costs $5X.
- 99.99% costs $50X.

Each nine is significantly more expensive. Reliability beyond user
need is waste.

Be honest with stakeholders: "We can ship more features OR be more
reliable; pick where to invest." Hidden trade-offs are how teams
end up either fragile or slow.

## Common pitfalls

### Aspirational SLOs no one believes

```
SLO: 99.99% uptime
Reality: we're at 99.5% but write 99.99% in the contract
```

You'll burn the budget continuously, alerts will be meaningless,
the SLO becomes shelf-ware. Set targets you can actually meet.

### Counting noise as outage

```
Synthetic monitor flakes, fires an "outage" alert
```

If your "outage" includes flaky tests and monitor glitches, your
SLI is wrong. Filter noise; measure user-visible signals.

### Postmortems without action items

The team has an interesting conversation, writes a document, files
it. Six months later, the same incident happens. Action items
must be tracked.

### On-call alone

One person paged at 3am, no one to escalate. Burnout, single point
of failure for institutional knowledge. Always a secondary.

### Reliability theater

Adding redundant systems without testing failure. "We have a
backup database in another region!" Has anyone tested failover?
Untested redundancy is fictional.

### Blaming individuals in postmortems

```
Root cause: Alice deployed a bug.
```

Wrong. The root cause is "our deploy pipeline allowed the bug
through." Focus on systems. People work within them.

## The mental model

SRE is **applying engineering to operations**. Define what reliable
means (SLO), measure it (SLI), spend the budget on changes (error
budget), and respond systematically when things break (on-call,
incident management, postmortems).

For 2026 production:

- **Pick SLOs** for your top 5 user-visible services. Start
  conservative; tighten over time.
- **Multi-window burn rate alerts**. Stop paging on raw
  thresholds.
- **Bound toil** at < 50% of an engineer's time.
- **On-call** with primary + secondary, weekly rotation, fair
  compensation.
- **Blameless postmortems** with tracked action items for every
  Sev 1.
- **Quarterly chaos drills**.
- **Capacity planning** quarterly.

You don't need a separate SRE team to do this. Most modern dev
teams do SRE-style work alongside features. The discipline is
treating reliability as an engineering deliverable, not an
afterthought.
