# Consensus: Raft and Paxos

Consensus is how a group of unreliable machines agree on a single
value — the next log entry, the current leader, the latest config —
in the face of network drops, crashes, and slow replicas. Every
modern coordination system you've heard of (etcd, Consul, CockroachDB,
TiKV, Kafka KRaft, FoundationDB's coordinators) runs Raft or a Paxos
variant under the hood.

You almost never implement consensus yourself. You do need to know
what it gives you, what it doesn't, and where its sharp edges are
when you build on top.

## What problem consensus actually solves

A group of N nodes wants to agree on a sequence of values such that:

1. **Agreement**: no two non-faulty nodes ever decide on different
   values for the same slot.
2. **Validity**: the agreed value was proposed by some node.
3. **Termination**: as long as a majority can talk to each other,
   the group eventually decides.

That's it. Consensus alone doesn't give you a database, a queue, or
a lock service. It gives you a **replicated log** that all replicas
agree on, in the same order. Everything else (state machines,
linearizable reads, leases) is built on top.

The FLP impossibility result says no deterministic consensus
algorithm can guarantee termination in an asynchronous network with
even one crash failure. Real systems sidestep this with timeouts and
randomized backoff — they're correct (agreement + validity) always
and live (termination) usually.

## Raft, in one page

Raft was designed (Diego Ongaro, 2014) to be understandable, after
years of teams getting Paxos wrong. It decomposes consensus into
three independent sub-problems: **leader election**, **log
replication**, **safety**.

### Three roles

At any moment each node is one of:

- **Follower** — passive. Accepts AppendEntries from the leader,
  votes when asked.
- **Candidate** — running for election after a timeout.
- **Leader** — exactly one per term. Accepts client writes, replicates
  log entries to followers.

### Terms and elections

Time is divided into **terms** (monotonically increasing integers).
Each term has at most one leader.

```
Follower → (election timeout, no heartbeat) → Candidate
Candidate → (votes from majority) → Leader
Candidate → (heard from leader with higher term) → Follower
Leader → (sees higher term in any RPC) → Follower
```

A follower that hears no heartbeat for ~150–300ms increments its
term, votes for itself, and asks the rest for votes. Random timeouts
prevent split votes from looping forever. A node grants its vote
only if the candidate's log is **at least as up-to-date** as its own
(safety property — see below).

### Log replication

The leader appends client commands to its log and sends
**AppendEntries** RPCs to followers. An entry is **committed** once a
majority (including the leader) has it durably. The leader then
applies it to its state machine and tells followers to do the same
on the next heartbeat.

```
client → leader: SET x=5
leader: append to log @ index 7, term 4
leader → followers: AppendEntries(prevIdx=6, prevTerm=4, entries=[(7, "SET x=5")])
followers: append, ack
leader (majority acked): commit index = 7, apply to SM, reply OK to client
```

### The safety invariant

If a log entry is committed in term T, it must be present in every
future leader's log. Raft enforces this by:

- Only granting votes to candidates whose log is at least as
  up-to-date (longer log or same length with same final term).
- A new leader never overwrites or deletes log entries — it only
  appends. It brings followers in line by overwriting **their**
  divergent tail.

This is what makes Raft "safe under all conditions, live when a
majority can communicate."

## What's the same in Paxos

Paxos (Leslie Lamport, 1998) solves the same problem and predates
Raft by 15+ years. Multi-Paxos (the practical variant — Basic Paxos
is just one slot) and Raft are isomorphic in what they guarantee:

- Both require a **majority quorum** for both proposes and decides
  (a 5-node cluster tolerates 2 failures).
- Both use a **monotonic term/ballot number** to detect stale
  leaders.
- Both elect a **stable leader** in steady state to batch decisions
  efficiently (Multi-Paxos's "distinguished proposer").
- Both achieve the same FLP-bound liveness — eventually decide if a
  majority can communicate.

What's different is presentation. Paxos describes two phases
(`Prepare`/`Promise`, `Accept`/`Accepted`) that are powerful but
notoriously hard to reason about correctly. Raft fuses leader
election with log catch-up and bans gaps in the log — you lose some
generality, you gain the ability to ship a correct implementation in
a quarter rather than a decade.

If you see "Paxos" in a 2026 codebase it's almost certainly
Multi-Paxos with a stable leader, or a derivative like **EPaxos**
(leaderless, commutative-command optimization) or **Flexible Paxos**
(decouples leader-election quorum from commit quorum).

## What Raft does NOT give you

This is where people get hurt building on Raft.

### Linearizable reads, by default

A follower may have committed entries the leader hasn't told it
about yet, or may be lagging entries the leader has committed.
Reading from a follower is not linearizable. **Even reading from
the leader is not linearizable** unless you do one of:

- **Read index**: leader confirms it's still leader (heartbeat to a
  quorum) before serving the read. Cheap, one round-trip.
- **Leader lease**: leader holds a time-bounded lease (granted by
  followers); within the lease it can serve reads without
  contacting the quorum. Faster, requires clock-skew bounds.
- **Quorum read**: read from a majority, take the entry with the
  highest term/index. Most expensive, rarely used.

etcd, CockroachDB, and TiKV all do leader-lease reads by default.
If your "Raft store" library doesn't, you can stale-read after a
network blip even when nothing seems wrong.

### Performance under partition

If the leader is partitioned from a follower minority, those
followers can't make progress and the partitioned minority can't
elect a new leader (no majority). Writes on the majority side keep
going. Clients on the wrong side of the partition see timeouts
until topology heals or they're routed away.

This is correct CP behavior (see **cap-and-pacelc.mdx**) but it
means a Raft cluster has a hard floor on availability — minority
partitions are unavailable for writes, period.

### Cross-shard transactions

A Raft group agrees on one log. If your data is sharded into many
Raft groups (CockroachDB style), cross-shard atomicity is a separate
layer — typically 2PC over per-shard Raft commits. Raft doesn't
solve distributed transactions; it gives you a building block.

### Anything in fewer than 3 nodes

Consensus needs a majority. A 2-node cluster tolerates zero
failures (majority is 2, so any failure stalls). A 1-node "cluster"
is just a single point of failure with extra steps. Run 3 or 5 — 5
if you can afford it (tolerates 2 failures and one node can be down
for maintenance without losing fault tolerance).

## Where you actually meet Raft

Every modern coordination system uses it:

- **etcd** — Kubernetes' config store. Raft over gRPC. Watch
  primitive built on top.
- **Consul** — HashiCorp's service discovery + KV. Raft for the
  server pool.
- **CockroachDB**, **TiKV / TiDB**, **YugabyteDB** — per-range
  Raft groups, sharded.
- **Kafka (KRaft mode)** — replaced ZooKeeper in Kafka 4.0 (March
  2025). The controller quorum is Raft.
- **FoundationDB** — uses a custom variant for the coordinator role.
- **MongoDB replica set elections** — Raft-derived since 3.2.

ZooKeeper uses **ZAB**, not Raft — a leader-based total-order
broadcast protocol that's essentially a different presentation of
the same idea.

## Building on Raft, practically

When you reach for Raft, you almost always reach for **etcd** rather
than implementing it. etcd gives you:

```js
// node-etcd or etcd3 client
import { Etcd3 } from 'etcd3';
const client = new Etcd3({ hosts: ['etcd-0:2379', 'etcd-1:2379', 'etcd-2:2379'] });

// Atomic compare-and-swap via transaction
await client.if('config/version', 'Value', '==', '7')
            .then(client.put('config/version').value('8'))
            .else(client.put('config/conflict').value('true'))
            .commit();

// Leader election via lease + key
const lease = client.lease(10);   // 10s TTL
await lease.put('leader/myservice').value('node-A');
lease.on('lost', () => { /* I am no longer leader */ });
```

This is the right level of abstraction. You get a linearizable KV
store, leases, watches, and CAS — all backed by Raft, all somebody
else's problem to operate.

<Callout type="info">
For "is there exactly one of me running" use cases (leader-only
cron, singleton consumer), etcd or ZooKeeper leases beat Redis
locks on safety. Redis `SET NX EX` is fast but doesn't survive
split-brain cleanly — see **data/distributed-ids-and-locks.mdx**
for the Redlock debate.
</Callout>

## Common misuses

### Treating a 2-node setup as HA

Two nodes with Raft is strictly worse than one node. Any single
failure halts the cluster (no majority). Use 3 or 5.

### Sharing a single Raft group across all data

A Raft group has throughput ceilings (~10–50k commits/sec depending
on hardware and batching). For database-scale workloads, shard the
data into many small Raft groups (the CockroachDB / TiKV model).

### Reading from a "Raft replica" expecting freshness

Replicas can lag. Either route reads to the leader (with read index
or lease) or accept eventually-consistent semantics. Don't assume
"it's Raft, it's consistent" without checking how reads are served.

### Using consensus for high-frequency data

A 5-node Raft group with cross-AZ replication has a write latency
floor of ~5–20ms per commit. That's fine for config and metadata,
ruinous for hot-path application writes. Per-write consensus belongs
to the coordination plane, not the data plane.

### Long GC pauses breaking leader leases

A leader whose process pauses (full GC, swap, fork) past its lease
expiry can serve reads after a new leader has been elected — classic
split-brain. Tune leases longer than worst-case pause, or use
fencing tokens at the next layer down.

## The mental model

Consensus produces a **replicated log that a majority agrees on, in
order**. That log drives a state machine, and the state machine is
your KV store, your config, your leader election, your lock service.

For 2026 backend services:

- **Don't implement Raft.** Use etcd, Consul, or the consensus layer
  baked into your database.
- **Use it for coordination, not data.** Leader election, config,
  service discovery, distributed locks — not your hot-path writes.
- **Run odd-sized clusters of 3 or 5.** Tolerates 1 or 2 failures
  respectively.
- **Understand how reads are served.** Linearizable reads need a
  read-index, lease, or quorum read — they are not free.
- **Trust the algorithm; mistrust the implementation.** Most
  consensus bugs in the wild are in the surrounding code (snapshot
  handling, membership changes, log compaction), not in the core
  protocol.

Consensus is one of the few areas where the academic theory and the
production reality are tightly aligned — and one of the few where
rolling your own is almost always wrong.
