# Flexible Paxos and EPaxos

`consensus-algorithms-overview` and `paxos` both name-drop a
handful of variants — Flexible Paxos, EPaxos, Generalized
Paxos — and then move on. This page is where they live in full.

The question this page tries to answer honestly is: **when do
the leaderless or quorum-flexible variants actually pay off, and
why do production systems mostly still pick Raft anyway?**

The short version: the variants are mathematically real wins
for specific workloads (geo-distributed writes, write-heavy
with rare elections, commutative operations), but the
implementation and operational cost is high enough that the
default answer remains "use Multi-Paxos or Raft per shard, run
many shards." The longer version is below.

## Why anyone bothers with variants

Classic Multi-Paxos and Raft share three properties that look
like constraints once you start running them at scale:

- **A single leader is the write bottleneck.** Every write
  goes through one node. In a geo-distributed deployment that
  is one region paying the WAN cost for every other region's
  writes.
- **Quorum intersection is rigid.** In classic Paxos, both the
  leader-election quorum and the write quorum are majorities,
  because two majorities always intersect. This is sufficient
  but not necessary.
- **Strict total ordering is overkill for commutative work.**
  If two writes operate on disjoint keys, ordering them
  globally is wasted effort. The protocol does it anyway.

Each variant attacks one of these.

## Flexible Paxos

Flexible Paxos (Howard, Malkhi, Spiegelman, 2016) is a small
but powerful observation about the Paxos safety argument:

> Paxos is safe as long as **every phase-1 quorum (Q1)
> intersects every phase-2 quorum (Q2)**. There is no
> additional requirement that Q1 or Q2 individually be
> majorities.

In classic Paxos, both Q1 and Q2 are majorities — which
trivially satisfies the intersection condition. But you can
shrink one at the cost of growing the other:

- For 5 nodes, classic uses **{Q1=3, Q2=3}**.
- Flexible Paxos allows **{Q1=4, Q2=2}** — writes commit with
  only 2 acks, elections need 4.
- Or **{Q1=2, Q2=4}** — writes need 4 acks, elections need 2.
  (Rarely useful, but legal.)

```
Five-node cluster, write path under different (Q1, Q2) settings:

Classic Paxos        : write needs 3/5 acks, election needs 3/5
Flexible {4, 2}      : write needs 2/5 acks, election needs 4/5
Flexible {3, 3}      : equivalent to classic
Flexible {5, 1}      : write needs 1/5 ack (the leader's own),
                       election needs all 5 (impossible if any node is down)
```

### When this helps

In write-heavy workloads where leader elections are rare —
which is the steady-state case for most production
consensus — paying more for the rare election to make every
write faster is a good trade. {4, 2} on a 5-node cluster
shaves a round trip off the median write at the cost of slower
failover during the unusual case of leader loss.

The extreme {5, 1} is occasionally proposed: writes are local
to the leader, but the cluster becomes unavailable for elections
if any node is down. This is rarely useful in practice but is
sometimes used in research configurations.

### Production status

Flexible Paxos has appeared in academic systems and some
experimental NewSQL configurations. As of 2026, it is not the
default in any major open-source consensus library — etcd-raft,
hashicorp/raft, and dragonboat all use standard majority
quorums. WPaxos and some research databases experiment with
flexible quorums in geo-distributed configurations.

The barrier is not correctness. It is that "smaller write
quorum, larger election quorum" requires operators to reason
about the asymmetric failover behavior — a category of
operational complexity that has not paid off enough to
overcome the default.

## EPaxos / Egalitarian Paxos

EPaxos (Moraru, Andersen, Kaminsky, SOSP 2013) is the more
ambitious variant: it removes the leader entirely.

### The core idea

Every replica can **propose its own commands** in its own
instance space. There is no single sequencer. Two commands that
**do not conflict** (the conflict relation is
application-defined — for a KV store, two writes to different
keys do not conflict) commit in **one round trip** with a fast
quorum.

Two commands that **do** conflict (same key, or in general any
pair the application says conflicts) require an extra round to
establish a dependency order between them — they cannot be
committed in arbitrary order.

```
Three regions, A in us-east, B in eu-west, C in asia-east.

EPaxos, uncontended writes (different keys):
  client in eu-west → leader B → 1 RTT, eu-local quorum → commit.
  No WAN round-trip to us-east or asia-east.

EPaxos, contended writes (same key from us-east and eu-west):
  proposers detect conflict, take an extra round to agree on order.
  Two RTTs for the contended writes.

Raft, same scenario:
  every write goes to the elected leader (say in us-east).
  eu-west and asia-east always pay the WAN cost.
```

The win is real for **geo-distributed deployments with
write-locality** — clients write keys that are mostly
geographically local, and conflicts are rare. In that
workload, EPaxos avoids the WAN round-trip that Raft
unconditionally pays.

### Where it falls down

- **Conflict-heavy workloads.** Every conflict costs an extra
  round; if conflict rates approach 100%, EPaxos is **slower**
  than Raft (which paid for one leader-bounce, but only once,
  not per conflict).
- **Implementation complexity.** EPaxos has more state per
  instance, more inter-replica messages, and a recovery
  protocol that is genuinely subtle. Multiple published
  implementations have shipped bugs in the dependency tracking
  or recovery paths.
- **Tail latency is harder to reason about.** A workload with
  occasional bursts of contention sees variable round counts
  per commit. Raft's tail latency is predictable: it is the
  slowest of the quorum acks.

### Production status

EPaxos has not displaced Raft in mainstream production. The
notable attempts:

- **Internal hyperscaler systems** at a few companies (research
  papers reference Microsoft and Facebook experiments).
- **A handful of NewSQL and research databases.**
- **CockroachDB** — explicitly evaluated EPaxos and chose
  per-range Raft instead, citing operational and complexity
  reasons in their public design docs.

The pattern: companies who could implement EPaxos correctly
mostly decided that running many small Raft groups (one per
partition or range) buys them the same horizontal scaling
without the implementation risk. EPaxos is a real win on
paper; Raft-per-shard is a real win in production.

## Generalized Paxos

Generalized Paxos (Lamport, 2005) is the most abstract variant.
It extends Paxos to handle **commutative** operations
natively — two operations that commute (e.g., "increment x by
5" and "increment x by 10") can be committed in any order, and
the protocol does not need to fix one.

Conceptually elegant; production implementations are rare. The
common cited use case is CRDT-shaped state, where commutativity
is structural — but in those workloads the application usually
chooses **eventual consistency** with CRDTs directly and
avoids the consensus protocol entirely.

We mention Generalized Paxos here for completeness; it is more
common in research literature than in deployed systems.

## Why production largely picked Raft anyway

A few honest reasons that go beyond "papers vs reality":

### Implementation complexity is a first-class cost

EPaxos's dependency tracking, conflict detection, and recovery
protocol are easy to get subtly wrong. Bugs in consensus
protocols destroy data; the bar for "good enough
implementation" is high. Raft's specification is short, has a
TLA+ proof, and is implemented correctly in multiple vetted
libraries. That is worth a lot.

### Predictable tail latency

Operators love p99 latency that they can model. Raft commits
in "majority ack" time — slow nodes drag out the tail in a
predictable way. EPaxos commits in "1 round if no conflict, 2
if conflict" — and the conflict rate depends on workload
patterns the operator may not control.

### Library ecosystem

`etcd-raft`, `hashicorp/raft`, `tikv-raft`, `dragonboat` are
all battle-tested. Picking one is a low-risk decision. Picking
EPaxos means either writing your own or vendoring an academic
implementation, both of which are high-risk decisions for any
team that does not have a research-grade distributed-systems
group.

### Sharding gets you most of the way

The EPaxos pitch is "avoid the leader bottleneck." But you can
**shard** — run many independent Raft groups, one per partition.
CockroachDB has tens of thousands of Raft groups per cluster;
TiKV does the same. The single-leader cost is bounded by what
one Raft group is asked to do, and one group handles a small
slice of the workload.

Spanner uses Paxos per shard for similar reasons: shard first,
then consensus per shard. The leader bottleneck disappears at
the shard granularity.

This is the structural reason EPaxos has not won. The problem
EPaxos solves at the protocol layer was already solved at the
**architecture** layer by sharding.

## When the variants do pay off

There are workloads where the variants genuinely beat Raft:

<Cheatsheet>
| Workload | Best variant | Why |
|---|---|---|
| High write rate, rare elections, can accept slow failover | Flexible Paxos {N-1, 2} | Smaller Q2 = faster writes; rare elections absorb the larger Q1 cost |
| Geo-distributed, write-local, rare key conflicts | EPaxos | Avoids WAN round-trip to a single leader for local writes |
| Commutative operations (counters, CRDT-like) | Generalized Paxos (or just use CRDTs) | Native commutativity removes ordering overhead |
| Anything else | Multi-Paxos or Raft per shard | Predictable, vetted, well-tooled |
</Cheatsheet>

## A pragmatic recommendation

Pick Raft (or Multi-Paxos) with a vetted library. Shard until
single-leader throughput is no longer a problem. Only reach for
the variants if you have **measured** the bottleneck the
variant addresses and the savings exceed the cost of running an
un-vetted protocol in production.

<Callout variant="warning">
Reading the EPaxos paper convinces most engineers that one
WAN round trip is worth saving. The papers are correct. They
are also written by people who own the codebase and the test
infrastructure. The cost of "we shipped a subtle EPaxos bug
to production" is high enough that almost every major
distributed database has weighed that math and chosen Raft
anyway.
</Callout>

The interesting consequence is that the variants live mostly
in the literature. They are worth knowing because they show up
in design conversations, in interview questions, and in
internal proposals at hyperscalers — but the answer to "should
we use one?" in 99% of cases is "use Raft, shard more."

## In summary

- **Flexible Paxos**: relaxes the requirement that both Q1
  (election) and Q2 (write) be majorities — only that they
  intersect. Lets you trade slower elections for faster
  writes, or vice versa. Used in research; rarely the default.
- **EPaxos**: removes the leader entirely. Any replica can
  propose; non-conflicting commands commit in one round,
  conflicting commands in two. Wins for geo-distributed
  workloads with write-locality and rare conflicts. Loses on
  implementation complexity, tail-latency predictability, and
  library maturity.
- **Generalized Paxos**: handles commutative operations
  natively. Conceptually elegant, rarely implemented;
  commutative workloads usually pick CRDTs instead.
- **Production reality**: Raft per shard, with sharding doing
  the horizontal-scaling work the variants promised. Pick a
  variant only if you have measured the bottleneck it
  addresses and can absorb the implementation cost.
