# CAP theorem

The CAP theorem is the most quoted and least correctly cited
result in distributed systems. "Pick two of three" is a
slogan that fits on a slide and misleads in 90% of real
arguments. The actual statement is narrower, more useful, and
worth getting right.

## What CAP actually says

Eric Brewer's conjecture (2000), formalized by Gilbert and
Lynch (2002):

> In an asynchronous network model, a distributed data store
> **cannot** simultaneously guarantee:
>
> - **Consistency** (linearizability: every read sees the
>   most recent write),
> - **Availability** (every request to a non-failing node
>   eventually receives a non-error response), and
> - **Partition tolerance** (the system continues to operate
>   despite arbitrary message loss between nodes).

The careful version of "pick two" is **during a network
partition, you must choose between C and A** — because P is
not a choice, it's a fact of life.

You cannot build a distributed system that "doesn't tolerate
partitions." Networks partition. The question is what your
system does when they do.

## The three properties, precisely

### Consistency (C in CAP)

Specifically **linearizability**: there exists a single
total order of operations consistent with real time, such that
every read returns the value of the most recent write in that
order.

This is **not** "eventual consistency," "ACID consistency"
(the C in ACID is about invariants), or "transactional
consistency." CAP-C is linearizability — the strongest
single-object consistency guarantee.

### Availability (A in CAP)

Every request to a non-failing node receives a non-error
response in finite time. Crucially: **every** non-failed node,
not "the system as a whole."

A system that returns errors from minority partitions during a
split is **not available** by CAP's definition, even though
the majority side is fine.

### Partition tolerance (P in CAP)

The system makes progress even when arbitrary subsets of
messages between nodes are lost.

In a single-node system, P is trivially "yes" — there are no
inter-node messages to lose. CAP only matters for systems
spanning multiple nodes that need to coordinate.

## What CAP actually proves

The proof is short and worth knowing.

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Suppose a distributed store guarantees C and A and P.">
Consider two nodes N1 and N2, partitioned from each other.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="A client writes value v to N1.">
By A, N1 must accept and acknowledge the write.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Another client reads from N2.">
By A, N2 must respond. By C, N2 must return v (the most recent
write). But N1 and N2 can't communicate — N2 has no way to
know about v.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Contradiction.">
At least one of C or A must be sacrificed during the partition.
</Step>
</Steps>

That's it. It's a result about **what happens during a
partition**, not a statement about steady-state behavior.

## Why "pick two" misleads

Three reasons the slogan breaks down.

### 1. P is not optional

In any real distributed system, partitions happen. They are not
something you can refuse to tolerate. "CA" — consistency +
availability without partition tolerance — describes a
single-node system or a synchronous, never-failing network.
Neither exists at scale.

The real choice is **CP** (during partitions, prefer
consistency, sacrifice availability on minority sides) or **AP**
(during partitions, prefer availability, accept temporary
inconsistency).

### 2. C and A are not binary

Real systems offer a spectrum:

- Linearizable per key with monotonic guarantees globally.
- Per-shard linearizable; eventually consistent across shards.
- Bounded staleness ("at most 5 seconds stale").
- Read-your-writes within a session.
- Eventually consistent (no guarantee).

Similarly availability has shades — "majority partition stays
up" vs "any single node stays up."

### 3. CAP says nothing about latency

In the absence of partitions, you can have both C and A. But
even then, there's a latency cost to maintaining strong
consistency (consensus round trips). Many real systems make a
**latency vs consistency** trade with no partition involved.
That's what PACELC extends.

## Where systems actually sit

<Cheatsheet>
| System | Partition behavior |
|---|---|
| Single-leader Postgres / MySQL | CP — leader on the wrong side of a partition has no quorum; writes stop |
| MongoDB replica set (majority readConcern) | CP — minority partitions can't accept writes |
| ZooKeeper, etcd, Consul | CP — quorum required; minority unavailable |
| Spanner, CockroachDB | CP — Paxos/Raft per range; quorum required |
| Cassandra (default tunable) | AP — sloppy quorum, hinted handoff, eventual |
| DynamoDB (default consistency) | AP — eventual reads; strong reads cost more |
| Riak | AP — siblings on conflict; merge in app |
| Redis (no Sentinel/Cluster) | CA-ish single node |
| Redis Sentinel / Cluster | AP-leaning; split-brain risk if mistuned |
| Kafka (with min.insync.replicas) | CP-leaning; minority partition can't write |
</Cheatsheet>

This classification is rough. Cassandra **can** be configured
for strong consistency per write (R + W > N + LWT for
serializable). Postgres **can** be deployed in ways that drop
data on failover.

The right framing: **what does the system do during a
partition?** Drop writes on minority sides (CP) or accept
writes and reconcile later (AP)?

## CP systems

Behavior under partition:

- Minority partitions reject writes (and often reads).
- Majority partition continues, possibly with reduced
  redundancy.
- After the partition heals, minority catches up from the
  majority's log.

Pros:

- **Linearizable** reads and writes for majority clients.
- **No conflict resolution** needed — only one history.
- **Predictable failure semantics** for developers.

Cons:

- **Minority unavailability** is potentially significant —
  if 2 of 5 nodes are partitioned off, 2 nodes are
  unavailable to clients in that partition.
- **Quorum requires odd cluster sizes** (3, 5, 7) to be
  efficient.

Use when correctness dominates: balance ledgers, inventory,
booking systems, configuration stores.

## AP systems

Behavior under partition:

- Every node continues to accept reads and writes.
- After partition heals, divergent versions are reconciled
  (last-write-wins, app merge, CRDTs, sibling resolution).

Pros:

- **Always available.** No matter which side of the partition
  you're on, you get a response.
- **Suitable for global / multi-region** with relaxed
  consistency.

Cons:

- **Conflicts are unavoidable** and must be handled.
- **Reads can return stale data** — sometimes very stale.
- **Reasoning about correctness** is harder; bugs are subtle.

Use when availability is paramount and stale data is
tolerable: shopping cart, social feeds, presence indicators,
metrics, IoT.

## What CAP doesn't say

A list of CAP misuses worth recognizing:

- **"NoSQL is AP, SQL is CP."** Some NoSQL is CP (HBase,
  MongoDB with majority); some SQL is AP-leaning (relaxed
  Postgres failover). The label doesn't determine the
  classification.
- **"CAP forces you to give up one of {C, A, P}."** During
  normal operation (no partition), you can have both. CAP
  only constrains behavior during partitions.
- **"Linearizability is the only consistency."** CAP-C is
  linearizability, but useful systems offer many other
  consistency models (causal, monotonic, bounded staleness).
  Most apps need something weaker than linearizability.
- **"CAP is a complete framework."** It says nothing about
  partition frequency, latency in non-partitioned operation,
  or operational cost. PACELC fills part of this gap; real
  design considers far more.

## Practical guidance

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Decide per data, not per database.">
A single system may need CP for billing and AP for the
activity feed. Some databases support both modes; many
products mix multiple stores.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="State the partition behavior explicitly.">
"When a network partition happens, writes to the wallet table
fail with 503; writes to the like-counter succeed and
converge." That's an actionable design statement.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Plan for partition recovery.">
After a CP partition, reads catch up via the log. After an AP
partition, you need conflict resolution code. Both are work.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Test partitions.">
Chaos engineering: introduce network drops, verify behavior
matches the design. Most CAP violations are discovered in
production.
</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout variant="info">
Brewer's 2012 retrospective (CAP Twelve Years Later) said the
clearest thing: design systems that handle partitions
**explicitly**. The "two of three" framing was a slogan from a
keynote; the engineering reality is more nuanced and more
useful. See PACELC for the natural follow-up.
</Callout>

## In summary

- CAP says: during a network partition, a distributed store
  must choose between linearizability (C) and full
  availability (A). P is not optional.
- "Pick two" is misleading. The real choice is **CP vs AP
  under partition**.
- Both C and A are spectrums; real systems make per-operation
  or per-key trade-offs.
- CAP is about partition behavior; PACELC extends it to
  non-partition latency.
- Decide CP vs AP per dataset, document the partition
  semantics, and test the partition behavior in chaos
  experiments.
