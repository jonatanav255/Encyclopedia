# Trace sampling strategies

Keeping every trace at any reasonable scale is unaffordable. A
service at 50K QPS producing 10 KB of span data per request is
~500 MB/s of trace storage — petabytes a month. **Sampling** is
the practice of choosing which traces to keep so cost stays
bounded without losing the interesting ones.

The trick is "interesting." Errors and slow tail traces are
worth orders of magnitude more than routine successes — but you
can't always tell ahead of time. Strategies differ in **when**
they decide, and **what** they bias toward.

## The decision: head vs tail

<Compare a="Head sampling" b="Tail sampling">
<>
Decide **at trace start** whether to record this trace. The
decision propagates down the call tree via the `sampled` flag
on `traceparent`.

- **Cheap**: a coin flip per trace; no buffering.
- **Composable**: every service obeys the upstream decision.
- **Blind**: you don't know yet if the trace will error or be
  slow. You may drop the very traces you wanted.
</>
<>
Decide **after the trace completes** based on what actually
happened — error status, total duration, presence of specific
attributes.

- **Smart**: keeps all errors, all slow traces.
- **Expensive**: must buffer all spans of a trace until it
  completes, then decide.
- **Stateful**: requires a collector that groups spans by trace
  ID and waits for the whole trace.
</>
</Compare>

In practice, head sampling is the **floor** (kept simple, scales
linearly), tail sampling is the **ceiling** (smart but stateful
and resource-hungry). Many systems use both: head sampling
with a low rate for routine traffic plus tail sampling that
keeps anything interesting that head sampling missed.

## Head sampling shapes

The simplest is **probabilistic**: keep p% of traces.

```ts
const sampler = new TraceIdRatioBasedSampler(0.01); // 1%
```

The hash of the trace ID is consistent across services, so all
spans of one trace agree on the sample decision. No drift, no
gaps.

Other head shapes:

- **Per-route rate**: keep 100% of `/checkout`, 1% of
  `/healthcheck`. Encode as multiple samplers chained.
- **Per-tenant rate**: keep 100% for free tier (low volume, full
  detail) and 0.1% for enterprise (high volume).
- **Always-on for tagged**: a debug header forces sampling for
  developer-initiated requests.
- **Rate-limited**: keep up to N traces/sec, drop the rest.
  Bounds cost regardless of upstream rate.

The composition rule: the **most permissive** sampler that
matches wins. A debug header should keep the trace even if the
default sampler rolled "drop."

## Tail sampling shapes

The collector groups spans by trace ID, waits N seconds for
late-arriving spans, then runs **decision policies** in
priority order:

<Cheatsheet>
| Policy | Keeps |
|---|---|
| Status | Traces with any span in `ERROR` status |
| Latency | Traces with total duration > threshold |
| Attribute | Traces with a specific attribute value (e.g. `customer.tier = enterprise`) |
| Rare endpoint | Traces hitting low-traffic routes (helps with diversity) |
| Probabilistic | Random p% of the remainder |

</Cheatsheet>

The first matching policy keeps the trace; if none match, it's
dropped (or sent at a low base rate). The result is a curated
sample: every error, every slow request, plus diversity from
random sampling.

The cost is real. A tail-sampling collector must:

- Buffer all spans of in-flight traces (memory).
- Wait long enough for late spans to arrive (latency).
- Cope with traces that never complete (eviction policy).
- Decide before storage backpressure piles up (throughput).

OpenTelemetry's `tail_sampling` processor implements this; Refinery
(by Honeycomb) is a popular standalone tail sampler.

## Dynamic and adaptive

The frontier. Sampling rate **changes based on observed
behavior**:

- **Adaptive rate**: target a fixed number of traces/sec
  per service. Sampling rate floats up at low traffic and down
  at high.
- **Error-biased**: keep error traces at a higher rate than
  successes regardless of overall load.
- **Cardinality-aware**: sample more from rare combinations
  (uncommon routes, rare error codes) and less from common ones.
- **Reservoir sampling**: maintain a fixed-size reservoir per
  category; replace older traces probabilistically.

Honeycomb's "Refinery" supports dynamic rates per-key (e.g.,
sample customer A at 1%, customer B at 100% during an
incident). The cost is the operator overhead of tuning the
policy.

## What "sampled" propagates as

The W3C traceparent flag bit signals the decision:

```
00-7c3b...-4f8a...-01
                     ^^
                     01 = sampled; 00 = not sampled
```

`01` means "downstream should record." `00` means "downstream
may skip recording entirely." The flag is **propagated**, not
re-decided. This consistency is what makes head sampling work
across services.

Tail sampling complicates this: every span must be **emitted to
the collector** even if it might be dropped later, because the
collector decides per-trace. So tail sampling doesn't save
agent-to-collector bandwidth; it saves backend storage and
indexing.

## Cost arithmetic

A rough back-of-envelope:

```
QPS = 50,000
Average spans/trace = 12
Average span size = 1 KB
Raw trace data = 50,000 × 12 × 1 KB ≈ 600 MB/s = 1.5 PB/month

At 1% head sampling      → 15 TB/month
At 10% tail sampling     → 150 TB/month
At "errors + 1% rest"    → ~30 TB/month (depends on error rate)
```

Storage at $0.10/GB-month — pick a backend's number — gives:

- 1% head: $1.5K/month
- 10% tail: $15K/month
- "errors + 1%": $3K/month with much higher signal

The "errors + 1%" curve is why almost everyone runs tail
sampling once they exceed a few thousand QPS.

## Caveats and pitfalls

- **Aggregates derived from sampled traces lie**. A "rate of
  errors per second" computed by counting error-tagged spans is
  inflated when error traces are oversampled. Reconstructing
  aggregates from samples requires knowing the sampling rate
  per category — and even then, it's noisy.
- **Sampling decisions made by a downstream lose context**. If
  service A says "not sampled" and service B decides anyway,
  the trace is partial. Either everyone respects the inbound
  decision or no one does.
- **Tail sampling collectors are stateful**. They need memory,
  good eviction, and clear timeout policies. Running them at the
  edge of every service is expensive; centralizing them adds
  network hops.
- **Adaptive samplers can hide signal**. A bug that suddenly
  doubles error traffic appears as fewer error traces because
  the adaptive sampler "rebalanced." Always have a separate
  error-rate metric.

<Callout variant="warning">
**Always use metrics for rates and errors, not span counts.**
Metrics are pre-aggregated and full-fidelity. Spans are sampled
and lie about volume.
</Callout>

## A practical default

For most teams the right starting policy is:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Head sample at the edge.">
1–5% probabilistic. Cheap, predictable cost. Bounds total
trace volume.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Tail sample in the collector.">
Keep 100% of error traces, 100% of traces over latency budget,
plus a small probabilistic floor of successful traces.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Always-on for debug.">
A header (`x-debug-trace: 1`) forces sampling, regardless of
either layer. Developers and on-call use this during
investigation.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Per-customer overrides during incidents.">
The collector supports rules like "sample tenant X at 100% for
the next hour." Use during active investigation.
</Step>
</Steps>

This composition keeps cost predictable, never drops errors,
and gives operators a knob for the unusual case.

## In summary

- You cannot keep 100% of traces past a few thousand QPS.
- Head sampling: cheap and consistent; biased toward routine.
- Tail sampling: smart and stateful; keeps errors and slow
  traces.
- Most production systems run both — head for cost ceiling, tail
  for signal.
- The `traceparent` sampled flag carries the head decision; tail
  needs span buffering in the collector.
- Aggregates derived from sampled spans lie. Always use
  metrics for rates.
- Provide always-on debug overrides for developers and
  incidents.
