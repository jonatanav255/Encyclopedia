# SLI / SLO / SLA

The three-letter vocabulary of reliability. The Google SRE
book is the canonical source; everyone copies the model
because it works.

## Definitions

- **SLI** — Service Level Indicator. A measurement of some
  aspect of service behavior. "p99 latency of POST /orders."
  "Error rate for /search." "Availability of the
  /healthz endpoint."
- **SLO** — Service Level Objective. A target value for an
  SLI. "p99 latency under 200 ms over a 30-day window."
  "99.9% of requests return non-5xx."
- **SLA** — Service Level Agreement. A contract with
  consequences. "If we drop below 99.5%, customers get a 10%
  refund."

The relationship: SLI is what you measure. SLO is what you
aim for internally. SLA is what you sign with customers.

SLA ⊆ SLO ⊆ what you actually achieve. The SLA is the
worst public promise; the SLO is the internal stricter goal;
real performance is hopefully better than both.

## Picking good SLIs

A good SLI:

- **Measures what users experience.** Not internal CPU; the
  latency they actually see.
- **Is a ratio.** "Successful requests / total requests."
  Easy to reason about; easy to alert on.
- **Has clear numerator and denominator.** What counts as
  success? What's in scope?

Typical SLIs:

<Cheatsheet>
| SLI | Numerator | Denominator |
|---|---|---|
| Availability | Requests not 5xx | All requests |
| Latency | Requests faster than threshold T | All requests |
| Quality | Requests with valid response shape | All successful responses |
| Freshness | Reads of data younger than X | All reads |
| Durability | Bytes successfully readable | Bytes written |
| Correctness | Operations producing correct result | All operations |
</Cheatsheet>

## Picking SLO targets

The wrong question: "What's the highest we can promise?"
The right question: "What do users actually need?"

- **Internal services** that only serve other internal
  services: 99.9% (53 min downtime/month) is often enough.
- **Customer-facing web**: 99.95% — 22 min/month is rough but
  achievable.
- **Critical payment paths**: 99.99% — 4 min/month. Real
  engineering investment to hit.
- **Read-mostly endpoints**: tighter latency, looser
  availability than write paths.

Higher numbers cost exponentially more. Going from 99.9% to
99.99% may cost 10× the engineering effort for a benefit
that's invisible to most users.

## The error budget

SLO of 99.9% over 30 days = 43 min of allowed badness. That's
your **error budget**.

- **Within budget**: ship features fast, take risks.
- **Out of budget**: stop launching, focus on reliability.

This aligns product and SRE: product wants to ship; SRE wants
stability. The error budget gives a number both can argue
from. (See error-budgets.)

## Time windows

SLOs need a window:

- **30-day rolling**: classic; the budget refreshes
  gradually.
- **Quarter**: align with planning cycles.
- **Weekly**: faster feedback, more noise.

Shorter windows = more variance; longer = slower to act.
30-day rolling is the standard.

## Multi-tier SLOs

One SLO per service isn't enough; you need per-endpoint or
per-user-journey:

- p99 latency of GET /search: 100ms
- p99 latency of POST /orders: 500ms (more complex)
- p99 latency of GET /admin/reports: 5s (low traffic)

The naive "global p99" hides bad endpoints behind good ones.

## SLAs as a forcing function

If you sign a 99.9% SLA, you'd better have a 99.95% internal
SLO. The buffer absorbs noise; it gives you margin to fix
issues before users see them.

The SLA structure typically:

```
If monthly availability falls below 99.9%, customer receives
service credits per the schedule:
  99.0% to 99.9% → 10% credit
  95.0% to 99.0% → 25% credit
  Below 95.0% → 50% credit
```

The exact percentages and credit amounts vary; the structure
is consistent across cloud providers.

## What's not an SLO

### "CPU utilization under 80%"

Not an SLO. CPU is an internal signal; users don't experience
CPU. The SLO is the user-visible outcome (latency, errors).

### "100% availability"

Impossible. Always allow some error budget. 100% targets
either lie about events or force a "stop launching everything"
posture.

### "Mean latency"

Means hide tails. p50 might be perfect while p99 is
catastrophic. Always SLO percentiles, never means.

## Alerting on SLOs

The temptation: alert immediately on any SLO breach. The
problem: noise.

Better pattern: **burn rate** alerting. If you're burning your
30-day budget in 1 hour, page. If you're burning it in 24
hours, ticket. If you're burning it gradually, just
dashboard.

(See alerting-design.)

## Reviewing and adjusting

SLOs aren't permanent. Quarterly review:

- Did we hit the SLO?
- If yes consistently, can we tighten (and free up engineering
  capacity for features)?
- If no, do we need to invest in reliability or adjust the
  SLO?

SLOs are a tool for prioritization, not a sacred contract.

## In summary

- SLI: what you measure (latency, availability, freshness).
- SLO: internal target for the SLI.
- SLA: external promise to customers, with consequences.
- Good SLIs are user-experienced ratios.
- SLO targets reflect user needs, not maximum-possible.
- Error budget = 1 − SLO; aligns product and reliability.
- Alert on burn rate, not on every breach.
- Quarterly review of SLOs; they're tools, not commitments.
