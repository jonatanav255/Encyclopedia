# Queueing theory and Little's Law

There is one piece of math every backend engineer should
internalize, and it explains more production incidents than any
other single tool: as utilization approaches 100%, response time
approaches infinity. Not gradually — explosively. A service
sitting at 90% CPU has roughly **10×** the queueing delay of one
sitting at 50%. At 99%, it has **100×**. The wall is not a
metaphor; it falls out of the algebra.

Two results carry most of the weight. **Little's Law** tells you
the steady-state relationship between concurrency, arrival rate,
and latency. The **M/M/1 response curve** tells you how that
relationship behaves as a queue fills up. Together they explain
why "we just need to push utilization a bit higher to save
money" is the single most expensive sentence in capacity
planning.

## Little's Law

In any system in steady state:

```
L = λ · W

L = average number of items in the system
λ = average arrival rate (items per unit time)
W = average time each item spends in the system
```

This holds for **any** queueing discipline (FIFO, priority,
round-robin), for **any** arrival distribution (Poisson, bursty,
heavy-tailed), and for **any** service-time distribution. There
is no fine print. If you measure two of the three, the third is
fixed.

### Worked examples

**Example 1**: A service handles 1,000 req/sec with 50 ms
average end-to-end latency. Average concurrency:

```
L = 1000 · 0.050 = 50 in-flight requests
```

At any instant, 50 requests are being processed somewhere. If
your monitoring shows 200 in-flight, either λ is higher than
1000 or W is higher than 50 ms — and it's almost always W,
because λ is set by clients and is what it is.

**Example 2**: Your service uses a 32-thread pool, each thread
blocking on I/O for 50 ms per request. Maximum sustainable λ:

```
L_max = 32  (threads)
W     = 0.050 s
λ_max = 32 / 0.050 = 640 req/sec
```

Push past 640 req/sec and requests **queue** in front of the
thread pool. The queue adds waiting time, W rises, but L can't
exceed 32 active — so the queue grows without bound until
something gives (timeouts, OOM, load shedding).

**Example 3**: A database connection pool of size 20 serves
queries averaging 10 ms. The pool tops out at:

```
λ_max = 20 / 0.010 = 2000 queries/sec
```

If application QPS exceeds 2000, queries queue on connection
acquisition, not on the database itself. The database CPU graph
looks fine; the latency graph looks awful. This is the most
common shape of "the database is the bottleneck" incidents that
aren't actually about the database.

## The utilization wall — M/M/1

Little's Law is universal but doesn't model **what happens as
the queue fills**. For that, the textbook starting point is the
M/M/1 queue: Poisson arrivals, exponentially distributed service
times, one server.

For an M/M/1 queue with arrival rate `λ`, service rate `μ`, and
utilization `ρ = λ/μ`:

```
mean response time   W = 1 / (μ − λ) = (1/μ) · 1 / (1 − ρ)
```

That `1 / (1 − ρ)` factor is the **utilization wall**. Multiply
the raw service time by it to get the actual experienced
latency.

<Cheatsheet>
| Utilization ρ | Latency multiplier 1/(1−ρ) |
|---|---|
| 50% | 2× |
| 70% | 3.3× |
| 80% | 5× |
| 90% | 10× |
| 95% | 20× |
| 99% | 100× |
| 99.9% | 1000× |
</Cheatsheet>

Read this table once a week. It explains everything from
"why does our p99 spike during peak hours" to "why is the
autoscaler chasing its tail."

<Callout variant="warning">
The wall is convex. Going from 80% to 90% utilization is
nominally "10% more load" but actually **doubles** mean response
time. Going from 90% to 95% doubles it again. Treating
utilization as linear is the most common capacity-planning
mistake.
</Callout>

## Why p99 is even worse

Mean response time follows `1/(1−ρ)`. **Higher percentiles grow
faster.** In M/M/1, the p99 of waiting time grows roughly as
`ln(100) · 1/(1-ρ)` of the mean — about 4.6× the mean wait at
any given ρ. So:

- At ρ = 50%, p99 wait is small.
- At ρ = 90%, mean response is 10× service time, p99 is
  somewhere around 30–50× service time.
- At ρ = 95%, mean is 20× service, p99 enters the
  hundreds-of-multiples regime.

The intuition: variance accumulates in the queue. A single
unusually long request blocks many short ones behind it, and
the longer the average wait, the more "bad luck" the worst
percentiles inherit. See `tail-latency` for what to do about
this beyond capacity planning.

## M/M/c — more servers, statistical multiplexing

A single 4-CPU server modelled as M/M/c with c=4 is **not** the
same as four 1-CPU servers each modelled as M/M/1. With one
shared queue and c servers, a burst at one server can borrow
idle capacity from the others. The result: M/M/c hits the wall
later than 4 × M/M/1.

In practice this is why:

- **One big pool beats many small pools.** A 100-thread pool
  draining one queue handles burstier load than 10 separate
  10-thread pools.
- **Worker steal queues** (Tokio, Rayon, Go's scheduler) exist —
  they let idle workers grab work from busy ones, recovering
  statistical multiplexing inside the process.
- **Per-tenant isolation has a cost.** Splitting capacity into
  per-customer pools improves fairness but reduces effective
  utilization headroom; you pay for the isolation with more
  total capacity to keep tail latency bounded.

The same math says: **two replicas at 50% utilization each are
not equivalent to one replica at 100% — they're equivalent to a
single pool at 50%, which has 2× the latency of zero load, not
infinity.**

## Practical implications

### 1. Capacity-plan for 60–70% steady state, not 90%

The textbook curve says you should provision so that average
utilization sits around 0.6 to 0.7. Above that, mean latency
swells fast; tail latency swells faster. This isn't waste — the
headroom is what **bounds your tails**.

The temptation to "save 30% by running at 95%" is real and
quantifiable: at 95%, you have ~20× the queueing delay, and any
burst that pushes you to 99% briefly is a multi-minute outage.

### 2. Batching effectively raises μ

If batching halves the per-request service cost (because you
amortize syscalls, network round trips, or per-request fixed
overhead), `μ` doubles. At fixed λ, ρ halves, and `1/(1−ρ)`
collapses. Batching is the most cost-effective latency
intervention when it's applicable, precisely because it shifts
ρ, not because it makes individual items faster. See
`batching-and-coalescing`.

### 3. Backpressure and shed before the queue grows

The wall is so steep that **the queue is the enemy**. Bounded
queues, deadlines that propagate, and explicit load shedding
keep ρ below the cliff. Once the queue grows, you're already in
the regime where serving the queue is making things worse — work
in progress accumulates faster than it drains. See
`backpressure` and `load-shedding`.

### 4. Autoscaling fights the wall — but slowly

Adding capacity reduces ρ, which is the right answer. But
autoscalers have minutes-scale loops, while the wall acts in
seconds. The autoscaler is the long-term remedy; load shedding
is the short-term remedy. See `autoscaling`.

### 5. Fair queueing matters under saturation

When ρ ≈ 1, **who is in the queue** determines who suffers.
Priority and fair-queueing disciplines decide whether one heavy
tenant degrades everyone or only themselves. Under saturation,
queueing discipline is the difference between graceful
degradation and a uniformly broken service.

## A worked capacity-planning calculation

A service has measured service time of 20 ms (μ = 50 req/sec
per worker). The team wants p99 latency under 200 ms at a peak
load of 800 req/sec. How many workers?

```
At each ρ, p99 ≈ ~3-5× the mean in M/M/c (rough rule, c >= 4).

Target: mean response time ≤ 50 ms (so p99 stays ≤ ~200 ms).
mean = (1/μ_total) · 1/(1-ρ) where μ_total = c · 50 req/sec/worker.

Try c = 20: μ_total = 1000, ρ = 800/1000 = 0.8 → wait_factor = 5×
  mean ≈ 20ms · 5 = 100 ms, p99 ≈ 400 ms.  too slow.

Try c = 32: μ_total = 1600, ρ = 800/1600 = 0.5 → wait_factor = 2×
  mean ≈ 40 ms, p99 ≈ ~120-160 ms.  works.

c = 32 workers, sitting at 50% utilization at peak.
```

Note the workers run at 50% even at peak. That is the headroom
the wall demands. Anyone arguing "but we're only using half the
boxes" is asking for a multi-100× p99 spike during the next
burst.

## When the math is approximate

Real workloads aren't M/M/1. Service times are often
heavy-tailed (rare, very slow requests); arrivals are bursty
(synchronized retries, scheduled jobs); requests have priorities
and dependencies. The exact formulas don't hold, but **the
qualitative behavior does**: utilization has a convex wall, and
the wall is steep above ~80%.

For more realistic models, look at M/G/1 (general service-time
distribution; the Pollaczek-Khinchine formula adds a variance
term that makes things worse than M/M/1) and M/D/1 (deterministic
service; lower latency than M/M/1 at the same ρ, since variance
disappears). The pattern is the same in all of them.

## In summary

- **Little's Law**: `L = λ · W`. Holds for any queueing system in
  steady state. Two of the three quantities pin the third.
- **The utilization wall**: in M/M/1, mean response time is
  `service_time / (1 − ρ)`. At ρ = 50% you pay 2×; at 90%, 10×;
  at 99%, 100×.
- **p99 grows faster than mean.** Variance compounds in the
  queue; the higher the percentile, the steeper the wall.
- **M/M/c statistical multiplexing**: one big pool of c servers
  beats c independent single-server pools at the same total
  capacity — the same load reaches the wall later.
- **Capacity-plan for 60–70% steady state**, not 90%. The
  headroom isn't waste; it's what bounds tail latency.
- **Batching** is the cheapest latency intervention because it
  raises μ and collapses ρ; **bounded queues + load shedding**
  prevent the wall from being touched at all; **autoscaling**
  is the slow remedy.
- Pair with `tail-latency`, `backpressure`, `load-shedding`,
  `autoscaling`, and `batching-and-coalescing` for the
  operational toolkit that lives downstream of this math.
