# Service decomposition strategies

You've decided to break a monolith into services. The question is
**where to cut**. The first cut matters more than any later one —
get it wrong and you've built a distributed monolith that costs more
to fix than it cost to build.

Richardson's *Microservices Patterns* and Newman's *Monolith to
Microservices* converge on four strategies. None is universally
right; the trick is knowing which applies to which slice.

## The four strategies

<Cheatsheet>
| Strategy | The cut | When it fits |
|---|---|---|
| By business capability | "What the business does." Order management, billing, fraud, search. | Most teams; aligns with Conway's law and bounded contexts. |
| By subdomain (DDD) | Bounded contexts identified via event storming. | Same as above, with more rigor; the DDD-native version. |
| By transaction (atomic unit) | Keep things in one service if they must change atomically. | When existing transactions reveal natural boundaries. |
| By volatility | Group code by how often it changes / by who changes it. | Stable foundation vs. fast-moving feature areas. |
</Cheatsheet>

In practice you mix all four. Capability is the **starting frame**;
transaction and volatility are **validation checks**; DDD is the
**how**, not a different answer.

## Strategy 1: By business capability

A capability is "something the business does," named in business
language: `BillCustomers`, `RecommendProducts`, `DetectFraud`,
`FulfillOrders`. Capabilities are stable — they describe what the
business is, not how this quarter's code is organized.

**Find them by asking the business**, not the engineers:

- What are the things this company does for customers?
- What are the major operational processes?
- Which teams correspond to which outcomes?

A small e-commerce company's capabilities:

```
- Catalog (what we sell)
- Inventory (what's in stock)
- Pricing (what it costs today)
- Cart (in-flight shopping)
- Checkout (intent to buy)
- Payment (collecting money)
- Fulfillment (getting it shipped)
- Returns (post-sale)
- Customer accounts (who's buying)
- Recommendations (suggesting)
- Fraud (catching abuse)
```

Each is a candidate service. Note these are **not entities** (no
"UserService," "ProductService"). They're verbs and outcomes.

This usually gives you a reasonable first cut: 8–15 services for a
medium business, each one team can own.

## Strategy 2: By subdomain (DDD)

The DDD-native version of capability decomposition. Evans
distinguishes:

- **Core domain**: the part of the business that's the competitive
  edge. Most engineering attention; usually one or two contexts.
- **Supporting subdomain**: necessary but not differentiating;
  off-the-shelf or simple in-house.
- **Generic subdomain**: commodity (auth, payments, email); buy or
  use SaaS.

You decompose by **bounded context** within those subdomains. The
practical method is event storming (see **Service boundaries and
bounded contexts**):

```
1. Map domain events.
2. Group events by which process owns them.
3. Each group is a candidate context.
4. The boundary is where events cross between groups.
```

The result is finer-grained than capability mapping but converges
in practice. A capability like "FulfillOrders" might be one context;
in some businesses it's multiple (pick, pack, ship).

When in doubt: capability for first cut, DDD for refinement.

## Strategy 3: By transaction (atomic unit)

A practical test from Richardson: **what changes together, stays
together**. If two pieces of data must be updated atomically (in one
ACID transaction), splitting them into separate services creates a
saga where you didn't need one.

Look at your monolith's existing transactions. Each transaction is
a clue about a tight coupling that must remain inside one service.

Example: order placement that updates `orders`, `order_items`, and
`inventory_reservations` in one transaction. If these end up in three
different services, you need a saga with compensating actions for
something that's currently atomic. That's a real loss — sagas are
harder to write, debug, and reason about than transactions.

Heuristics:

```
- Same transaction → same service (default).
- Different services → eventual consistency between them (assume).
- If you need atomicity across services, you have a saga (see
  Choreography vs orchestration).
- Avoid sagas where transactions sufficed; only adopt when the cut
  is otherwise correct and the saga is the price.
```

Transactions are a **constraint** on decomposition, not a target.
Some sagas are worth their cost; many aren't.

## Strategy 4: By volatility

Code that changes weekly and code that changes once a year don't
belong in the same service:

<Compare a="High-volatility area" b="Low-volatility area">
<>
- Promotions, A/B experiments, recommendation tuning, marketing
  workflows.
- Touched daily by product/data teams.
- Lots of small deploys, frequent rollbacks.
- Want fast deploys, light review.
</>
<>
- Auth, billing core, ledger, identity.
- Changes carefully, with audit.
- Few deploys, heavy review.
- Want stability, strict tests.
</>
</Compare>

Mixing them in one service forces low-volatility code to inherit the
high-volatility area's deploy pace and risk — or vice versa, forcing
high-velocity teams to wait for billing's release process.

This is *Software Architecture: The Hard Parts* (Ford, Richards) at
its core: **rate of change** is a first-class architectural
property, and aligning services with it pays off.

## Combining strategies

In a real decomposition, you use all four:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Start with capabilities.">
Sketch services along business capability lines. Aim for 1 team per
service; don't worry about smaller cuts yet.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Refine with DDD where ambiguity remains.">
Where capability boundaries are fuzzy, event-storm that area.
Specifically: where the same domain word means different things,
split.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Validate against transactions.">
List the current monolith's transactions. For each, check that the
proposed services don't split it. If a service split forces a saga,
ask: is the saga worth it?
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Validate against volatility.">
Identify weekly-change and yearly-change areas. If they're in the
same proposed service, split or merge accordingly.
</Step>
<Step n={5} title="Validate against team ownership.">
Each service must be ownable by one team. If a proposed service
spans two teams or splits one team's natural surface, redraw.
</Step>
</Steps>

Don't optimize for a small number of services. Optimize for **the
right ones**.

## Sizing services

Newman is direct: "small" is the wrong target. **Right-sized** is the
target. A reasonable service is one that:

- One team can own end-to-end.
- One developer can hold in their head.
- Has a coherent purpose stateable in a sentence.
- Can be redeployed without rolling other services.
- Has its own database (or schema) it solely writes to.

A service can be tens of thousands of lines and still right-sized.
The "nanoservice" anti-pattern — services so small they don't justify
their own deploy pipeline — generates the same operational cost as
real services with none of the benefits.

Rough numbers: most production services are 5K–50K lines. Below
5K, ask whether it should have been part of a bigger one. Above 50K,
ask whether it has multiple internal contexts trying to escape.

## What NOT to decompose by

Common bad bases for decomposition:

### By layer (controller/service/repo)

"AuthController service, AuthService service, AuthRepo service." This
is an in-process layered architecture exploded across the network.
Every call now traverses three network hops. Don't.

### By entity

"User service, Order service, Product service." This is the path to
distributed monolith — every request fans out across them. See
**The distributed monolith**.

### By tech preference

"The Python service, the Go service, the Node service." Tech-aligned
decomposition has no relationship to business capability. Sometimes
language polyglotism is real (data science in Python, services in
Go) — but it's a constraint, not a decomposition principle.

### By "what's easy to extract"

The first slice is often whatever's loosely coupled. That's fine for
**starting** — but if every subsequent slice continues to be "what's
easy," you end up with a decomposition shaped by accidental coupling,
not domain.

## Decomposing incrementally

You don't decompose all at once. From Newman:

```
1. Add a strangler facade in front of the monolith.
2. Extract one capability (the easiest one with clear value).
3. Move its data (CDC, then dual-write, then full cutover).
4. Delete the old code path.
5. Repeat. After every 2-3 services, pause and reassess.
6. Stop when the operational cost stops paying for itself.
```

Crucially: **decomposition has a stopping point**. The goal is not
"100% microservices." Some modules will remain part of the (now
smaller) monolith forever. That's fine.

## A reusable map

When designing a decomposition, fill this in:

```
- Capabilities: list them in business language.
- Bounded contexts: where does the language change?
- Transactions: list them; mark which must stay atomic.
- Volatility: which areas change weekly vs. yearly?
- Teams: who owns what today; who could own what tomorrow?
- Data: who owns each table; what cross-table joins exist?
- First slice: which capability is small, leaf-like, and valuable?
```

Decomposition is design work. It is slower than coding. Skip it and
the cuts will be defined by accident.

<Callout variant="warning">
"How many services?" is the wrong question. The right one is:
"which cuts make our team move faster, our system more reliable,
and our code more changeable — and at what cost?" If you can't
answer per-cut, don't cut.
</Callout>

## In summary

- Four strategies: by capability, by subdomain (DDD), by transaction,
  by volatility. Use all four together.
- Capability gives the first cut; DDD refines it; transactions and
  volatility validate it.
- Don't decompose by layer, by entity, or by tech preference. Those
  produce distributed monoliths.
- Right-sized services: one team, one head, one purpose, own DB,
  independent deploys. Nanoservices are an anti-pattern.
- Decompose incrementally, with a strangler. Stop when the cost
  stops paying for itself; 100% microservices is not the goal.
- The cut is the design. Spend more time on it than on the code.
