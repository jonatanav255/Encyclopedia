# Autoscaling

Autoscaling means "add capacity automatically when load goes
up, and remove it when load goes down." It is one of the most
attractive ideas in cloud computing and one of the easiest to
get badly wrong. Most autoscaling outages aren't from too
little capacity — they're from autoscaling reacting to the wrong
signal, too slowly, or too fast.

## What autoscaling actually solves

Three real problems:

1. **Variable load.** Daily peaks 3× troughs. Black Friday is
   100× normal. Without autoscaling, you size for peak and pay
   for idle servers 23 hours a day.
2. **Failed nodes.** A replica dies; autoscaling brings up a
   replacement.
3. **Bursty traffic.** Marketing email goes out, traffic
   spikes for 10 minutes, autoscaling expands and contracts.

It does not solve:

- A bottleneck downstream (DB, cache, broker). Adding app
  servers when the database is the bottleneck makes things
  worse — more connections, more contention.
- Slow code. If a request takes 5 seconds, autoscaling will
  scale you to a thousand replicas and the user still waits
  5 seconds.
- Cost. Autoscaling can lower your bill, but only if your code
  releases resources properly during scale-in.

## The two flavors

### Reactive autoscaling

Watch a metric. When it crosses a threshold, scale. When it
falls, scale back. Most cloud autoscalers (AWS ASG, GCP MIG,
K8s HPA) are reactive.

```
if avg_cpu > 70% for 2 minutes → add 1 instance
if avg_cpu < 30% for 5 minutes → remove 1 instance
```

Pros: simple, no prediction needed. Cons: always lags real
load. By the time CPU hits 70%, the queue is already growing.

### Predictive autoscaling

Forecast load from historical patterns (time of day, day of
week, marketing schedule) and provision ahead of the spike.

Pros: capacity is ready before the spike hits. No warm-up lag.
Cons: forecasts are wrong. Unanticipated spikes still fall
through. Costs more (you're paying for capacity you might not
need).

Production reality: most teams use **reactive** with
**scheduled minimums** — predefined floor capacity at known
busy times (Monday morning, retail Friday evening, etc.), with
reactive scaling above that. Pure prediction is rare.

## What signal to scale on

This is the single biggest cause of autoscaling failure: scaling
on a signal that doesn't represent saturation.

<Cheatsheet>
| Signal | Good for | Bad for |
|---|---|---|
| CPU utilization | CPU-bound services (image processing, ML, JSON parsing). | I/O-bound services where 100% CPU isn't your bottleneck. |
| Request rate (RPS) | Predictable per-request cost services. | When request cost varies a lot (cheap GET vs expensive search). |
| Latency (p95/p99) | Detecting saturation regardless of cause. | Reactive only — by the time latency is bad, you're already in trouble. |
| Queue depth (workers) | Async workers. The most accurate signal for queue-driven systems. | Synchronous services with no explicit queue. |
| Connection count | Long-lived connection servers (WebSocket). | HTTP services where connections are short. |
| Custom (business metric) | "Active games" for game servers, "active streams" for video. | Complexity — you have to define and emit the metric. |
| Memory utilization | Memory-bound services (caches, in-memory DBs). | Most app servers (memory usually isn't the bottleneck). |
</Cheatsheet>

The mistake new teams make: **CPU on a Node.js or Python app
server**. These services are I/O-bound — they spend most of
their time waiting on the DB or downstream APIs, with low CPU.
By the time CPU hits 70%, the service is wildly overloaded on
its actual bottleneck (event loop lag, connection saturation).

For Node.js, scale on **event loop lag** or **p95 latency**.
For background workers, scale on **queue depth**. For
classic synchronous services, scale on **request rate
per replica**.

## Warm-up and cold-start

Autoscaling can only add capacity as fast as a new instance
becomes useful. That gap is **warm-up time**:

- VM boot + OS + container pull: 30–120 s.
- App startup, JIT warm-up, cache warm: 30 s – several minutes.
- Health-check passes, LB registers: 10–30 s.

In the worst case, you've signaled "add a server" 5 minutes
before that server actually carries traffic. If your spike is
30 seconds long, autoscaling missed it entirely.

Mitigations:

- **Keep a warm baseline** of N replicas — never scale below it
  during known-busy hours.
- **Pre-pull container images** on standby nodes.
- **Optimize startup**. AOT-compile, prefetch dependencies,
  warm caches from a shared source.
- **Use serverless** for truly unpredictable bursts. Functions
  cold-start fast (low hundreds of ms for modern platforms),
  scale to thousands of concurrent invocations in seconds.

<Callout variant="warning">
The most common autoscaling outage: load spike arrives, the
existing replicas saturate, latency goes through the roof,
autoscaler kicks in. New replicas take 90 s to start. During
those 90 s, queues fill, retries amplify load, and the system
melts. By the time new capacity is up, the spike is over and
you're paying for replicas you don't need.
</Callout>

## Scale-in is the dangerous direction

Scale-out adds capacity. The worst case is "you spent some
money on idle servers." Scale-in removes capacity — and that
can fail much harder:

- **Active connections get killed**. Long-running uploads,
  WebSockets, large queries cut mid-flight.
- **In-flight work is lost** if the service doesn't drain
  gracefully.
- **Caches go cold** when a replica is destroyed; the remaining
  replicas absorb the load of cold-start traffic.

Patterns that mitigate scale-in damage:

- **Graceful shutdown**: on `SIGTERM`, stop accepting new
  requests, drain in-flight ones, then exit. Health-check
  fails before the LB sends a SIGKILL.
- **Connection draining**: LBs (ALB, NLB, K8s) support draining
  — new connections go elsewhere; existing connections finish.
- **Termination delays**: don't scale in faster than the
  longest acceptable request duration.
- **Slower scale-in than scale-out**. Scale out aggressively
  (it's safe); scale in conservatively (it's destructive).

Most autoscaler configs ship with `scale_out_cooldown = 60s`
and `scale_in_cooldown = 300s` for exactly this reason.

## Autoscaling and downstream dependencies

When the app tier scales out, every downstream system feels it:

- **Database connections**: 10 → 50 replicas at 100 connections
  each = 5000 connections to Postgres, which maxes at ~500
  without PgBouncer. Add a connection pooler before
  autoscaling the app tier.
- **Cache hit rate**: more replicas = each has a smaller share
  of traffic = local caches are colder = more hits to Redis or
  DB.
- **Rate limits on third-party APIs**: doubling your app tier
  doubles outbound traffic. Make sure the provider can handle
  it.

The app tier's autoscaling group must be sized to a database
that can support the maximum scale-out. Otherwise you've moved
the bottleneck downstream.

## Kubernetes HPA, VPA, CA

The three Kubernetes autoscalers, in case the term comes up:

- **HPA (Horizontal Pod Autoscaler)**: scales pod replicas.
  The closest analog to traditional ASGs.
- **VPA (Vertical Pod Autoscaler)**: changes pod resource
  requests (more CPU/RAM per pod). Often used for batch jobs
  where horizontal doesn't fit.
- **CA (Cluster Autoscaler)**: adds nodes to the cluster when
  pods can't be scheduled. Sits below HPA.

HPA + CA is the typical pair: HPA decides "we need 20 pods,"
CA notices "we need another VM to fit 20 pods," cloud provider
provides it.

## Serverless autoscaling

Functions-as-a-service (AWS Lambda, Google Cloud Functions,
Cloudflare Workers) autoscale to thousands of concurrent
executions per second with no config. You write a function;
the platform handles capacity entirely.

Tradeoffs:

- **Pros**: zero capacity planning. Pay per invocation. Truly
  bursty workloads (sporadic) cost almost nothing.
- **Cons**: cold starts (especially on JVM/CLR). Limited
  execution time. Vendor lock-in. Hard to use for stateful or
  long-running work.

For unpredictable, spiky, mostly-async work, serverless beats
traditional autoscaling. For steady-state services with
predictable load, containers/VMs are cheaper and faster.

## Cost vs capacity

Autoscaling promises elasticity. The dishonest sell: "you'll
pay only for what you use." The honest version:

- You'll pay for **your peaks plus your warm baseline plus
  buffer**.
- Provisioning is **lossy** — instances are usually billed in
  whole hours or whole instances, not seconds.
- **Reserved capacity** (1–3 year commits) is 30–60% cheaper
  than on-demand. Past a stable baseline, reserved beats
  autoscaled.

The right config is usually: **reserved instances for the
baseline + autoscaled on-demand for the peaks + spot/preemptible
for non-critical work**.

## Common pitfalls

### Scaling on CPU for I/O-bound services

Already covered, but worth repeating: a Node.js app at 30% CPU
might be hitting event loop lag of 200 ms. CPU never gets
high. Latency-based scaling catches this; CPU-based scaling
doesn't.

### Aggressive scale-in on health blips

A transient health check failure triggers scale-in. The
replicas that "failed" come back, but autoscaler already
replaced them — net effect is a cold-cache event for no reason.
Use cooldowns and require sustained signal before acting.

### Retry storms during scale-out lag

Existing replicas saturate; clients retry; retries amplify
load; replicas degrade further. By the time new capacity
arrives, the system is in a feedback loop. Mitigate with
exponential backoff + jitter on clients, load shedding on
servers, and circuit breakers between tiers.

### Missing minimum replicas

Setting `min_size = 1` means autoscaler can take you to one
replica. Lose that replica and you're out. Always
`min_size >= 2` for any non-trivial service, in different AZs.

### Per-instance state breaking scale-in

Replica holds cached data, an in-progress upload, an active
WebSocket. Scale-in destroys it. See stateless-services. If
your service has state, it can't be autoscaled freely.

### Autoscaling everything

Some services should be fixed-size: caches (autoscaling
invalidates the cache), stateful coordinators (you don't add a
second leader), single-purpose orchestrators. Autoscaling is
for stateless tiers.

## In summary

- Reactive autoscaling on the right signal is the default.
  Predictive is rare and oversold.
- For I/O-bound services, scale on latency, queue depth, or
  event-loop lag — not CPU.
- Warm-up gap means autoscaling is bad at very short spikes.
  Keep a warm baseline and consider serverless for spikes.
- Scale-in is the dangerous direction. Drain gracefully, slow
  down the in-cooldown, plan for connection cleanup.
- Verify downstream can absorb your max scale-out. Pool DB
  connections.
- Reserved baseline + autoscaled peaks beats pure autoscaling
  on cost.
