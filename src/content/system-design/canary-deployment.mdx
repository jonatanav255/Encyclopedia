# Canary deployment

Roll the new version out to a **small fraction of traffic
first** — 1%, then 5%, then 25%, then 100% — and compare its
behavior to the old version at each step. If the canary
regresses, roll back before the rest of the fleet is affected.

The name comes from coal miners' canaries: a small, sensitive
sample that warns of danger to the larger group. The
architectural payoff: the **blast radius of a bad deploy is
capped at the canary fraction** until you have evidence it's
safe.

## The mechanics

A canary rollout in steps:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Deploy the new version alongside the old.">
A small number of pods running v2. Old version still handles
the majority of traffic.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Route a small fraction to the canary.">
1% is a common starting point. Either by weighted load
balancing, service mesh routing, or feature flag.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Compare metrics — old vs new.">
Error rate, latency p50/p99, business metrics. The point is
not "is the new version OK in absolute terms" but "is it
better, same, or worse than the old."
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="If healthy, advance.">
1% → 5% → 25% → 50% → 100%. Each step is gated on the
comparison.
</Step>
<Step n={5} title="If unhealthy, roll back.">
Drop canary traffic to 0%. Old version handles all requests
again. Diagnose at leisure.
</Step>
</Steps>

The healthy variant is uneventful. The unhealthy variant
short-circuits at 1% and never reaches the other 99%.

## Why it's the default for user-facing services

The "if your deploy is bad, only 1% of users see it" property
is hard to overstate. It changes the cost of shipping:

- Engineers ship more often because individual deploys are
  smaller and lower-risk.
- Regressions caught at canary cost a rollback; regressions
  caught after full rollout cost an incident.
- The team's relationship to deploy fear improves measurably.

Canary deployments are the default at most large web companies
for this reason.

## Traffic split mechanisms

<Cheatsheet>
| Mechanism | How | Granularity |
|---|---|---|
| Pod count ratio | 1 canary pod + 99 stable; LB round-robins | ~1% if connections are balanced |
| Weighted routing | LB rule: 1% to canary, 99% to stable | Exact percentage |
| Service mesh | Mesh route: weight `v2=1, v1=99` | Exact, with sticky-session support |
| Feature flag | App-level: % of requests go through new path | Per-request, fine-grained |
| Header-based | Internal users / dogfood by header | Targeted, not random |

</Cheatsheet>

Pod-count ratio is the simplest and works in plain Kubernetes
with no extra tooling. Mesh and weighted routing are more
precise. Feature flags give you in-app control without needing
infra changes.

## Comparing the canary

A canary is only as good as the comparison. You're asking: "is
v2 worse than v1 on the same traffic?"

The discipline:

- **Same metrics, both versions, side by side.** Don't compare
  v2's error rate to "the historical baseline." Compare to v1
  **right now**.
- **Same traffic mix.** Canary should get a representative
  sample, not 1% of low-traffic endpoints.
- **Wait long enough.** A canary at 1% for 30 seconds has too
  few samples to detect regressions. Wait for statistical
  power; minutes, sometimes longer for low-traffic services.
- **Watch the right metrics.** Error rate, latency p50 + p99
  (split by success/fail), business metrics (conversions,
  signup completions). CPU/memory of the canary process if
  resource regressions matter.

Automation: tools like **Argo Rollouts**, **Flagger**, **Spinnaker
Kayenta**, **Linkerd's traffic-shifting** automate this — compute
metrics, decide healthy/unhealthy, advance or roll back.

## What "regression" means

The canary's metrics need explicit thresholds. Examples:

- Error rate of v2 must not exceed v1 by more than 0.5% absolute,
  measured over the last 5 minutes.
- p99 latency of v2 must be within 20% of v1's p99.
- Business metric (conversions/min) must be within 2 standard
  deviations of v1's recent rate.

Without explicit thresholds, you'll either roll back on every
noise spike or fail to catch real regressions. The
**comparison is statistical**; treat it that way.

## Phases and dwell time

A typical canary plan:

```
0%  → 1%   (wait 5 min)
1%  → 5%   (wait 5 min)
5%  → 25%  (wait 10 min)
25% → 50%  (wait 10 min)
50% → 100% (full rollout)
```

The numbers depend on traffic volume. A high-traffic service
collects enough samples in 1 minute at 1% to make a decision; a
low-traffic service needs longer or a higher initial percentage.

The "dwell time" at each step is **statistical confidence**, not
arbitrary. Less = faster deploys, more risk; more = safer, slower.

## State and migrations

Canary deployments inherit the same state problems as
blue-green:

- Schema must be backward-compatible — both v1 and v2 are running
  simultaneously and both write to the same DB.
- Write-side regressions can affect data in ways that don't
  rollback when traffic does.
- Caches see writes from both versions; key versioning matters.

The expand-then-contract migration discipline applies here just
as in blue-green. See `blue-green-deployment`.

## Variants

Canary deployment has several flavors:

<Cheatsheet>
| Variant | What |
|---|---|
| Random canary | 1% of all users see v2 |
| Internal canary | Employees / dogfood users see v2 first |
| Geographic canary | One region rolls first; spread on success |
| Cell-based canary | One "cell" rolls first; see `cell-based-architecture` |
| Sticky canary | Same user stays on the same version for the session |

</Cheatsheet>

Internal canary is the cheapest: zero customer risk for the
first phase. Sticky canaries matter when a user's experience
across requests should be consistent (multi-step forms,
real-time apps).

## Automatic rollback

The most valuable canary feature: **automatic rollback** when
metrics regress. Without it, a canary still requires a human to
watch the dashboard during a deploy. With it, the deploy runs
overnight and rolls itself back if something goes wrong.

The trade-off: false positives. An automatic rollback on noise
is annoying; an automatic rollback on a real bug is invaluable.
Tune thresholds; budget for both.

## Anti-patterns

- **Canary too small or too short.** 0.1% for 30 seconds
  collects almost no data and gives false confidence.
- **Comparing to historical baselines.** The traffic shape
  yesterday was different. Compare to v1 right now.
- **Skipping the canary for "small" changes.** "It's just a
  config change" is the lead sentence of many incident
  postmortems.
- **Manual gating only.** A canary you advance by hand at 2am
  is a canary that becomes "screw it, just push" by 3am.
  Automate.
- **Single metric.** A canary that only checks error rate
  misses latency regressions. Check several signals.

<Callout variant="warning">
A canary doesn't catch **all** regressions. Slow-burning bugs
(memory leaks, data corruption) may not show up in a 10-minute
canary window. Pair canary with longer-term monitoring after
full rollout.
</Callout>

## Canary vs feature flags

Both gate exposure. The difference:

- **Canary** = whole new code path / binary, gated by traffic
  routing.
- **Feature flag** = single code path, gated by configuration
  evaluated per request.

You can use both together: canary the new binary, feature-flag
the new feature inside it. Decouple deploy from release.

See feature flags at architecture level.

## In summary

- Roll new versions to a small fraction first, advance on
  evidence.
- Compare v2 metrics to v1 **right now**, not to historical
  baselines.
- Schema and state must be backward-compatible during the
  rollout.
- Automate the advance and rollback — humans gating canaries by
  hand at 3am defeats the purpose.
- Pair with feature flags for finer-grained, per-feature
  exposure control.
- Canaries don't catch slow-burning bugs; keep monitoring after
  full rollout.
- The default deploy strategy for user-facing services in
  2026.
