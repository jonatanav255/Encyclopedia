# Alerting design

The point of alerting is to wake a human up when, and only
when, a human is actually needed. Every wake-up that wasn't
needed erodes the on-caller's attention; every wake-up not
delivered when needed erodes user trust. Both failure modes
compound — and the second is usually the consequence of the
first.

The SRE book and *Implementing Service Level Objectives* (Alex
Hidalgo) converge on the same prescription: page on **symptoms
that map to user pain**, with **burn-rate alerts** anchored to
**SLOs**. Everything else is detail.

## Symptom-based vs cause-based

<Compare a="Symptom-based" b="Cause-based">
<>
"Users are seeing high latency on checkout for the last 5
minutes."

- Captures **user impact directly**.
- Robust to architecture changes — the symptom is the same.
- Page-worthy because by definition users are hurting.
- One alert per critical user journey, not per microservice.
</>
<>
"DB connection pool on payment-service is 95% full."

- Captures **a possible cause**.
- Fires whether or not users are affected.
- Decays in value as architecture changes (the pool moves, the
  threshold drifts).
- Good for diagnosis runbooks, bad for paging.
</>
</Compare>

The rule, from the SRE book: **page on symptoms, debug on
causes**. The on-call alert tells you something users care
about is broken. The diagnostic dashboards tell you why. Two
different things, two different signals.

The single most common alerting mistake is paging on causes
("CPU > 80%"). At 4am, you wake up to a CPU number with no idea
whether users care. Half the time they don't.

## SLOs are the contract

Every page should trace back to an SLO that's burning. If you
can't say "this alert fires because we're going to miss our 99.9%
availability SLO this quarter," the alert isn't earning its
keep.

An SLO is a target — 99.9% of requests succeed over 30 days.
The **error budget** is what you can spend — 0.1% × total
requests = the number of errors allowed. The **burn rate** is
how fast you're spending it.

See SLI / SLO / SLA for the full mechanics.

## Burn-rate alerting

The wrong way: "page if error rate > 1% for 5 minutes." Two
problems — it always fires at low traffic (1% of 100 requests
is 1 error, signal-less), and it under-alerts at high traffic
(0.5% of 10M requests/hour is a burning budget but never trips
the threshold).

The right way: burn rate against the budget window.

```
budget burn rate = (errors observed) / (errors budgeted for this window)
```

If your SLO is 99.9% over 30 days and you've used 14× the
allotted budget over the last hour, you'll burn the entire month's
budget in just over a day. That's a page.

A two-window strategy is standard:

<Cheatsheet>
| Severity | Threshold | Page? |
|---|---|---|
| Fast burn | 14.4× over 1h **and** 14.4× over 5m | Yes — immediate page |
| Slow burn | 6× over 6h **and** 6× over 30m | Yes — page with lower urgency |
| Trickle burn | 1× over 3d (concerning but slow) | Ticket, not page |

</Cheatsheet>

The "two windows" pattern (e.g., 1h AND 5m) keeps a slow
sustained problem from triggering on a single bad minute and
keeps a fast problem from waiting for the long window to fill.
Both conditions must be true.

Numbers cited in the SRE Workbook; pick the ones that match your
SLO and incident tolerance.

## Multi-window, multi-burn-rate

The SRE Workbook's "alerting on SLOs" chapter goes further:
three or four thresholds, each with a paired short+long window.
The math is straightforward — pick the largest burn rate you'd
tolerate (e.g., "burning 100% in 2 hours = 360× rate, page
fast") and a slower rate (e.g., "5% in a day = 1.5× rate,
ticket").

The principle: **the size of the alert matches the size of the
fire**. A 14× burn deserves a page. A 1.5× burn over a week
deserves a Slack message you'll read on Monday.

## What gets paged

A short, opinionated list:

<Cheatsheet>
| Page-worthy | Not page-worthy |
|---|---|
| Customer-visible error rate burn fast | A single retry happening |
| Customer-visible latency burn fast | A single slow request |
| Total service unreachable | One pod restarting |
| Data loss in progress | Disk at 60% |
| Authentication/authorization broken | A code-coverage drop |
| Payment / checkout broken | A non-critical job late by 5min |

</Cheatsheet>

If you have 50 alerts mapped to "pages," 45 of them are wrong.
A healthy on-call rotation has a handful of distinct page
reasons per service, all tied to SLOs.

## The noisy-pager death spiral

Once an on-caller starts getting paged for non-issues, three
things happen:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Attention erodes.">
After three false pages this week, the fourth feels false too.
The on-caller checks the dashboard slower; the real incident
takes longer to find.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Trust in monitoring collapses.">
Alerts get muted ("we know about that one"), filtered, or
ignored. Some are muted for a real reason; some bleed into the
quiet pile.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="The on-call rotation breaks.">
People skip rotations. New hires don't want it. Incidents that
require institutional knowledge happen to the people who don't
have it. Errors compound.
</Step>
</Steps>

The cure isn't "be tougher about it." The cure is **alert
hygiene** — every page is reviewed in the postmortem, and
non-actionable pages get fixed within a week. This is the most
important post-incident practice and the most consistently
skipped one.

## Paging vs ticketing

Two distinct channels:

- **Paging**: phone call / SMS / push notification that wakes
  someone up. Reserved for things that need a response **right
  now**. SLO-burn alerts, total outages, data loss.
- **Ticketing / Slack**: a message that someone will see during
  business hours. SSL cert expiring in 7 days, disk filling
  slowly, a single dependency timing out and recovering.

The cost of a page is roughly **the sleep of the human who
gets it**. Don't spend that on anything but a real problem.

The cost of a ticket is **eventual attention**. Use it for
"someone should look at this, but not at 3am."

## Where alerts live: the system view

A good alert lives in one place:

- **The alerting system** (Alertmanager, PagerDuty, Opsgenie,
  Splunk OnCall) handles routing, schedules, escalations,
  deduplication.
- **The monitoring system** evaluates the conditions.
- **The metrics store** holds the data.
- **The runbook** describes what to do when the alert fires.

A runbook is **mandatory for every paging alert**. "The page
fires, you wake up — what do you do?" If the answer isn't
written, the alert fails its first principle: it's not
actionable. A page without a runbook is a hazard.

## Things to monitor but never page on

The diagnostic layer. Graph them; alert quietly if at all:

- Resource utilization (CPU, memory, disk).
- Per-component error rates (downstream service errors,
  pool acquire timeouts).
- Replication lag (if not yet user-visible).
- Build failures, deploy failures.
- Slow queries, individual slow requests.

These tell you **why** when an SLO-symptom page fires. They
**don't** page on their own.

## Edge cases that need explicit handling

- **Cron / batch jobs**. A nightly job that fails twice in a row
  is page-worthy if it feeds an SLO; otherwise it's a ticket.
- **Low traffic services**. SLO-percentage math breaks at low
  volume (0% errors of 10 requests proves nothing). Use
  absolute thresholds with care.
- **Maintenance windows**. Either silence alerts during planned
  windows or have a "deploy in progress" suppression so noisy
  but expected behavior doesn't page.
- **Cascading failures**. One root cause can trip 50 alerts.
  Use grouping and alert correlation; one page per incident, not
  per signal.

<Callout variant="warning">
"All cause-based alerts can be removed once symptom-based
SLO alerts are in place." That's the SRE Workbook's bold claim.
It's usually wrong in detail and right in spirit: aim that
direction, even if you keep some cause-based for legacy.
</Callout>

## A starting set of alerts

For a typical user-facing service, this is enough to start:

- **Availability burn fast** — 14× over 1h+5m, page.
- **Availability burn slow** — 6× over 6h+30m, page.
- **Latency burn fast** — same shape, against latency SLO.
- **Total outage** — request rate near zero during business
  hours, page.
- **Auth/payment specific** — burn alerts on the SLOs of the
  critical user journeys.
- **Trickle and ticket** — everything else.

If that list is more than a dozen alerts, prune. Less is more
when sleep is on the line.

## In summary

- Page on symptoms (user-visible pain), debug on causes.
- Anchor every page to an SLO via burn-rate math.
- Two-window burn-rate alerts beat naive threshold alerts.
- A page without a runbook is broken.
- Tickets and Slack are for "look at this eventually"; pages
  are for "look at this now."
- Alert hygiene is a postmortem discipline, not a one-time
  tuning exercise.
- Less is more — a handful of well-chosen pages beats a
  hundred mediocre ones.
