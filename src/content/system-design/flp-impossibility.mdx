# FLP impossibility

The FLP result is the most cited theoretical result in
distributed systems, and the most operationally misread.
Fischer, Lynch, and Paterson proved in 1985:

> In a **fully asynchronous** system where even **one process
> may crash**, there is **no deterministic consensus
> algorithm** that always terminates.

That sentence, decoded, explains why distributed databases
look the way they do. It also explains why every real system
sidesteps FLP rather than violates it.

## What it actually says

The model FLP assumes:

- **Asynchronous network**: messages take arbitrary time;
  there is no upper bound. You can't tell a slow message from
  a lost one.
- **Crash failures only** (no Byzantine behavior — that's
  weaker than FLP needs).
- **Deterministic algorithm**: given the same input and
  message history, the algorithm makes the same decisions.
- **Consensus**: every non-faulty process must eventually
  decide on the same value, drawn from values proposed by some
  process.

In this model: **no algorithm can guarantee termination** for
all execution traces while preserving safety (agreement +
validity). For any deterministic protocol, there exists an
execution where consensus is never reached.

## What it does not say

A list of common misreadings:

- **"Consensus is impossible."** No — only **deterministic,
  always-terminating** consensus in **fully asynchronous**
  systems is impossible. Real systems use randomness, partial
  synchrony assumptions, or accept that progress isn't always
  guaranteed.
- **"Distributed systems can't reach agreement."** They can.
  FLP is about worst-case termination, not the common case.
- **"Networks can't be reliable enough."** FLP holds even with
  reliable message delivery — the issue is that "slow" and
  "dead" are indistinguishable when there's no timing bound.
- **"Paxos / Raft violate FLP."** They don't. They sacrifice
  guaranteed termination to preserve safety — they may not
  decide in pathological scenarios, but they never decide
  *wrongly*.

## Why the proof works

The core argument: in an asynchronous system, you can't tell
whether a process is **dead** or **slow**. Any deterministic
algorithm that tries to make progress despite one possible
failure has a "critical" decision moment that can be derailed
indefinitely by carefully timing message delays.

Sketch:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Define bivalent configurations.">
A protocol configuration is **bivalent** if executions from it
could decide either 0 or 1. Initially the system is bivalent
(values are proposed but not yet decided).
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Define univalent configurations.">
Eventually the protocol must commit to one decision —
"univalent". The transition from bivalent to univalent happens
on some specific event (a message delivery).
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Show that bivalence can be preserved.">
By carefully delaying that critical message and delivering
something else first, the adversary can keep the system
bivalent. Repeated indefinitely, the protocol never decides.
</Step>
</Steps>

The adversary doesn't need to crash processes or drop
messages — just delay them. And in an asynchronous model,
arbitrary delay is allowed.

## How real systems sidestep FLP

Real protocols don't violate FLP; they relax one of its
assumptions.

### 1. Partial synchrony

The most common escape. Assume the network is **eventually
synchronous** — there exist (unknown) bounds on message delay
and processing time, eventually. After some unknown "global
stabilization time," timeouts are reliable.

Paxos, Raft, and Multi-Paxos all rely on this. They preserve
**safety** (never decide wrongly) unconditionally; **liveness**
(eventually decide) holds once the network behaves.

In practice: a healthy datacenter satisfies eventual synchrony
nearly always. Pathological partitions or extreme overload
break it temporarily; protocols halt rather than decide
incorrectly, and resume when conditions improve.

### 2. Randomization

Ben-Or's randomized consensus terminates in expected finite
time. Each process flips a coin to escape symmetric scenarios
the deterministic version can't.

Used by: PBFT-style protocols, some cryptocurrencies.
Practical for small clusters; doesn't scale to large ones.

### 3. Failure detectors

Chandra and Toueg formalized **unreliable failure detectors**
as a way to model "noisy crash detection." With a "weak
accuracy" failure detector (no process is detected dead before
it actually fails), consensus is solvable.

Real heartbeat-based failure detectors don't meet this
strictly — they sometimes flag healthy processes as dead — but
they approximate it well enough in practice. See failure
detectors for details.

### 4. Quorum-based progress

Instead of requiring all processes to agree, require a
**majority quorum**. As long as a majority is alive and
reachable, the protocol can decide.

This is how Paxos / Raft / ZAB practically achieve liveness:
they decide when a majority responds, ignore the minority,
and let the minority catch up later.

## Consequences for system design

FLP's practical implications shape every distributed database:

### 1. Quorum is fundamental

If you need consensus across N nodes, you need a majority to
agree. 3 nodes survive 1 failure; 5 nodes survive 2. This is
not arbitrary — it's the only way to guarantee that two
distinct decisions can't be reached on opposite sides of a
partition.

### 2. Timeouts are heuristics, not truths

A timeout doesn't tell you a process is dead. It tells you
you're tired of waiting. Algorithms must be safe under
false-positive failure detection — re-elections, retries,
fencing tokens, etc.

### 3. Liveness vs safety

Production protocols choose **safety always, liveness when
possible**. A Raft cluster without majority halts (no liveness)
but doesn't elect two leaders (safety preserved). The
alternative would be unsafe — and FLP shows there's no way to
have both guaranteed.

### 4. Designing for the common case

Most of the time the network is well-behaved; consensus
finishes in a round trip. The pathological FLP-style scenarios
are vanishingly rare. Protocols are designed to be fast in the
common case and **safe in the pathological case** — never
fast in pathological cases (FLP forbids it).

### 5. Why ZooKeeper / etcd exist

A coordination service is "FLP solved well enough so you
don't have to think about it." Build your distributed system
on top of one, and use Paxos-/Raft-backed primitives instead
of reinventing them.

## Common misuses in interviews and design docs

- **"FLP says consensus is impossible, so we use eventual
  consistency."** No. Eventual consistency isn't "consensus
  weakened"; it's a different consistency model entirely.
  FLP is irrelevant to LWW or CRDTs.
- **"Raft violates FLP."** No. Raft preserves safety; it
  doesn't guarantee termination in pathological asynchrony.
- **"FLP means distributed transactions are impossible."**
  No. Distributed transactions exist; they just may not
  terminate in pathological cases (the same protocols handle
  the common case fine).
- **"FLP is only about consensus."** It's specifically about
  consensus, but consensus is the foundation of leader
  election, atomic commit, distributed locks, replicated logs,
  and configuration management. Most "I need a distributed X"
  reduces to consensus.

## The relationship to CAP

CAP is the brittle, partition-flavored cousin of FLP. Both say
that distributed agreement has fundamental costs. CAP frames
it as "during partitions, sacrifice C or A." FLP frames it as
"in async systems with failures, sacrifice guaranteed
termination."

The two are deeply related:

- **Linearizable systems are CP** by CAP. They use consensus
  protocols (Paxos/Raft) that hit FLP's wall during sustained
  asynchrony — they halt rather than violate safety.
- **AP systems** sidestep both CAP and FLP by not requiring
  consensus for every write. They accept conflicts and
  reconcile.

If you need linearizability, you must accept FLP's tax — a
small probability of unavailability under pathological network
conditions.

## Useful mental shortcut

> Consensus is solvable when the network mostly works and you
> have a majority alive. Otherwise, protocols halt — by
> design, because halting is safer than disagreeing.

That captures everything you need from FLP in practice.
Whenever someone says "this system has 99.999% uptime for
writes," what they mean is "the network behaves long enough,
often enough, that consensus terminates routinely — FLP's
worst-case never strikes."

## In summary

- FLP: in a **fully asynchronous** system with even **one
  crash failure**, no deterministic consensus algorithm can
  guarantee termination.
- Real systems sidestep with **partial synchrony**,
  **randomization**, **failure detectors**, and **quorum-based
  progress**.
- Paxos and Raft preserve **safety always**, sacrificing
  **liveness in pathological cases**.
- Quorum (majority) is the structural answer to FLP-style
  failures — you can't have linearizability without a
  majority alive and reachable.
- Use coordination services (etcd, ZooKeeper) so the FLP
  workaround is solved once and inherited.
