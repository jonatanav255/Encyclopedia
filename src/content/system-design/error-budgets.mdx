# Error budgets

The mechanism that turns an SLO from a number into a
decision-making tool.

If your SLO is 99.9% availability over 30 days, that's
**43 minutes** of allowed "bad time" — your error budget. As
long as you're not exhausting it, you're free to ship. When
you blow through it, the rules change: feature work pauses,
reliability work takes priority.

## Why error budgets exist

Pre-budget, product and reliability had no shared currency.
Product wanted to ship; SRE wanted to slow down to avoid
breaking things. Neither could prove the other wrong.

Error budgets give a number both teams argue from:

- "We've used 50% of the budget; we're safe to launch."
- "We've used 110% of the budget; no new launches this month."

The budget reframes reliability from "we're worried" to "we
have N minutes left to spend."

## How it works

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Define an SLO.">
"99.9% of requests return non-5xx over a 30-day window."
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Compute the budget.">
0.1% of 30 days = 43 minutes of allowed bad time, or 0.1% of
total requests in error.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Track consumption.">
Real-time burn rate dashboard. "We've consumed 18 minutes
this month, 25 left."
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Set policies tied to budget state.">
- Budget green (under 25% used): ship aggressively.
- Budget yellow (25-75% used): cautious launches.
- Budget red (over 75%): no risky launches; focus on
  reliability.
- Budget exhausted: launch freeze.
</Step>
</Steps>

The policies are agreed in advance. When the budget is
exhausted, the response isn't "argue"; it's "execute the
policy."

## Burn rate

Raw "minutes used" doesn't capture rate. A small steady
problem might consume the budget gradually; a catastrophe
might consume it all in one hour.

**Burn rate** = how fast you're consuming the budget relative
to the SLO window:

- 1x burn rate: at this rate, you'll exactly exhaust the
  budget by the end of the window. Normal.
- 10x burn rate: you'd exhaust the budget in 1/10th the
  window. Page someone.
- 100x burn rate: you're consuming 30 days of budget in 7
  hours. Immediate response.

Google SRE recommends multi-window alerts:

```
fast burn:  5% of budget in 1 hour    → page immediately
slow burn:  10% of budget in 6 hours  → ticket
```

(See alerting-design.)

## What burns the budget

- **Failed deploys** with rollback time.
- **Bad releases** before detection.
- **Capacity issues** (overload, OOM).
- **Dependency failures** (DB down, third-party API down).
- **Network events** (partition, DDoS).
- **Maintenance windows** (if not exempted).

What doesn't burn the budget:

- **Planned, pre-announced downtime** outside the SLO scope.
- **Errors the customer caused** (4xx, with care).

## Negotiating the budget

Product wants to launch a risky feature. The conversation:

> "We're at 60% of monthly budget. This launch has a 5% chance
> of consuming another 30%. If it goes wrong, we're at 90% by
> Tuesday with two weeks left in the month. Can we hold off,
> or do we have an aggressive rollback ready?"

The number forces the trade-off into a measurable shape.
Without the budget, the conversation is "I'm worried."

## Common mistakes

### Treating 100% availability as the goal

You'd never launch. Reliability work would dominate forever.
The budget says: it's OK to spend some.

### Not exempting planned events

A scheduled maintenance window during which the service is
intentionally down should not consume the user-facing budget
(it should be a separate "planned downtime" tracker, with
its own SLA).

### Penalizing the wrong cause

A third-party payment provider goes down for an hour. Your
service errors. Your budget is consumed. But the cause
wasn't your code — should you stop launching?

The honest answer: yes, often. Customer experience was bad
regardless of cause. But the post-mortem and the response
should focus on the dependency, not your team's deploy
practices.

### Inconsistent enforcement

Budget is exhausted. Product launches anyway. Why? Pressure
from above. Now SRE has no leverage; the budget is theater.

Either the org enforces budget policies consistently, or
they don't really work.

## Budget rollover

Question: if I have unused budget at month-end, can I roll
over to next month?

The honest answer: usually no. The budget is a guardrail,
not a savings account. Rolling over encourages "save up the
budget for a big risky launch," which is the opposite of
healthy.

But: a service that's chronically under its SLO (using only
20% of budget) suggests the SLO is too loose; tighten it
rather than rolling over.

## When error budgets don't fit

- **Brand-new services** with no traffic history; no baseline.
  Set provisional SLOs, revise after a quarter of data.
- **Services with very few requests** (admin tools, internal
  cron jobs); percentage-based budgets are noisy. Use
  absolute counts instead.
- **Hard-real-time systems** (medical, aviation). Reliability
  isn't negotiable; budgets don't apply the same way.

## In summary

- Error budget = 1 − SLO over a window. The amount of
  badness you're allowed.
- Drives policy: green (ship), yellow (cautious), red
  (freeze).
- Burn rate captures how fast you're consuming, not just how
  much.
- Aligns product and SRE on a shared number.
- Policies must be agreed in advance and enforced
  consistently — otherwise it's theater.
- Doesn't apply uniformly to brand-new or hard-real-time
  systems.
