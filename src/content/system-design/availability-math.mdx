# Availability math

"Three nines" sounds vague until you do the math. **99.9%
uptime** is **8.76 hours of downtime per year**. **99.99%** is
**52 minutes**. The difference is the difference between "two
afternoons of incidents" and "no observable downtime." These two
SLAs require very different architectures, and you can't pick
one without knowing the numbers.

This page is the arithmetic of availability — the budget table,
how availability composes across components, and why naïve
microservice architectures *erode* availability rather than
improve it.

## The nines table

<Cheatsheet>
| Availability | Downtime / year | Downtime / month | Downtime / week |
|---|---|---|---|
| 99% (two nines) | 3.65 days | 7.3 hours | 1.68 hours |
| 99.5% | 1.83 days | 3.65 hours | 50.4 min |
| 99.9% (three nines) | 8.76 hours | 43.8 min | 10.1 min |
| 99.95% | 4.38 hours | 21.9 min | 5.04 min |
| 99.99% (four nines) | 52.6 min | 4.38 min | 60.5 s |
| 99.999% (five nines) | 5.26 min | 26.3 s | 6.05 s |
| 99.9999% (six nines) | 31.5 s | 2.63 s | 0.6 s |
</Cheatsheet>

Each extra nine costs **10x more downtime budget shrinkage**.
The engineering cost is super-linear: going from three to four
nines is typically harder than from two to three.

## What "uptime" actually means

The number is meaningless without saying **what was measured and
how**. Three pitfalls:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Probed from where?">
Internal pings will always show better availability than external
synthetics. Customer-perceived availability is what's measured
from outside the network you run.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="What counts as down?">
Hard 500s, sure. But also: 200 with the wrong data; correct
data but past the latency SLO; correct data but on the wrong
version after a botched deploy. Different teams draw the line
differently.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Aggregated how?">
"99.99% across the year" can hide a single multi-hour outage if
the rest of the year is perfect. Most SLAs report *per-month* or
use *request-success rate* (good requests / total) to avoid this.
</Step>
</Steps>

The honest SLO is a triple: **(metric, target, window)**. Example:
"99.95% of API requests succeed within 200 ms, measured by edge
probes, in any rolling 30-day window."

## Composing availability — series vs parallel

If your request must succeed at A *and* B *and* C — they're in
**series** — availability multiplies.

```
A_series = A * B * C
```

Example: a request that needs the API server (99.9%), DB
(99.9%), and cache (99.9%) all up.

```
0.999 × 0.999 × 0.999 = 0.997  (≈ 99.7%)
```

Three "three nines" components in series give you **less than
three nines** combined. Series erodes availability.

If your request can succeed at A *or* B *or* C — they're in
**parallel** (redundant replicas) — unavailability multiplies.

```
A_parallel = 1 - (1 - A) × (1 - B) × (1 - C)
```

Example: three replicas, each 99.9%.

```
1 - 0.001^3 = 0.999999999 (~9 nines, in theory)
```

In practice replicas share failure modes (same network, same
region, same deploy), so the gain is smaller than the math says.
But the direction is right: **parallelism increases availability,
serial chains decrease it**.

## Why microservices *reduce* availability without effort

A request that touches 10 services, each at 99.9% individually,
chains them in series:

```
0.999^10 = 0.99004 ≈ 99% (two nines)
```

Ten three-nines services collectively give **two nines**. To
maintain three nines end-to-end with 10 components in series,
each must be ~99.99%.

```
x^10 = 0.999  →  x ≈ 0.9999
```

Each service must be **10x more available** than the target SLO.
This is the "microservices availability tax." Solutions:

- **Fewer hops on the critical path** — the deepest available
  call chain has the deepest budget.
- **Graceful degradation** — non-critical services treated as
  optional; their unavailability returns partial results, not
  failure.
- **Caching** — last known good result returned when the
  downstream is unavailable.

## SLI, SLO, SLA — the SRE vocabulary

<Compare a="SLO (Objective)" b="SLA (Agreement)">
<>
The **internal target**. "99.95% of requests succeed in 200 ms,
30-day window." Your team owns and tunes this. Engineering
decisions are driven by SLOs.
</>
<>
The **external promise**. The number you write in customer
contracts, with associated refunds/credits if you miss it.
Always **looser** than the SLO — your SLA is the SLO with
safety margin baked in. If SLO is 99.95%, SLA is typically
99.9% or 99%.
</>
</Compare>

The thing measured is the **SLI** (Service Level Indicator) —
e.g. "fraction of HTTP responses in any 5-minute window with
status < 500 and latency < 200 ms."

SLO = target on the SLI. SLA = contractual promise on the SLO.

## Error budgets

If your SLO is 99.95%, your **error budget** is 0.05% — the
amount of failure you're allowed to have without missing the
SLO. In a 30-day month:

```
30 days × 24 hr × 60 min = 43 200 minutes
Error budget @ 99.95% = 0.0005 × 43 200 ≈ 21.6 min/month
```

Error budgets are the **risk currency** of an SRE org:

- Spending it on planned things (e.g. chaos experiments, risky
  deploys) is fine.
- Spending it on unplanned outages without learning is the
  problem.
- Burning the entire budget early in the window → freeze
  risky changes until the window resets.

This is how Google SRE separates "we shipped fast" from "we
should slow down." Without an error budget, the only question
is "did we have an outage today?" and that conversation has no
trade-off lever.

## MTBF, MTTR, and their relationship to availability

Availability decomposes into two numbers:

```
Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR)

MTBF = mean time between failures
MTTR = mean time to recovery
```

A system with **infrequent but long** outages and one with
**frequent but short** outages can have identical availability.
Two levers:

- **Increase MTBF** — make failures less frequent (redundancy,
  better testing, careful releases).
- **Decrease MTTR** — make failures shorter (faster detection,
  automated rollback, runbooks, hot standbys).

Most modern reliability work targets **MTTR**, not MTBF. You
can't eliminate failures at scale; you can absolutely **shorten
them**. Automated rollback turns a 30-minute outage into a 90-second
blip.

## A worked example

Service composed of:

- Edge (CDN): 99.99%
- API gateway: 99.99%
- Auth service: 99.99%
- App tier (3 replicas, each 99.9%): parallel.
- Postgres (HA pair, primary+replica failover): 99.95% effective.
- Redis (3-node cluster): 99.95%.

Compute end-to-end on the read path:

```
App tier parallel  = 1 - 0.001^3 ≈ 0.999999  (call it 99.9999%)
Series:
  Edge × Gateway × Auth × App × Postgres × Redis
= 0.9999 × 0.9999 × 0.9999 × 0.999999 × 0.9995 × 0.9995
≈ 0.9982 (≈ 99.82%)
```

End-to-end availability ≈ **99.82%**, below three nines, driven
by Postgres and Redis. To hit three nines you'd:

- Run Postgres in active-active or with sub-30-second automated
  failover (target 99.99%).
- Run Redis with replicas and accept eventual consistency on
  near-miss reads.
- Cache aggressively so that Redis being briefly unavailable
  serves stale-but-acceptable data.

## Designs that buy availability

- **Replication** (parallel components).
- **Stateless services** (instances are interchangeable).
- **Automated failover** (no human in the recovery loop).
- **Graceful degradation** (failed dependencies don't fail the
  whole request).
- **Backpressure & shedding** (overload doesn't compound into
  outage).
- **Bulkheads / pools** (one slow thing doesn't drain everything).
- **Health checks + load balancer eviction** (route around
  broken nodes in seconds).

Most of these come from *Release It!* (Nygard) and the Google SRE
books. They're not optional at four nines.

## Designs that destroy availability

- **Singletons in the request path** — the leader, the gateway,
  the one cache node. Single point of failure.
- **Deep synchronous call chains** — availability multiplies.
- **Shared resources** — one tenant exhausting capacity for all.
- **Manual recovery** — your MTTR is now "however long until
  on-call wakes up."
- **No circuit breakers** — one slow dep cascades.

<Callout variant="warning">
Beware "we have a hot standby" without **regularly tested
failover**. Failover paths that aren't exercised in production
*will not work* when needed. Netflix's Chaos Monkey exists
because the team that doesn't kill servers in normal times finds
out the failover is broken in a crisis.
</Callout>

## In summary

- Three nines = ~9 hours/year, four nines = ~52 min/year, five
  nines = ~5 min/year.
- Components in series multiply availabilities — a long chain
  of three-nines services is a two-nines system.
- Components in parallel multiply unavailabilities — replicas
  add nines, in theory.
- Microservices erode availability unless every service is one
  nine better than the target.
- Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR). Most reliability work
  shortens MTTR, not extends MTBF.
- Error budgets convert availability into a trade-off currency
  the org can actually reason about.
