# Throttling and quotas

Rate limiting protects **the system**. Quotas constrain **the
customer**. Throttling is the act of slowing or rejecting traffic
when either is exceeded. The mechanisms overlap; the contracts and
user experience differ sharply.

Confusing these is one of the most common API-design mistakes
(Geewax devotes a chapter to it in *API Design Patterns*).

## The distinction

<Compare a="Protective rate limit" b="Contractual quota">
<>
**Defends the system.** Internal capacity protection. Limits are
chosen by the operator. Crossing one is a hint to back off; it's
expected occasionally. Returns 429.

Examples: "no more than 100 req/sec/IP," "no more than 1 query/sec
per user against `/reports`."
</>
<>
**Defines what the customer paid for.** Part of the product
contract. Limits are tied to a plan or agreement. Crossing one is
the customer's problem — they need to upgrade. Returns 402 or
403 (or 429 with billing context).

Examples: "Free tier: 1 000 API calls/month," "Pro tier: 100 000
events/day."
</>
</Compare>

Same mechanism (counter + reject), very different meaning:

- Rate limit hit → "please retry in 30 seconds."
- Quota hit → "you're out for this billing period; upgrade or wait."

The user experience must reflect the difference. Saying "too many
requests" to a paying customer who hit their monthly quota is a
support ticket waiting to happen.

## Why both exist

Without rate limits, one buggy customer can take down the system.
Without quotas, the business can't price tiered plans. Both layers
of protection are needed:

```
[client]
   ↓
[rate limit: 1000 req/sec/key]     ← protects the system; rejects bursts
   ↓
[quota check: 100 000 req/month]   ← enforces business contract
   ↓
[service]
```

A request can be:

- Accepted (under both).
- Rate-limited (over 1000/sec; can retry in seconds).
- Over quota (over 100k/month; can retry next month, or upgrade).

Both go to "rejected," but the meaning to the customer is different.

## Quota mechanics

Quotas are slower and stateful:

<Cheatsheet>
| Property | Rate limit | Quota |
|---|---|---|
| Time window | Seconds to minutes | Days to months |
| Reset | Automatic, frequent | Aligned to billing cycle |
| Counter location | Hot path (Redis) | Slower path (DB / billing system) |
| Visibility | Mostly internal | Surfaced in customer dashboard |
| Per-key cost | Small (~1 KB hot) | Variable; aggregated nightly OK |
| Failure cost | Rare, retry-able | Customer-facing; impacts revenue |
| Status code | 429 | 402 Payment Required, or 429 with billing context |
</Cheatsheet>

Quotas can usually tolerate **eventual consistency** — being a few
seconds behind in counting requests is fine, as long as the billing
period total is correct. Rate limits cannot — they must be enforced
in milliseconds.

A common architecture:

```
- Hot path: fast in-memory increment per request.
- Periodic flush: roll up local counts to a central billing service.
- Customer dashboard: queries billing service.
- Hard cap: when central count exceeds quota, push a flag back to
  the hot path: "deny this key."
```

This avoids hitting the billing system on every request.

## Quota policies: hard vs soft

<Compare a="Hard quota" b="Soft quota">
<>
At the limit, requests are **rejected**. Customer must upgrade or
wait. Predictable cost ceiling, painful user experience.
</>
<>
At the limit, requests **succeed but bill at overage rate**.
Higher revenue, can surprise customers with bills. Common in cloud
APIs.
</>
</Compare>

Hybrid: soft quota up to a multiplier, hard quota above that. AWS-
style: "100 GB included; pay per GB up to 10 TB; refuse beyond."

The UX expectation matters:

- Consumer SaaS: hard quota is friendlier ("you're out; here's how
  to upgrade") than a surprise bill.
- Enterprise: soft quota with overage is normal; customers expect
  burst tolerance.

Communicate the policy clearly. Surprise bills generate support
tickets and churn.

## Throttling: the mechanism, not the contract

"Throttling" is what happens when limits are crossed. Two flavors:

### Reject

```
HTTP/1.1 429 Too Many Requests
Retry-After: 60
{ "error": "rate_limit_exceeded", "retry_after": 60 }
```

Or, for quota:

```
HTTP/1.1 429 Too Many Requests
X-Quota-Resource: api_calls
X-Quota-Limit: 100000
X-Quota-Remaining: 0
X-Quota-Reset: 2026-06-01T00:00:00Z
{ "error": "quota_exceeded", "upgrade_url": "https://example.com/billing" }
```

The headers tell the client (and the customer support team) exactly
what was hit and when it resets.

### Delay / queue

The request waits until capacity is available, then is served. Used
for:

- Internal traffic with known senders (queue consumers).
- Batch operations where latency is tolerated.

Doesn't fit user-facing APIs — clients with their own timeouts will
abandon, and held connections cost server resources. Default to
reject.

## Tier shapes

Common pricing-tier shapes, from Continuous API Management:

```
Tier         Rate limit       Quota          Concurrency
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Free         10 req/sec       1 000/month    1
Developer    100 req/sec      100 000/month  5
Pro          1 000 req/sec    1 000 000/mo   25
Enterprise   negotiated       negotiated     negotiated
```

Each tier has all three axes: rate (per second), quota (per period),
concurrency (in flight). Different tiers expose different axes;
real customers care about different ones (rate for chatty clients,
quota for monthly users, concurrency for batch jobs).

Pricing on a single axis is brittle: "10 000 calls/month" doesn't
prevent 10 000 calls in one second from breaking the system.

## Per-endpoint differentiation

Not all endpoints are equal. A search endpoint is 100× more expensive
than a "get user." A naïve flat limit (100 req/sec for everything)
either over-protects cheap endpoints or under-protects expensive
ones.

Two common approaches:

### Cost-weighted limits

Each endpoint has a "cost" (in units); the limit is per-units, not
per-request:

```
GET /users           = 1 unit
GET /search?q=...    = 10 units
POST /reports/run    = 100 units
```

Quota: "1 000 units/sec." Search effectively limited to 100/sec;
report generation to 10/sec. Fair to the system.

### Per-endpoint limits

Separate counters per endpoint:

```
/search:   10/sec/key
/users:    1000/sec/key
/reports:  1/sec/key
```

Easier to communicate, easier to tune individually, more counters
to operate. Common in mature APIs.

GraphQL has its own version of this: **query cost analysis**, where
the cost of a query is computed from its shape (depth × breadth ×
known per-field costs).

## Failure modes

### Hard quota without warning

Customer hits 100% of their monthly quota silently. Production
breaks. Customer is furious.

The fix: warn at 80%, 90%, 95%, 100%. Email, dashboard, response
headers. Quotas should never surprise a paying customer.

### Quota counters drift

The hot path increments locally; the billing system aggregates
nightly. By month end, the totals don't match. Customer disputes.

The fix: reconcile daily; treat the billing system as source of
truth for invoices, the hot path as source of truth for enforcement;
log discrepancies and resolve them.

### Quota reset confusion

"100 000/month" — does that reset on the 1st of the month, or
30 days from signup? Billing-period-aligned is normal but must be
documented. Customers will plan their batch jobs around resets.

### Throttling expensive operations cheaply

A 1 req/sec rate limit lets a customer issue 1 expensive report per
second, costing the system 100 cheap-request-equivalents. The
limit was the wrong unit. Use cost-weighted or per-endpoint limits.

### Throttling internal traffic by accident

Your own service-to-service calls hit the public rate limiter and
get 429'd. Internal traffic should have its own path (different
key, different rate-limit policy, or a separate endpoint).

### Confusing 429 and 402

Returning 429 "rate limited" when the customer is actually out of
their plan's quota. They retry. They still get 429. They don't
understand they need to upgrade. The fix: when quota is the cause,
say so in the response body and surface upgrade links.

## Communication

Quotas are a product feature, not just an infra concern:

- Customer dashboard shows current usage and remaining quota.
- Response headers report quota state on every successful call
  (cheap for the customer to track).
- Billing emails warn at 50%/75%/90%/100%.
- Documentation explains rate limit vs quota vs concurrency
  distinctly.

The infra implementation is the easy part. The customer-experience
work — communicating limits, warnings, upgrade paths — is where most
teams underinvest.

<Callout variant="info">
Rate limit answers "is the system in danger?" Quota answers "did
the customer pay for this much?" They should give different errors,
different docs, and different recovery instructions. Conflating them
is the most common cause of confused customers and lost revenue.
</Callout>

## In summary

- Rate limit defends the system; quota defines the customer
  contract. Same mechanism, different meaning.
- Rate limits live in the hot path (Redis); quotas can be eventually
  consistent (billing system).
- Hard quotas predict cost; soft quotas with overage maximize
  revenue; choose by audience.
- Tier on multiple axes: rate, quota, concurrency. A single axis is
  brittle.
- Use cost-weighted or per-endpoint limits when endpoints differ
  greatly in expense (search vs. get).
- Communicate quota state proactively (warnings, dashboard, response
  headers). Surprise bills churn customers.
- Use distinct error codes and messaging for rate vs. quota — don't
  conflate.
