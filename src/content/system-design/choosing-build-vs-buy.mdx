# How to choose: build vs buy vs managed

For any non-trivial system component, you have three options:
build from scratch, buy a product (OSS or commercial), or
consume a managed service (cloud-hosted).

The right answer is usually managed, occasionally bought,
rarely built.

## The honest cost of "build"

Engineers underestimate the cost of building infrastructure
by ~10×. The initial implementation is the cheapest part.

You also commit to:

- **Ongoing operations.** On-call. Tuning. Capacity planning.
  Upgrades.
- **Feature parity.** Users will want what the commercial
  product has.
- **Security patches.** The dependency chain you took on now
  needs patching forever.
- **Migration cost.** When the homegrown thing finally dies,
  someone has to move off it.

A "month of work" to build a message broker is 1 year of
production operations to learn what RabbitMQ already knows.

## When to build

- **The component is your core product differentiator.**
  Google built MapReduce; Netflix built ChaosMonkey;
  Stripe built their billing engine. These were existential
  to their business.
- **No vendor or OSS matches your specific requirements.**
  Truly unique workload, not "I want it slightly different."
- **You have an SRE team that can carry the ops burden.**
- **The total cost of buying / using managed would be
  prohibitive at your scale.** Common in hyperscalers; rare
  elsewhere.

## When to buy (commercial or OSS)

- **A mature product fits your needs reasonably well.** Don't
  let "we'd use only 80%" stop you.
- **You want to self-host for compliance or cost reasons.**
- **You have ops capacity but not invention capacity.**

For OSS like Postgres, Kafka, Redis, you take on
operating-it cost (provisioning, upgrades, monitoring, scaling)
in exchange for no per-feature license cost.

## When to use a managed service

- **The component is undifferentiated.** Your business doesn't
  care which database engine; the schema is what matters.
- **Operating it is not your core competence.** You're a
  fintech, not a database company.
- **Time-to-value matters.** Spinning up RDS takes 10 minutes;
  installing and tuning Postgres takes weeks.
- **The fully-loaded cost of self-hosting (ops time, on-call
  burden, dropped balls) is higher than the managed bill.**

For most companies, the answer is managed, every time.

Almost every row above has a "we built it ourselves" team
that regrets it.

## The fully-loaded cost calculation

A fair comparison includes:

- **Engineering time.** Senior engineer hours at fully-loaded
  cost (~$200/hr in many companies). A 6-month build = $200K
  in time alone.
- **Opportunity cost.** Those engineers could have built
  product features.
- **On-call burden.** A pager going off at 3 AM has measurable
  burnout cost.
- **Infrastructure cost** of self-hosting (instances,
  networking, storage).
- **Migration cost** when you eventually move.

vs.

- **Managed service bill.** Often $10K–$100K/year for
  meaningful components.

The "build it ourselves to save money" calculation almost
never wins when honest costs are tallied. The exceptions are
hyperscalers who write off the build cost over millions of
internal users.

## When managed bites you

- **Vendor lock-in.** Migrating off DynamoDB is months of
  work. Some managed services are stickier than others.
- **Cost surprises at scale.** S3 egress, Datadog
  per-host costs, Cloud SQL on a hot day — bills can spike.
- **Limited customization.** "We need PostgreSQL with a custom
  index extension" — RDS doesn't allow that.
- **Dependency on the vendor's reliability.** If S3 goes down,
  you go down. (See `multi-region-architecture` for
  mitigation strategies.)

## Hybrid: managed for the common case, build for the edge

A common shape:

- **Managed Postgres** for the main app.
- **Self-hosted ClickHouse** for analytics (no equivalent
  managed at acceptable cost).
- **Managed Kafka** (MSK / Confluent) for streaming.
- **Build a thin custom layer** for your domain-specific
  routing.

Use managed for everything that's well-served; build
selectively where the differentiation lives.

## Decision questions

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Does this differentiate our business?">
If yes → consider build. If no → managed or buy.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Is a mature product close enough?">
If yes → buy / managed. Stop trying to be special.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Can our team operate this in production for the next 5 years?">
If no → managed. The ops cost will eat you.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="What's the lock-in cost if managed becomes unviable?">
High lock-in (Lambda, Aurora-specific features) is a real
tax. Plan an exit path.
</Step>
</Steps>

## In summary

- Default to managed services for undifferentiated
  components.
- Buy when self-hosting OSS gives you what you need and ops
  capacity is available.
- Build only for components that are core differentiators or
  where no alternative exists.
- Always include fully-loaded engineering, on-call, and
  opportunity costs in the comparison.
- Hybrid is fine: managed for common, build for the
  differentiating edges.
- Watch for managed-service lock-in and cost surprises.
