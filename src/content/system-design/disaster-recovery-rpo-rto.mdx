# Disaster recovery: RPO and RTO

Two numbers define your disaster-recovery strategy:

- **RPO (Recovery Point Objective)**: how much **data** are
  you willing to lose? Measured in time. "Up to 1 hour of
  data" means anything written in the last hour before the
  outage might be gone.
- **RTO (Recovery Time Objective)**: how long can you be
  **down**? "RTO 30 minutes" means service must be restored
  within 30 minutes of disaster.

These numbers shape every disaster-recovery decision: which
replication, which backup strategy, which failover automation.

## The RPO/RTO spectrum

<Cheatsheet>
| RPO | RTO | Strategy | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 (no loss) | seconds | Synchronous replication + auto-failover | Very high |
| seconds | minutes | Async replication + automated failover | High |
| minutes | hour | Async replication + manual failover | Medium |
| hours | hours | Daily backup + restore | Low |
| 24 hours | days | Off-site backup + rebuild from scratch | Very low |
</Cheatsheet>

Tighter RPO/RTO costs exponentially more. Spending $1M to
move from RPO=10min to RPO=10sec may be irrational if a
"10-minute loss" affects nothing the business cares about.

## Disaster scenarios

What's a "disaster"?

- **Single host failure**: 1 server dies. RPO ~0 if you have
  HA replication.
- **AZ failure**: one availability zone offline. Multi-AZ
  handles this transparently.
- **Region failure**: an entire AWS region goes down. Whole
  product affected unless multi-region.
- **Account compromise**: attacker has admin access. Backups
  in a separate account help.
- **Data corruption**: a bug wrote bad data; replication
  faithfully copied it. Point-in-time recovery needed.
- **Ransomware**: attacker encrypts everything. Offline
  backups + immutable storage are the answer.

Each scenario has different RPO/RTO impact and different
mitigations.

## Backup strategies

### Full backup

Periodic complete copy. Simple; large. Restore: copy the
whole thing back.

Used as a baseline; combined with incrementals.

### Incremental backup

Only changes since the last backup. Smaller, faster.
Restore: apply full + all increments. Slower restore but
faster backup.

### Continuous backup (CDP)

Stream changes to a backup store in real time. RPO approaches
zero. Cost is real-time write amplification.

Modern cloud databases offer continuous backup (Aurora, S3
versioning, etc.).

### Point-in-time recovery (PITR)

Combination: a base backup + WAL/binlog from that point
forward. Restore to any specific timestamp.

Standard for SQL databases.

## The 3-2-1 rule

- **3 copies of every important piece of data.**
- **2 different media or storage types.**
- **1 copy off-site.**

If your "off-site" is the same cloud provider in a different
region, an account-wide compromise (credentials leaked) still
takes both. True off-site means a different provider or
physical media.

## Backups must be tested

A backup you've never restored is a backup you can't trust.
Test:

- **Monthly**: restore to a staging environment from
  production backups. Compare row counts, run smoke tests.
- **Quarterly**: full DR exercise — simulate region loss,
  practice failover, time it.
- **Annually**: cross-team game day with the full runbook.

Documented in your DR procedures. Many outages have been
made worse by backups that didn't actually work.

## Failover automation

For low RTO, failover must be **automated**:

- Detection: who decides it's time to fail over?
- Decision: how is the human confirmation gated (if any)?
- Execution: the steps to switch traffic to the DR target.

Pitfalls:

- **Trigger-happy automation**: failed over for a network
  blip; data loss from in-flight writes.
- **Manual failover too slow**: humans take 30 min to decide
  when SLO says 5 min.
- **Bad runbook**: steps are stale; can't execute under
  pressure.

The right balance varies by RTO. RTO 5 minutes → full
automation. RTO 4 hours → automated detection, human
decision.

## Practice: chaos engineering

Inject the failures you've designed for. Make sure the system
behaves as expected.

- Kill an AZ. Does multi-AZ work?
- Kill the primary region. Does failover happen?
- Restore from backup. Is the data complete?

(See chaos-engineering.)

## RPO of zero is expensive

Sync replication across regions means every commit pays
cross-region round-trip. For a typical OLTP workload, that's
30-200ms per write.

Most teams don't actually need RPO=0. RPO of 30 seconds (the
async lag) is usually acceptable for everything except
financial-trade settlement.

## Cost vs benefit

A small SaaS doing $100K/year of revenue probably can't
justify multi-region with auto-failover. The math:

- Cost of MRA: $50K/year extra infra + 1 engineer-month/year
  to operate.
- Benefit: avoid downtime costing... maybe $10K total
  revenue impact per year.

Don't overinvest. Match RPO/RTO to actual business impact.

## In summary

- RPO: how much data can you lose. RTO: how long can you be
  down.
- Tighter values cost exponentially more.
- Strategies: continuous backup (low RPO), point-in-time
  recovery, full + incremental, off-site.
- 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 media types, 1 off-site.
- Test backups regularly — untested backups fail when needed.
- Automate failover for low RTO; human decision for higher.
- Don't overinvest — RPO=0 is rarely the right target.
