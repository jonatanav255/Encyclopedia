# On-call and incident response

"On-call" is the human contract that turns monitoring into
recovery. Pages don't fix themselves. The shape of how teams
respond — who's on call, what they do when paged, how they
learn — is as much engineering as the code itself.

This page maps the canonical Google SRE / SRE Workbook playbook:
rotations, runbooks, severity, incident commander, blameless
postmortems. The vocabulary is consistent across companies of
all sizes; the implementation isn't.

## The rotation

A pager that wakes one person on a schedule. The minimum
shape:

<Cheatsheet>
| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Primary | The person paged first |
| Secondary | Pages if primary doesn't ack within N minutes |
| Manager escalation | Pages if both don't ack within M minutes |
| Schedule | Weekly is common; shorter for new rotations |
| Compensation | Time-off-in-lieu, pay, or both — never "implicit" |

</Cheatsheet>

The single most important property: **bounded sleep loss per
person**. The SRE book recommends no more than two paging
incidents per 12-hour shift on average. More than that and the
job is unsustainable; people quit, the rotation falls apart, the
remaining people get worse coverage.

If pager load exceeds budget for two consecutive weeks, **stop
shipping features and fix the alerts and the underlying
reliability** until the load comes down. This is policy, not a
suggestion — the alternative is burnout-driven attrition.

## Severity levels

A standardized severity vocabulary. The exact names vary;
what matters is everyone uses the same ones.

<Cheatsheet>
| Sev | What | Response |
|---|---|---|
| SEV-1 | Full or near-full customer outage, data loss in progress | All hands, immediate page, incident commander assigned, status page updated |
| SEV-2 | Significant degradation for many users | Page on-call, declare incident, target restore within hours |
| SEV-3 | Limited impact (one region, one feature, slow degradation) | Page on-call, no incident commander, mostly handled by on-call |
| SEV-4 | Negligible customer impact, but worth tracking | Ticket, not a page |

</Cheatsheet>

The severity is **declared at incident open** and can be
**upgraded or downgraded** as understanding changes. A
seemingly small alert turns into a SEV-1 when you discover it's
data corruption; a scary alert downgrades to SEV-3 when you
discover only 0.1% of users are affected.

## Runbooks

A runbook is the playbook for a specific paging alert: "the
page fires — here's what to do."

A good runbook contains:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="What the alert means.">
"Checkout latency p99 above 500ms for 5 minutes." In plain
words, what user pain this maps to.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Where to look first.">
The dashboard link. The trace explorer query. The log filter.
Specific URLs, not "go look at observability."
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Known causes.">
"If the payment dashboard shows DB pool saturation, check the
last deploy for connection-pool changes." Past root causes,
each with a quick check.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Mitigation steps.">
Not the fix — the **mitigation**. "Roll back the last deploy."
"Increase pool size to 100 in feature flag X." "Failover to
the read replica." Quick reversible steps that stop the
bleeding.
</Step>
<Step n={5} title="Escalation path.">
"If the above doesn't help in 15 minutes, page the database
team."
</Step>
</Steps>

Runbooks are **living documents**. After every incident, update
the runbook with what you learned. If you didn't, the next
person paged at 3am will rediscover the same thing.

A page without a runbook is broken. See alerting design.

## The incident commander role

For SEV-1 and SEV-2, the **incident commander (IC)** is the
person who owns the incident — not the person who fixes it.
Their job:

- Declare and label the incident.
- Coordinate communication — who's doing what, no two people
  step on each other.
- Decide on mitigations (with input from technical responders).
- Update status pages and stakeholders.
- Call the incident resolved.
- Make sure the postmortem happens.

The IC is **explicitly not technical lead** during the incident.
The IC may even hand off the keyboard to other responders and
focus entirely on coordination. The technical lead deep-dives;
the IC keeps the whole operation coherent.

Big systems train ICs separately. ICs come from any role —
engineer, manager, SRE. The skill is calm coordination under
pressure, not technical depth.

The "Managing Incidents" chapter of the SRE book describes the
model in detail; it's adapted from emergency-response
"incident command system" (ICS) used by fire departments.

## The incident channel

A single chat channel per incident, opened at declaration,
closed at resolution. Everything happens there:

- Timeline updates.
- Hypotheses and data.
- Decisions ("we're rolling back").
- Hand-offs ("Alice is taking the keyboard for the next 30 min").

This channel **is** the incident log. The postmortem reads it
back. Side conversations in DMs are forbidden — they fracture
context.

Many teams add an additional "broadcast" channel for
stakeholders (support, comms, leadership) — read-only summaries
posted by the IC, no debate. The technical work stays in the
working channel.

## The five-step response

A pattern that fits almost every incident:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Acknowledge.">
Click the page. The escalation timer stops. The team knows
someone is on it.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Triage.">
What's the scope? Which users? Which services? What is the
worst-case if we do nothing? Declare severity.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Mitigate.">
Stop the bleeding. Rollback. Failover. Feature-flag off. **Do
not fix the root cause** in the middle of the fire — restore
service first.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Diagnose.">
With service restored (or stable), figure out **what
happened**. Logs, traces, dashboards, recent changes.
</Step>
<Step n={5} title="Resolve.">
The permanent fix, possibly deferred. Close the incident.
Update communications. Schedule the postmortem.
</Step>
</Steps>

The order is critical. **Mitigate before diagnose**. A common
mistake — especially among senior engineers who can "see" the
bug — is to try to fix the root cause directly. This extends
the outage. Roll back first; fix later in calmer code review.

## Blameless postmortems

Within a few days of any SEV-1 or SEV-2 (and many SEV-3s), a
written postmortem. The shape:

- **Summary** — what happened, in two paragraphs.
- **Impact** — duration, users affected, SLO budget consumed,
  data lost.
- **Timeline** — minute-by-minute. Pulled from the incident
  channel.
- **Root cause** — usually multi-layer ("the deploy + the
  monitoring gap + the runbook error" each contributed).
- **What went well** — yes, write this part. Reinforce the
  things to keep doing.
- **What went poorly** — concrete, not personal.
- **Action items** — owned, with deadlines, prioritized.

The **blameless** part is the heart. The premise: every
incident is a system failure, not an individual failure.
Whoever pushed the bad commit was the trigger; the
**system that let the bad commit reach prod** is the root
cause. Reviews focus on the system, not the person.

This isn't soft. A genuinely blameless postmortem produces
**more** systemic fixes than a blame-driven one because people
freely describe what they actually did, which is the data you
need.

<Callout variant="info">
*Building Secure and Reliable Systems* (Google) and the SRE book
both insist: blameless postmortems are an institutional choice
and protected by culture. The first time someone gets punished
for what they wrote in a postmortem, the next ten postmortems
become useless theatre.
</Callout>

## Action items must close

A list of action items that no one ever does is worse than no
list — it teaches the team that postmortems are theatre.
Closure requires:

- An **owner**, named, not "team X."
- A **deadline**, in calendar weeks.
- A **priority**: P0 closes before next on-call shift; P2 closes
  this quarter.
- A **tracking mechanism**: tickets in the system the team uses
  for everything else.

Postmortem action items get the same priority as features. If
the org consistently deprioritizes them, the on-call rotation
slowly degrades.

## Healthy vs unhealthy signs

Healthy rotation: people complain about the on-call duties,
but no one is quitting over them. SEV-1s are rare (months
apart). Postmortems happen within a week. Action items close.

Unhealthy rotation: chronic SEV-1s, no postmortems for SEV-3s,
the "primary" is always paged twice per shift, action items
have a backlog older than a quarter. This is the noisy-pager
death spiral — see alerting design — and the response is the
same: stop shipping features, fix the things.

## In summary

- A rotation that paged more than ~2x per shift on average is
  burning the team down.
- Severity is a small fixed vocabulary; declare and adjust.
- Every paging alert has a runbook.
- An incident commander coordinates SEV-1/2 incidents; they're
  not the technical fixer.
- Mitigate before diagnose. Stop the bleeding first.
- Blameless postmortems within a week; action items get real
  ownership and deadlines.
- The culture is upstream of the tooling.
