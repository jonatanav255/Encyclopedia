# Papers worth reading

A book can give you a tour. A paper gives you the primary
source — the actual people who solved the actual problem,
writing for an audience of peers, with the precision that
implies. Every system you'll touch in production traces back
to one of these.

Fifteen papers. If you internalize even ten of them, you'll
know more about distributed systems than 90% of people who say
they do.

## Consensus and coordination

The mechanical core of distributed systems: how a group of
unreliable machines agrees on what happened.

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="Paxos Made Simple"
  authors="Leslie Lamport"
  year="2001"
  source="Microsoft Research"
  url="https://lamport.azurewebsites.net/pubs/paxos-simple.pdf"
>
Lamport's attempt to explain Paxos in plain English after his
1989 "Part-Time Parliament" parody paper was rejected for
being too playful. Still the canonical short introduction.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="In Search of an Understandable Consensus Algorithm"
  authors="Diego Ongaro & John Ousterhout"
  year="2014"
  source="USENIX ATC"
  url="https://raft.github.io/raft.pdf"
>
The Raft paper. Designed specifically to be easier to teach
and implement than Paxos. Etcd, Consul, CockroachDB, TiKV,
and others use Raft. Read this before you read any Raft
implementation.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="The Chubby lock service for loosely-coupled distributed systems"
  authors="Mike Burrows"
  year="2006"
  source="OSDI"
  url="https://research.google/pubs/the-chubby-lock-service-for-loosely-coupled-distributed-systems/"
>
Google's coarse-grained lock service. The paper introduced
the now-standard pattern: a small Paxos-replicated
coordination service that other systems lean on for leader
election and metadata.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="ZooKeeper: Wait-free coordination for Internet-scale systems"
  authors="Hunt, Konar, Junqueira & Reed"
  year="2010"
  source="USENIX ATC"
  url="https://www.usenix.org/legacy/event/usenix10/tech/full_papers/Hunt.pdf"
>
The open-source descendant of Chubby. Foundational for
Kafka, Hadoop, HBase and many others. Wait-free reads, FIFO
client ordering, atomic broadcast.
</Reference>

## Storage at scale

Every modern database owes something to one of these.

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="The Google File System"
  authors="Ghemawat, Gobioff & Leung"
  year="2003"
  source="SOSP"
  url="https://research.google/pubs/the-google-file-system/"
>
The blueprint that HDFS copied. Master + chunkservers,
append-mostly workloads, replication for durability,
checksums for integrity. The patterns are everywhere.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="Bigtable: A Distributed Storage System for Structured Data"
  authors="Chang et al."
  year="2006"
  source="OSDI"
  url="https://research.google/pubs/bigtable-a-distributed-storage-system-for-structured-data/"
>
The wide-column store. Sorted string tables (SSTables),
tablets, Chubby for metadata. Direct ancestor of HBase,
Cassandra's storage engine, and the LSM-tree pattern in
modern databases.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="Dynamo: Amazon's Highly Available Key-value Store"
  authors="DeCandia et al."
  year="2007"
  source="SOSP"
  url="https://www.allthingsdistributed.com/files/amazon-dynamo-sosp2007.pdf"
>
The leaderless, eventually-consistent design. Consistent
hashing, vector clocks, sloppy quorums, hinted handoff. The
intellectual ancestor of Cassandra, Riak, Voldemort and the
"AP" half of the CAP debate.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="Cassandra - A Decentralized Structured Storage System"
  authors="Avinash Lakshman & Prashant Malik"
  year="2009"
  source="LADIS"
  url="https://www.cs.cornell.edu/projects/ladis2009/papers/lakshman-ladis2009.pdf"
>
Facebook's Dynamo-meets-Bigtable hybrid. The paper that
launched the wide-column AP database. Read alongside
Dynamo — Cassandra borrows the gossip and partitioning,
adds the SSTable storage engine.
</Reference>

## Time, ordering and consistency

The papers that explain why distributed systems are hard.

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="Spanner: Google's Globally-Distributed Database"
  authors="Corbett et al."
  year="2012"
  source="OSDI"
  url="https://research.google/pubs/spanner-googles-globally-distributed-database-2/"
>
TrueTime, commit-wait, externally consistent transactions
across continents. The paper that demonstrates strict
serializability at global scale is achievable — given GPS
antennas and atomic clocks. Defining work on bounding
clock uncertainty.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="Calvin: Fast Distributed Transactions for Partitioned Database Systems"
  authors="Thomson et al."
  year="2012"
  source="SIGMOD"
  url="http://cs.yale.edu/homes/thomson/publications/calvin-sigmod12.pdf"
>
The deterministic alternative to Spanner. Sequence
transactions globally first, then execute deterministically;
no two-phase commit needed. FaunaDB descends from this line
of work.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="Brewer's Conjecture and the Feasibility of Consistent, Available, Partition-Tolerant Web Services"
  authors="Seth Gilbert & Nancy Lynch"
  year="2002"
  source="ACM SIGACT News"
  url="https://users.ece.cmu.edu/~adrian/731-sp04/readings/GL-cap.pdf"
>
The formal proof of CAP. Read this once and stop arguing
about whether your system is "CP" or "AP" — those labels
are network-partition-conditioned, not steady-state
properties.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="Impossibility of Distributed Consensus with One Faulty Process"
  authors="Fischer, Lynch & Paterson"
  year="1985"
  source="JACM"
  url="https://groups.csail.mit.edu/tds/papers/Lynch/jacm85.pdf"
>
FLP. In a fully asynchronous network with even one faulty
node, no deterministic consensus protocol can guarantee
both safety and liveness. Every real consensus algorithm
sidesteps this with timeouts or randomization.
</Reference>

## Scale and failure

The papers about operating at the size where physics starts
to hurt.

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="MapReduce: Simplified Data Processing on Large Clusters"
  authors="Jeffrey Dean & Sanjay Ghemawat"
  year="2004"
  source="OSDI"
  url="https://research.google/pubs/mapreduce-simplified-data-processing-on-large-clusters/"
>
The paper that launched a decade of big-data systems
(Hadoop, Spark, Flink owe their lineage here). Even if you
never write a MapReduce job, the patterns — shuffle, worker
failure, speculative execution — show up everywhere.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="paper"
  title="The Tail at Scale"
  authors="Jeffrey Dean & Luiz André Barroso"
  year="2013"
  source="CACM"
  url="https://research.google/pubs/the-tail-at-scale/"
>
Why p99 latency dominates user experience when requests
fan out. Hedged requests, tied requests, micro-partitioning.
The vocabulary the rest of the industry now uses for
tail-latency work.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="talk"
  title="Designs, Lessons and Advice from Building Large Distributed Systems"
  authors="Jeff Dean"
  year="2009"
  source="LADIS keynote / Stanford talk"
  url="https://static.googleusercontent.com/media/research.google.com/en//people/jeff/stanford-295-talk.pdf"
>
The slides where "numbers everyone should know" comes from
— L1 0.5 ns, RAM 100 ns, datacenter round-trip 500 µs, etc.
Not a paper, but quoted more than most papers.
</Reference>

## How to read these

Don't try to read all 15 cold. A practical order:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Start with Dean's talk and Tail at Scale.">
Both are short and reframe how you think about latency. Half
an hour each.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Then Dynamo and Raft.">
Read these together. Dynamo for the leaderless / eventual
side; Raft for the consensus side. Most modern systems sit
on the axis between them.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Then GFS, Bigtable, Spanner — in that order.">
The Google storage trilogy. Each builds on the previous.
After all three you understand the storage layer of every
hyperscaler.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="CAP and FLP last.">
They're foundational but more enjoyable once you've seen the
systems they constrain.
</Step>
</Steps>

The remaining papers — Chubby, ZooKeeper, MapReduce, Calvin,
Cassandra, Paxos — fill in detail once the core picture is in
your head.

<Callout variant="info">
Every paper here is freely available. If a URL stops
resolving, search the title — most are mirrored on Google
Scholar, the author's homepage, and ACM's open-access copy.
</Callout>

## In summary

- These 15 papers are the canonical primary sources for
  distributed systems. Every textbook you've read draws
  from them.
- Read Dean's talk and Tail at Scale first; CAP and FLP last.
- Each is cited from the relevant encyclopedia page so you
  can jump to the primary source as you read.
