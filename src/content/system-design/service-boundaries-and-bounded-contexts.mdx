# Service boundaries and bounded contexts

The cuts you make between services determine whether microservices
pay off or sink the team. **Bounded context** from Evans' *Domain-
Driven Design* is the sharpest tool for finding cuts that don't
generate chatty cross-service calls.

The rule: a service boundary should be a **language boundary** — a
point where the same word means different things on either side.

## Why "by entity" is wrong

The most common mistake is one service per entity: `UserService`,
`OrderService`, `ProductService`, `CartService`. It feels natural
because the database has those tables. It's wrong because:

- A single user request usually touches every one.
- Joins move from SQL (cheap) to RPC (expensive, fallible).
- Transactions span every service.
- "Show me my orders" becomes a fan-out across N services.

The boundary is **data-shaped**, not **capability-shaped**. You end
up with a distributed monolith — see the page of that name.

## Bounded context: the right unit

A **bounded context** is a section of the domain where:

- Terms mean one specific thing.
- One team owns the model.
- Internal consistency is required and achievable.

The same word — "Order" — means different things in different
contexts:

<Cheatsheet>
| Context | What "Order" means |
|---|---|
| Checkout | Cart contents, pricing, payment intent, draft status. |
| Fulfilment | Pick list, pack instructions, ship address, carrier. |
| Billing | Invoice line items, tax, currency, receipt. |
| Customer support | Status, refund history, communication log. |
| Analytics | A row in a fact table with dimensions and measures. |
</Cheatsheet>

You don't share one Order model across all five — you let each
context have its own. The translation between contexts is **explicit**
and happens at the boundary.

## How to find boundaries

Vernon (*Implementing DDD*) and Evans both recommend an event-storming
approach. The mechanics:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Map the domain events.">
Walk through the business as a sequence of past-tense events:
"OrderPlaced," "PaymentAuthorized," "ShipmentDispatched,"
"InvoiceGenerated." Don't think about services yet.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Group events by which team/process owns them.">
Events that belong to the same process (and the same vocabulary)
form a candidate context. The handover between contexts is usually
a single event flowing out.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Test each candidate boundary.">
For a few real user journeys, count the cross-context calls. If a
single journey crosses a boundary back and forth 5 times, the
boundary is wrong.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Define the contract at each boundary.">
Each boundary is a public API: events, commands, or queries.
Everything inside is private. Cross-boundary calls cannot reach into
the other context's database.
</Step>
</Steps>

This is slow work — it's design work, not coding. Skip it and the
boundaries will be defined by accident, in the wrong places.

## Heuristics for good cuts

Newman, Richardson, and Vernon converge on a few rules:

### Cut along team boundaries

A service owned by part of a team is fragile. A team that owns part of
a service is worse. **One service, one team.** Conway's law makes this
non-negotiable — the architecture will reflect the org chart whether
you want it to or not.

### Cut along business capability

"Fraud detection" is a capability. "User table" is not. Capabilities
are stable; entity schemas change every quarter. Service boundaries
drawn around capabilities outlive entity refactors.

### Cut along data-locality

If 95% of an operation's reads come from one context's data, it
belongs in that context. If an operation needs data from 3 contexts
to complete, it's either in the wrong place or designed wrong.

### Cut along change-rate

Modules that change weekly should not be coupled to modules that
change yearly. Different change rates → different services. Same
change rate → probably the same service.

### Cut along volatility

A part of the system that gets rewritten constantly should be cleanly
extractable. Stable foundational parts should be stable, not bleeding
into the volatile ones.

## What good boundaries look like

<Compare a="Good cut" b="Bad cut">
<>
- "Show user their orders" — 1 service call, possibly 0 if cached.
- Schema migration in one service doesn't ripple.
- A new feature is mostly within one service.
- Cross-service calls are async events, not synchronous RPC chains.
- Each service can be understood by one team end-to-end.
</>
<>
- "Show user their orders" — 5 service calls fanned out.
- Schema migration coordinates 4 services and 3 teams.
- A new feature requires touching 6 services.
- Sync RPC chains 4 services deep on the hot path.
- No single team can explain what the system does.
</>
</Compare>

The smell of a wrong cut: every change is a multi-service change.

## Context mapping

Once you have contexts, the **relationships between them** matter as
much as the contexts themselves. Evans catalogues these:

<Cheatsheet>
| Relationship | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Shared kernel | Two contexts share a small model. Risky; resist. |
| Customer/supplier | Downstream depends on upstream; upstream considers downstream's needs. |
| Conformist | Downstream accepts upstream's model wholesale. Cheap; coupling-heavy. |
| Anti-corruption layer | Downstream translates upstream's model into its own. Defensive. |
| Open host service | Upstream publishes a public protocol many can consume. |
| Published language | A shared schema/format (events) used between contexts. |
</Cheatsheet>

The most important one in practice is the **anti-corruption layer
(ACL)**. When integrating with a legacy or external system, you
translate at the boundary. Your internal model doesn't catch the
disease of theirs. Without an ACL, you let an external schema dictate
your domain — and you can't refactor anything without breaking the
boundary.

## Smells that the boundary is wrong

```
- Two services always deploy together.
- Service A reads service B's database directly.
- A single API call requires synchronous fan-out to 5+ services.
- A domain term has the same meaning in 4 different services.
- Cross-service transactions are common; sagas are everywhere.
- A bug in service C breaks service A's unrelated feature.
- Half the team's tickets say "needs change in 3 services."
```

Each one points back to the cut, not to the code. Fixing the code
without redrawing the boundary just rearranges the pain.

## Don't extract too early

A bounded context only becomes a service when there's a reason
(team autonomy, scaling, deploy independence). Inside a monolith, a
bounded context is a **module**, not a service. The cut is the same
discipline — the deployment is different.

This is the spirit of the modular monolith: get the boundaries right
first, extract later, only when needed. Newman's *Monolith to
Microservices* spells this out.

<Callout variant="info">
The hardest part of microservices isn't running them. It's drawing
the boundaries. Spend the time on event storming and domain
modeling — the alternative is years of paying for the wrong cuts.
</Callout>

## A practical exercise

Pick a feature in your product. Trace it:

1. Which **domain events** does it produce?
2. Which contexts care about each event?
3. Which contexts must agree synchronously? (Hopefully zero.)
4. Which language/terms are reused across contexts? Are they really
   the same concept, or do they mean different things?

If step 3 has anything other than zero, you have a saga, not a clean
boundary. If step 4 reveals overloaded terms, you've found your real
contexts hiding behind shared vocabulary.

## In summary

- A service boundary is a **language boundary**: a point where the
  same word means different things.
- Don't cut by entity. Cut by **bounded context** — sections of the
  domain with their own model and team.
- Test boundaries against real user journeys: count cross-service
  calls and shared transactions; both should be near zero on the
  hot path.
- Inside a monolith, contexts are modules; extract to services only
  when there's a team or scaling reason.
- Anti-corruption layers protect your model from external schemas;
  always use one at integration points.
