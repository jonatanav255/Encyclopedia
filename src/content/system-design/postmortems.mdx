# Postmortems worth studying

Failure modes pages explain *what can go wrong*. These
postmortems show what *did* go wrong — at companies with
billion-dollar budgets and senior staff who knew what they
were doing.

The pattern: it's almost never one bug. It's a small mistake
plus an unexpected interaction plus a missing safety net.
That's worth internalizing — your systems will fail the same
way.

## Capacity exhaustion and cascading failure

When one component slows down and the rest of the system
helpfully amplifies the problem.

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Summary of the Amazon Kinesis Event in the Northern Virginia (US-EAST-1) Region"
  authors="AWS"
  year="2020"
  source="AWS post-event summary"
  url="https://aws.amazon.com/message/11201/"
>
A new Kinesis front-end fleet hit the OS thread limit;
existing front-ends started failing; recovery required a
careful restart sequence because cache rebuild was
expensive. CloudWatch, Cognito, IoT, and others depended on
Kinesis and went down with it.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Summary of the AWS Service Event in the Northern Virginia (US-EAST-1) Region"
  authors="AWS"
  year="2021"
  source="AWS post-event summary"
  url="https://aws.amazon.com/message/12721/"
>
December 2021. An internal network scaling action triggered
a feedback loop between front-end services and their
internal control plane. Recovery took hours because the
control plane itself was needed to fix the control plane.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Slack's Incident on 2-22-22"
  authors="Laura Nolan et al."
  year="2022"
  source="Slack engineering blog"
  url="https://slack.engineering/slacks-incident-on-2-22-22/"
>
A configuration change increased load on a backend; that
backend's slowdown produced retries; retries produced more
slowdown. Classic retry storm with very honest writing about
what they wish they'd done differently.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Summary of June 8 outage"
  authors="Nick Rockwell"
  year="2021"
  source="Fastly blog"
  url="https://www.fastly.com/blog/summary-of-june-8-outage"
>
A single customer's valid configuration triggered a latent
bug in Fastly's edge software. Half the internet went dark
for an hour. The lesson: blast radius is what your software
can produce, not what your customers usually do.
</Reference>

## Configuration and deployment failures

Most outages are configuration changes, not code bugs.

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Summary of the Amazon S3 Service Disruption in the Northern Virginia (US-EAST-1) Region"
  authors="AWS"
  year="2017"
  source="AWS post-event summary"
  url="https://aws.amazon.com/message/41926/"
>
An engineer's typo in a debugging command removed more
servers than intended. Recovery required restarting
subsystems that hadn't been restarted in years and were
slow to come back up. The AWS service health dashboard
itself depended on S3 — and went down with it.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Cloudflare outage caused by bad software deploy"
  authors="John Graham-Cumming"
  year="2019"
  source="Cloudflare blog"
  url="https://blog.cloudflare.com/cloudflare-outage/"
>
A new WAF rule contained a catastrophic regex. CPU spiked
to 100% on every machine globally within seconds. Required
a manual kill switch to recover. The kill switch existed.
Few WAF teams have one.
</Reference>

<Reference

A network configuration rollout intended to be incremental

## Database and data-loss incidents

The hardest incidents — the ones with permanent consequences.

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Postmortem of database outage of January 31"
  authors="GitLab"
  year="2017"
  source="GitLab blog"
  url="https://about.gitlab.com/blog/2017/02/10/postmortem-of-database-outage-of-january-31/"
>
A tired engineer ran `rm -rf` on the wrong host. Of five
backup mechanisms, none worked. Six hours of data lost.
Now mandatory reading on every "do your backups actually
work" checklist.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="October 21 post-incident analysis"
  authors="Jason Warner"
  year="2018"
  source="GitHub blog"
  url="https://github.blog/2018-10-30-oct21-post-incident-analysis/"
>
A network partition split MySQL clusters between East and
West coast regions. Both sides accepted writes for 43
seconds. Reconciling them took 24 hours of careful manual
work. The classic split-brain story.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Summary of the Amazon DynamoDB Service Disruption and Related Impacts in the US-East Region"
  authors="AWS"
  year="2015"
  source="AWS post-event summary"
  url="https://aws.amazon.com/message/5467D2/"
>
Metadata service under DynamoDB hit a capacity limit; the
resulting cascade brought down a dozen dependent services.
The original "control plane is itself a database" story.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Why we spent the last month eliminating PostgreSQL subtransactions"
  authors="Grzegorz Bizon & Stan Hu"
  year="2021"
  source="GitLab blog"

The kind of debugging that justifies senior salaries.

## Network and protocol failures

The category that's hardest to test before it breaks.

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="A Byzantine failure in the real world"
  authors="Tom Lianza & Chris Snook"
  year="2020"
  source="Cloudflare blog"
  url="https://blog.cloudflare.com/a-byzantine-failure-in-the-real-world/"
>
A switch entered a partial-failure state (LACP/BGP links up,
vPC down) and silently dropped packets from a subset of
peers. Cloudflare's internal etcd cluster saw inconsistent
reachability and produced behavior outside its crash-fault
fault model. The post itself notes this is more precisely an
"omission" failure than a Byzantine one — and that's the
lesson: real production failures often don't fit textbook
fault models cleanly.
</Reference>

<Reference
  kind="postmortem"
  title="Cloudflare System Status — November 2023 control plane incident"
  authors="Cloudflare"
  year="2023"
  source="Cloudflare status page"
  url="https://www.cloudflarestatus.com/incidents/8gmlb2qswffp"
>
Power loss at a primary data center took down Cloudflare's
control plane. Customer traffic kept routing fine (data
plane intact) — dashboards, config changes, and analytics
did not. The blast-radius separation between planes worked
exactly as designed.
</Reference>

## Patterns that show up over and over

Across these 20 incidents:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="The failure is rarely the bug; it's the response.">
Almost every cascading outage starts small and escalates
because retries, dependent services, or auto-scaling made
it worse.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="The control plane is itself a system that can fail.">
S3 2017, AWS 2021, DynamoDB 2015 — the system used to fix
the broken system was itself broken. Plan a recovery path
that doesn't require your own control plane.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Backups need to be tested by actually restoring them.">
GitLab's "five backup mechanisms" all looked fine until
one was needed.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Configuration changes outnumber code bugs as outage causes.">
Treat config changes with the same rigor as code deploys:
canary, blast-radius limits, kill switch.
</Step>
<Step n={5} title="Blast radius is the most under-engineered property.">
"Worst case, what fraction of users are affected?" is the
question every architecture review should ask. If the
answer is "all of them," redesign.
</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout variant="warning">
Reading postmortems is a perishable skill. The vocabulary,
the patterns, the small distinctions between "transient" and
"persistent" — these are easy to forget. Re-read a handful
every six months.
</Callout>
