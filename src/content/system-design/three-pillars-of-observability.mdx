# The three pillars of observability

Observability is the property of a system that lets you answer
**arbitrary questions about its internal state from its external
output** — without shipping new code first. The "three pillars"
framing names the three classical signal types that, together,
make that possible: **logs, metrics, traces**.

The framing is older than the term "observability" itself and is
imperfect — Charity Majors and others argue it understates the
role of **wide structured events** — but every running system in
2026 still emits these three.

## What each pillar answers

<Cheatsheet>
| Pillar | Shape | What it answers | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Logs | Discrete events with context | "What happened in this request?" | High volume, cheap per event |
| Metrics | Numeric time-series | "How is the system behaving in aggregate?" | Tiny, pre-aggregated |
| Traces | Causally-linked spans across services | "Where did the time go?" | Sampled, medium cost |
</Cheatsheet>

Each is great at one thing and terrible at another. You need all
three because no single signal type answers every question.

## Logs

A log is a timestamped event with attached fields. In a healthy
system, every interesting state transition produces one.

```js
log.info('payment.captured', {
  orderId: 'o_123',
  amount: 4999,
  currency: 'USD',
  durationMs: 142,
});
```

What logs are good at:

- **High cardinality**: every field can have unlimited distinct
  values (user IDs, order IDs, error messages).
- **Forensics**: replay what happened in a specific request,
  hours after the fact.
- **Free-form context**: easy to add a new field without changing
  a schema.

What logs are bad at:

- **Aggregation at scale**: counting "errors per second across
  the fleet" by scanning text logs is slow and expensive.
- **Pre-computed dashboards**: querying logs ad-hoc isn't a
  substitute for a metric you graph in real time.

See structured logging — free-form text logs are nearly useless
for anything beyond `grep`.

## Metrics

A metric is a numeric value sampled over time, tagged with a small
set of **low-cardinality** labels.

```js
httpRequests.inc({ route: '/checkout', status: 200 });
httpDuration.observe({ route: '/checkout' }, 0.142);
```

What metrics are good at:

- **Cheap aggregation**: count/sum/percentile over millions of
  events without scanning them.
- **Dashboards and alerts**: graph p99 latency, fire when error
  rate spikes.
- **Long retention**: pre-aggregated numbers compress well — a
  year of 10-second samples is megabytes.

What metrics are bad at:

- **High cardinality**: a label per user ID explodes the storage
  cost. Most TSDBs choke past ~1M unique label combinations per
  metric.
- **Individual investigation**: a p99 spike tells you something
  is slow, not which user or which request.

The cardinality limit is the **fundamental** constraint. If you
need to graph "p99 latency per customer ID" with 10M customers,
metrics are the wrong tool — that's a trace or wide-event
problem.

## Traces

A trace follows a single request across services. Each unit of
work is a **span** with a start time, duration, parent ID, and
attached attributes. Spans share a **trace ID**.

```
trace_id=abc123
  span: api-gateway          [====]      8 ms
  span: order-service        [============]    24 ms
    span: inventory-rpc          [===]         6 ms
    span: payment-rpc            [========]   18 ms
      span: db.charge              [====]      9 ms
```

What traces are good at:

- **Causality across services**: which downstream call ate the
  latency budget?
- **Per-request detail with structure**: the parent/child shape
  is queryable in ways flat logs aren't.
- **Performance debugging**: find the 1% of requests that hit a
  slow path.

What traces are bad at:

- **Whole-fleet aggregation**: traces are usually sampled, so
  rate-based metrics computed from traces are noisy.
- **Storage cost at 100% sampling**: a full trace per request is
  expensive — see sampling strategies.

## Why all three

A motivating example. The on-call alert fires: "p99 checkout
latency above 800ms for 5 minutes." How each signal contributes:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Metrics — the alarm and the scope.">
The dashboard shows p99 jumped on the `/checkout` route 4
minutes ago. p50 is unchanged. Error rate is normal. Throughput
is normal. So the symptom is **tail latency**, not a global
outage.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Traces — find the bottleneck.">
Sample a few slow traces from the last 5 minutes. They all
show the same `payment-service → db.charge` span ballooning
from 10ms to 600ms. The aggregation in metrics told you
**what** is slow; traces told you **where**.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Logs — root cause.">
Pull the structured logs from the payment-service pod handling
those traces. There's a `WARN db.pool.exhausted` message
correlated in time. The pool is starving. Now you know **why**.
</Step>
</Steps>

Metrics found it, traces localized it, logs explained it. With
only one pillar, you'd be guessing at one of those steps.

## Overlap and tension

The pillars overlap more than the framing suggests:

- **Logs can produce metrics** — count log events of a given
  shape. This is how many systems work (Splunk, Loki + recording
  rules). Drawback: you pay log-storage costs to get a number you
  could have emitted directly.
- **Traces can produce metrics** — span counts and durations
  aggregate into RED-method graphs. Drawback: sampled traces
  produce noisy aggregates.
- **Wide events** (Honeycomb-style) try to unify the three: every
  request emits one fat structured event with hundreds of fields,
  and you derive logs, metrics, and traces from queries over
  those events.

The wide-event camp argues the three-pillar split is a historical
artifact of separate storage backends (Elasticsearch for logs,
Prometheus for metrics, Jaeger for traces) and that a single
event store does the job better. There's truth to this — but
metrics' compression and traces' parent/child structure remain
genuinely different shapes.

<Callout variant="note">
Charity Majors, Liz Fong-Jones, and George Miranda's
*Observability Engineering* makes the case that observability is
a property of the **questions you can answer**, not of which
signals you emit. The three pillars are a means; arbitrary-query
debugability is the end.
</Callout>

## What each pillar costs

<Compare a="Cheap to add" b="Expensive at scale">
<>
**Metrics**. A counter is bytes per scrape. Add as many as you
want — until the label cardinality explodes. Dashboards and
alerts come almost free once you have the metric.
</>
<>
**Logs and full-sample traces**. Storage and indexing scale with
request volume. A high-traffic service producing 10 KB of logs
per request at 50K QPS is 500 MB/sec — eight figures a year of
log-storage spend. This is where sampling, retention tiers, and
aggregation live.
</>
</Compare>

## Common mistakes

- **Logging what should be a metric.** Counting `INFO request
  processed` lines to compute QPS is a metric in log clothing.
  Emit a counter.
- **Metric-ing what should be a log.** Tagging a metric with
  `user_id` blows up cardinality. That's a per-request question
  — log it or trace it.
- **Not propagating trace context.** A trace that stops at the
  service boundary because the next service didn't pull the
  headers is useless. See distributed tracing.
- **Treating all three as one budget.** They have different unit
  economics. Sample traces aggressively; keep metrics cheap;
  tier log storage (hot 7d, warm 30d, cold 1y).

## In summary

- **Logs** = "what happened" — discrete events with high-cardinality context.
- **Metrics** = "how it's behaving" — cheap aggregated numbers with low-cardinality labels.
- **Traces** = "where the time went" — causally linked spans across services.
- You need all three because each shines at a question the others answer poorly.
- Wide structured events are a credible unification but don't change the questions.
- Cost differs by 100x across the pillars; budget them separately.
