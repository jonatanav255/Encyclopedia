# Service mesh

A service mesh moves cross-cutting service-to-service concerns —
mTLS, retries, timeouts, traffic shifting, telemetry — **out of
application code** and into a sidecar proxy that lives next to every
service. Istio, Linkerd, Consul Connect, and AWS App Mesh are the
common implementations.

It's powerful and operationally heavy. Most teams adopt it too early.

## What it actually is

Every service pod gets a sidecar (typically **Envoy** or
**linkerd-proxy**) that intercepts all inbound and outbound traffic.
The application talks to localhost; the sidecar handles the network.

```
[app] ↔ [sidecar] ─── network ─── [sidecar] ↔ [app]
            ↑                          ↑
            └── control plane (policy, certs, config) ──┘
```

The **data plane** is the fleet of sidecars. The **control plane** is
the operator's brain: pushes config, rotates certs, collects telemetry.

## What it gives you

Things the mesh handles uniformly so app code doesn't:

<Cheatsheet>
| Concern | Without mesh | With mesh |
|---|---|---|
| mTLS between services | Per-language TLS code, cert rotation in app. | Automatic, identity-based, rotated by control plane. |
| Retries with backoff | Per-language client lib, often inconsistent. | Declarative policy per route. |
| Timeouts | Per-call code; easy to forget. | Enforced at the proxy. |
| Circuit breaking | Library per language. | Outlier detection by the mesh. |
| Traffic splitting (canary, blue/green) | Custom routing logic. | Weighted routes, header-based, percent rollouts. |
| L7 metrics (RED) | Per-language instrumentation. | Free, uniform Prometheus metrics. |
| Distributed tracing | Per-language SDK with manual propagation. | Automatic for HTTP/gRPC; app still needs to propagate context. |
| Authz policy | Custom middleware. | Declarative — "only frontend can call billing." |
</Cheatsheet>

The strongest argument is **uniformity**. A polyglot fleet (Go, Node,
Python, Java) gets the same retry/timeout/mTLS behavior without a
per-language library and without trusting every team to configure it
right.

## What it costs

Real costs, from the SRE Workbook and production reports:

- **Latency per hop**: sidecar adds 1–5 ms p50 and more at tail.
  Fine for most services; matters for low-latency hot paths.
- **CPU and memory**: each sidecar is a real process. At scale, 5–20%
  of cluster resources go to the mesh.
- **Operational complexity**: Istio is, by general consensus, a
  full-time job to operate. Linkerd is lighter; still non-trivial.
- **Debug surface area**: when traffic breaks, "is it the app or the
  sidecar?" is a new question on every page.
- **Upgrade pain**: sidecar version drift across the fleet is
  inevitable; control-plane upgrades touch every pod.

If your team can't justify these costs against a specific problem,
don't adopt a mesh.

## When the complexity pays off

A few situations where a mesh is a clear win:

<Steps>
<Step n={1} title="Polyglot fleet at scale.">
You have ≥4 languages and ≥20 services. Per-language client libs for
mTLS/retries/timeouts diverge and rot. A mesh standardizes them.
</Step>
<Step n={2} title="Zero-trust networking requirement.">
You need mTLS between every pair of services and rotating identities,
and you can't trust application code to do it right. A mesh makes it
declarative and uniform.
</Step>
<Step n={3} title="Progressive delivery at scale.">
Canary rollouts, header-based routing, traffic mirroring — without a
mesh you build this in your gateway or in code. With a mesh it's a
manifest.
</Step>
<Step n={4} title="Compliance pressure for fleet-wide policy.">
You need to prove that only the right services can talk to billing,
that all traffic is encrypted, and to produce audit logs. The mesh
gives you a policy plane and a single place to audit.
</Step>
</Steps>

If none of these apply, you're paying for a feature you don't need.

## Mesh vs. alternatives

A mesh is one point in a design space. Cheaper alternatives often
suffice:

<Compare a="Service mesh" b="Library-based">
<>
Cross-cutting concerns in a sidecar, uniform across languages,
controlled by a policy plane. High operational cost; high control.
Examples: Istio, Linkerd.
</>
<>
Cross-cutting concerns in a per-language SDK (Finagle, gRPC
interceptors, Hystrix). Low ops cost, requires uniform library
adoption. Google ran on this for years; Finagle was Twitter's spine.
</>
</Compare>

<Compare a="Service mesh" b="API gateway only">
<>
mTLS, retries, telemetry between every pair of services in the
fleet. East-west traffic. Internal-policy plane.
</>
<>
Auth, rate-limiting, routing at the edge only. North-south traffic.
Internal services talk plain HTTP/gRPC. Far cheaper; misses the
east-west uniformity.
</>
</Compare>

For most teams with one or two languages, a well-configured gRPC or
HTTP client library + a good gateway gets you 80% of the value at
20% of the cost.

## Istio vs Linkerd in one paragraph

- **Istio**: Envoy-based data plane, the most features, the most
  knobs, the most complexity. Standard at very large organizations
  that have a platform team.
- **Linkerd**: own Rust-based proxy (`linkerd-proxy`), opinionated,
  far simpler to operate, fewer features. Standard for "we want
  mesh benefits without owning Istio."
- **Cilium / eBPF-based meshes**: emerging; replace some sidecars
  with kernel-level interception for less overhead. Less battle-
  tested.

If your team is asking "do we need a mesh," the answer for most teams
is "Linkerd or nothing."

## What the mesh does NOT do

Common misconceptions:

```
- It does not magically make a distributed monolith into microservices.
  Coupling is in the calls; the wire is fine.
- It does not give you observability for free. You still need traces,
  logs, dashboards, and SLOs. The mesh emits raw RED metrics; it
  doesn't decide what to alert on.
- It does not replace API design. Retries at the sidecar can hide bad
  endpoints — and double-spend idempotency-key-less calls.
- It does not provide application-level idempotency. The mesh retries;
  your endpoint must be idempotent. See "Idempotency keys."
- It is not the security boundary. It enforces mTLS and policy; your
  authn/authz logic still lives in the app.
```

## Failure modes

A mesh introduces a class of issues that don't exist without one:

### Sidecar startup race

The app starts and tries to call out before the sidecar is ready —
all calls fail. Fix with `holdApplicationUntilProxyStarts` (Istio)
or readiness gating. Boring once known, devastating on first deploy.

### Per-route policy explosion

Every team adds a few retry/timeout rules. Three months later, the
configuration is unreadable and conflicts. Centralize ownership of
mesh policy on a platform team.

### Duplicate retries

The app retries. The sidecar retries. The gateway retries. Total
request multiplier: 27. Pick one layer for retries — usually the
sidecar — and disable retries in the others.

### "Why is the request slow?"

p99 latency is up 30 ms. Is it the app, the sidecar, the network,
the control plane? Without good per-hop tracing, the mesh becomes a
black box. Run tracing from day one.

### Upgrade lockstep

The control plane version constrains the data plane version. A major
control-plane upgrade requires rolling every pod in the fleet. Plan
upgrades as projects, not maintenance windows.

## How adoption usually goes

A common arc, from the SRE workbook and platform-team blog posts:

```
1. Adopt for mTLS and traffic shifting on a few critical services.
2. Get bitten by upgrade pain; build platform tooling around it.
3. Expand to most of the fleet; centralize policy.
4. Eventually: 30% of platform engineering time is mesh.
5. Either: (a) it pays off because the org is large enough, or
          (b) you regret it and scope it back.
```

The honest version: a mesh is a platform-team commitment, not a
deployment. Don't adopt one unless you have, or will have, a platform
team to own it.

<Callout variant="info">
"Do we need a service mesh?" is the wrong question. The right one
is "what specific problem are we paying this much to solve, and is
there a cheaper option?"
</Callout>

## In summary

- A service mesh moves mTLS, retries, timeouts, telemetry, and
  traffic policy into sidecar proxies, out of app code.
- The killer feature is **uniformity** across a polyglot fleet at
  scale.
- Costs are real: latency, resources, ops complexity, debug surface.
  Treat the mesh as a platform-team commitment.
- For most teams, a good HTTP/gRPC library plus a well-run gateway
  is cheaper and sufficient.
- Linkerd is the default for "mesh without becoming an Istio shop."
- A mesh does not replace API design, idempotency, observability, or
  good service boundaries — it complements them.
