# KISS — Keep It Simple

Often expanded to "Keep It Simple, Stupid" (Kelly Johnson, Lockheed
Skunk Works, 1960). The original engineering form was about
aircraft maintainability: a design that an average mechanic can
repair in the field under combat conditions beats a clever one that
requires specialists.

The software translation: **prefer the simplest design that solves
the actual problem.** Cleverness is a cost. So is novelty.

## What "simple" means here

Rich Hickey's distinction (*Simple Made Easy*) is useful:

- **Easy** is what's familiar to *you* — close at hand, fits your
  current habits. Easy is subjective.
- **Simple** is what has few interleaved parts — *unentangled*.
  Simple is objective.

The principle aims at *simple*, not *easy*. A library you know well
might be "easy" to use but introduce a lot of interleaved concepts
into your code; a smaller, less-familiar tool might be "simple" but
require a day of learning.

A simple design has fewer:

- Moving parts (services, processes, queues).
- Concepts the reader has to hold at once.
- Special cases.
- Indirection layers.
- Dependencies.

## The cost of unnecessary complexity

Brooks's old line: "There are no silver bullets." Every layer of
indirection — a queue, a framework, a microservice boundary, a DI
container — has a fixed tax:

- More failure modes (queue full, service down, version mismatch).
- More to learn for new contributors.
- More dependencies to keep updated and patched.
- Slower debugging (every layer is somewhere a bug could be).

That tax is fine when the layer earns it. KISS pushes back when it
doesn't.

## "The simplest thing that could possibly work"

Kent Beck's XP version. The test:

- Can you describe the design in one sentence?
- Could you delete a piece and still solve the actual problem?
- Are there configuration options no one uses?

The danger of "could possibly work" is misreading it as "ship
sloppy code." It means: solve the *problem you have*, not the
problem you imagine. Simple is not the same as careless.

## Patterns of unnecessary complexity

A non-exhaustive list of frequent offenders:

- **Frameworks chosen for prestige**, not fit. A static site with a
  GraphQL gateway and a service mesh.
- **Abstractions with one implementation.** An `IFoo` interface
  whose only implementer is `Foo`.
- **Configurability for hypothetical needs.** Twelve flags, of
  which only the defaults are used in production.
- **Premature distribution.** Splitting a monolith into services
  before the team is large enough to need the autonomy.
- **Replacing standard library features with custom ones.** "Our
  own promise library." "Our own date library." Rarely worth it.
- **Generic-ifying a one-shot.** A function that handles three
  inputs taking a `Processor<T>` strategy.

## Worse-is-better

Richard Gabriel's classic essay frames a related tension: an
elegant, "correct" design often loses to a simpler, less-elegant
one that ships. KISS doesn't say "ugly wins"; it says **the cost
of complexity is paid in many places, and elegance often hides
that cost**.

A useful interpretation: you can refactor toward elegance after the
problem is well-understood. You can't refactor *backwards* out of a
distributed system you didn't need.

## How to apply KISS without being lazy

Some moves that keep you on the simple side:

- **Default to one process.** Add a service only when there's a
  clear forcing function (independent scaling, team autonomy,
  blast-radius isolation).
- **Default to the standard library.** Pull in a dependency only
  when the cost is justified.
- **Default to plain data.** Objects with methods and inheritance
  are heavier than structs and pure functions.
- **Default to synchronous and direct.** Add queues or callbacks
  only when something has to be asynchronous.
- **Inline first, extract second.** Extract when you have evidence
  of variation, not when you imagine it.

KISS doesn't forbid complexity — it asks you to **earn it**.

## Relationship to other principles

- **YAGNI** is the temporal version of KISS: don't build complexity
  for *imagined* futures.
- **DRY** done wrong violates KISS — premature abstraction is more
  complex than the duplication it replaces.
- **SOLID** can violate KISS if applied without judgment. Six
  interfaces with one implementer each is not simpler than a
  concrete class.
